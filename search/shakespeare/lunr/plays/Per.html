ACT I

SCENE I. Antioch. A room in the palace.

Enter ANTIOCHUS, Prince PERICLES, and followers
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Young prince of Tyre, you have at large received
  3. The danger of the task you undertake.
  1. PERICLES
  2. I have, Antiochus, and, with a soul
  3. Embolden'd with the glory of her praise,
  4. Think death no hazard in this enterprise.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Bring in our daughter, clothed like a bride,
  3. For the embracements even of Jove himself;
  4. At whose conception, till Lucina reign'd,
  5. Nature this dowry gave, to glad her presence,
  6. The senate-house of planets all did sit,
  7. To knit in her their best perfections.
Music. Enter the Daughter of ANTIOCHUS
  1. PERICLES
  2. See where she comes, apparell'd like the spring,
  3. Graces her subjects, and her thoughts the king
  4. Of every virtue gives renown to men!
  5. Her face the book of praises, where is read
  6. Nothing but curious pleasures, as from thence
  7. Sorrow were ever razed and testy wrath
  8. Could never be her mild companion.
  9. You gods that made me man, and sway in love,
  10. That have inflamed desire in my breast
  11. To taste the fruit of yon celestial tree,
  12. Or die in the adventure, be my helps,
  13. As I am son and servant to your will,
  14. To compass such a boundless happiness!
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Prince Pericles —
  1. PERICLES
  2. That would be son to great Antiochus.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Before thee stands this fair Hesperides,
  3. With golden fruit, but dangerous to be touch'd;
  4. For death-like dragons here affright thee hard:
  5. Her face, like heaven, enticeth thee to view
  6. Her countless glory, which desert must gain;
  7. And which, without desert, because thine eye
  8. Presumes to reach, all thy whole heap must die.
  9. Yon sometimes famous princes, like thyself,
  10. Drawn by report, adventurous by desire,
  11. Tell thee, with speechless tongues and semblance pale,
  12. That without covering, save yon field of stars,
  13. Here they stand martyrs, slain in Cupid's wars;
  14. And with dead cheeks advise thee to desist
  15. For going on death's net, whom none resist.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Antiochus, I thank thee, who hath taught
  3. My frail mortality to know itself,
  4. And by those fearful objects to prepare
  5. This body, like to them, to what I must;
  6. For death remember'd should be like a mirror,
  7. Who tells us life's but breath, to trust it error.
  8. I'll make my will then, and, as sick men do
  9. Who know the world, see heaven, but, feeling woe,
  10. Gripe not at earthly joys as erst they did;
  11. So I bequeath a happy peace to you
  12. And all good men, as every prince should do;
  13. My riches to the earth from whence they came;
  14. But my unspotted fire of love to you.
  15. To the Daughter of ANTIOCHUS
  16. Thus ready for the way of life or death,
  17. I wait the sharpest blow, Antiochus.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Scorning advice, read the conclusion then:
  3. Which read and not expounded, 'tis decreed,
  4. As these before thee thou thyself shalt bleed.
  1. Daughter
  2. Of all say'd yet, mayst thou prove prosperous!
  3. Of all say'd yet, I wish thee happiness!
  1. PERICLES
  2. Like a bold champion, I assume the lists,
  3. Nor ask advice of any other thought
  4. But faithfulness and courage.
  5. He reads the riddle
  6. I am no viper, yet I feed
  7. On mother's flesh which did me breed.
  8. I sought a husband, in which labour
  9. I found that kindness in a father:
  10. He's father, son, and husband mild;
  11. I mother, wife, and yet his child.
  12. How they may be, and yet in two,
  13. As you will live, resolve it you.
  14. Sharp physic is the last: but, O you powers
  15. That give heaven countless eyes to view men's acts,
  16. Why cloud they not their sights perpetually,
  17. If this be true, which makes me pale to read it?
  18. Fair glass of light, I loved you, and could still,
  19. Takes hold of the hand of the Daughter of ANTIOCHUS
  20. Were not this glorious casket stored with ill:
  21. But I must tell you, now my thoughts revolt
  22. For he's no man on whom perfections wait
  23. That, knowing sin within, will touch the gate.
  24. You are a fair viol, and your sense the strings;
  25. Who, finger'd to make man his lawful music,
  26. Would draw heaven down, and all the gods, to hearken:
  27. But being play'd upon before your time,
  28. Hell only danceth at so harsh a chime.
  29. Good sooth, I care not for you.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Prince Pericles, touch not, upon thy life.
  3. For that's an article within our law,
  4. As dangerous as the rest. Your time's expired:
  5. Either expound now, or receive your sentence.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Great king,
  3. Few love to hear the sins they love to act;
  4. 'Twould braid yourself too near for me to tell it.
  5. Who has a book of all that monarchs do,
  6. He's more secure to keep it shut than shown:
  7. For vice repeated is like the wandering wind.
  8. Blows dust in other's eyes, to spread itself;
  9. And yet the end of all is bought thus dear,
  10. The breath is gone, and the sore eyes see clear:
  11. To stop the air would hurt them. The blind mole casts
  12. Copp'd hills towards heaven, to tell the earth is throng'd
  13. By man's oppression; and the poor worm doth die for't.
  14. Kings are earth's gods; in vice their law's
  15. their will;
  16. And if Jove stray, who dares say Jove doth ill?
  17. It is enough you know; and it is fit,
  18. What being more known grows worse, to smother it.
  19. All love the womb that their first being bred,
  20. Then give my tongue like leave to love my head.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Aside Heaven, that I had thy head! he has found
  3. the meaning:
  4. But I will gloze with him. — Young prince of Tyre,
  5. Though by the tenor of our strict edict,
  6. Your exposition misinterpreting,
  7. We might proceed to cancel of your days;
  8. Yet hope, succeeding from so fair a tree
  9. As your fair self, doth tune us otherwise:
  10. Forty days longer we do respite you;
  11. If by which time our secret be undone,
  12. This mercy shows we'll joy in such a son:
  13. And until then your entertain shall be
  14. As doth befit our honour and your worth.
Exeunt all but PERICLES
  1. PERICLES
  2. How courtesy would seem to cover sin,
  3. When what is done is like an hypocrite,
  4. The which is good in nothing but in sight!
  5. If it be true that I interpret false,
  6. Then were it certain you were not so bad
  7. As with foul incest to abuse your soul;
  8. Where now you're both a father and a son,
  9. By your untimely claspings with your child,
  10. Which pleasure fits an husband, not a father;
  11. And she an eater of her mother's flesh,
  12. By the defiling of her parent's bed;
  13. And both like serpents are, who though they feed
  14. On sweetest flowers, yet they poison breed.
  15. Antioch, farewell! for wisdom sees, those men
  16. Blush not in actions blacker than the night,
  17. Will shun no course to keep them from the light.
  18. One sin, I know, another doth provoke;
  19. Murder's as near to lust as flame to smoke:
  20. Poison and treason are the hands of sin,
  21. Ay, and the targets, to put off the shame:
  22. Then, lest my lie be cropp'd to keep you clear,
  23. By flight I'll shun the danger which I fear.
Exit
Re-enter ANTIOCHUS
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. He hath found the meaning, for which we mean
  3. To have his head.
  4. He must not live to trumpet forth my infamy,
  5. Nor tell the world Antiochus doth sin
  6. In such a loathed manner;
  7. And therefore instantly this prince must die:
  8. For by his fall my honour must keep high.
  9. Who attends us there?
Enter THALIARD
  1. THALIARD
  2. Doth your highness call?
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Thaliard,
  3. You are of our chamber, and our mind partakes
  4. Her private actions to your secrecy;
  5. And for your faithfulness we will advance you.
  6. Thaliard, behold, here's poison, and here's gold;
  7. We hate the prince of Tyre, and thou must kill him:
  8. It fits thee not to ask the reason why,
  9. Because we bid it. Say, is it done?
  1. THALIARD
  2. My lord,
  3. 'Tis done.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Enough.
  3. Enter a Messenger
  4. Let your breath cool yourself, telling your haste.
  1. Messenger
  2. My lord, prince Pericles is fled.
Exit
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. As thou
  3. Wilt live, fly after: and like an arrow shot
  4. From a well-experienced archer hits the mark
  5. His eye doth level at, so thou ne'er return
  6. Unless thou say 'Prince Pericles is dead.'
  1. THALIARD
  2. My lord,
  3. If I can get him within my pistol's length,
  4. I'll make him sure enough: so, farewell to your highness.
  1. ANTIOCHUS
  2. Thaliard, adieu!
  3. Exit THALIARD
  4. Till Pericles be dead,
  5. My heart can lend no succor to my head.
Exit

SCENE II. Tyre. A room in the palace.

Enter PERICLES
  1. PERICLES
  2. To Lords without Let none disturb us. — Why should
  3. this change of thoughts,
  4. The sad companion, dull-eyed melancholy,
  5. Be my so used a guest as not an hour,
  6. In the day's glorious walk, or peaceful night,
  7. The tomb where grief should sleep, can breed me quiet?
  8. Here pleasures court mine eyes, and mine eyes shun them,
  9. And danger, which I fear'd, is at Antioch,
  10. Whose aim seems far too short to hit me here:
  11. Yet neither pleasure's art can joy my spirits,
  12. Nor yet the other's distance comfort me.
  13. Then it is thus: the passions of the mind,
  14. That have their first conception by mis-dread,
  15. Have after-nourishment and life by care;
  16. And what was first but fear what might be done,
  17. Grows elder now and cares it be not done.
  18. And so with me: the great Antiochus,
  19. 'Gainst whom I am too little to contend,
  20. Since he's so great can make his will his act,
  21. Will think me speaking, though I swear to silence;
  22. Nor boots it me to say I honour him.
  23. If he suspect I may dishonour him:
  24. And what may make him blush in being known,
  25. He'll stop the course by which it might be known;
  26. With hostile forces he'll o'erspread the land,
  27. And with the ostent of war will look so huge,
  28. Amazement shall drive courage from the state;
  29. Our men be vanquish'd ere they do resist,
  30. And subjects punish'd that ne'er thought offence:
  31. Which care of them, not pity of myself,
  32. Who am no more but as the tops of trees,
  33. Which fence the roots they grow by and defend them,
  34. Makes both my body pine and soul to languish,
  35. And punish that before that he would punish.
Enter HELICANUS, with other Lords
  1. First Lord
  2. Joy and all comfort in your sacred breast!
  1. Second Lord
  2. And keep your mind, till you return to us,
  3. Peaceful and comfortable!
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Peace, peace, and give experience tongue.
  3. They do abuse the king that flatter him:
  4. For flattery is the bellows blows up sin;
  5. The thing which is flatter'd, but a spark,
  6. To which that blast gives heat and stronger glowing;
  7. Whereas reproof, obedient and in order,
  8. Fits kings, as they are men, for they may err.
  9. When Signior Sooth here does proclaim a peace,
  10. He flatters you, makes war upon your life.
  11. Prince, pardon me, or strike me, if you please;
  12. I cannot be much lower than my knees.
  1. PERICLES
  2. All leave us else; but let your cares o'erlook
  3. What shipping and what lading's in our haven,
  4. And then return to us.
  5. Exeunt Lords
  6. Helicanus, thou
  7. Hast moved us: what seest thou in our looks?
  1. HELICANUS
  2. An angry brow, dread lord.
  1. PERICLES
  2. If there be such a dart in princes' frowns,
  3. How durst thy tongue move anger to our face?
  1. HELICANUS
  2. How dare the plants look up to heaven, from whence
  3. They have their nourishment?
  1. PERICLES
  2. Thou know'st I have power
  3. To take thy life from thee.
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Kneeling
  3. I have ground the axe myself;
  4. Do you but strike the blow.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Rise, prithee, rise.
  3. Sit down: thou art no flatterer:
  4. I thank thee for it; and heaven forbid
  5. That kings should let their ears hear their
  6. faults hid!
  7. Fit counsellor and servant for a prince,
  8. Who by thy wisdom makest a prince thy servant,
  9. What wouldst thou have me do?
  1. HELICANUS
  2. To bear with patience
  3. Such griefs as you yourself do lay upon yourself.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Thou speak'st like a physician, Helicanus,
  3. That minister'st a potion unto me
  4. That thou wouldst tremble to receive thyself.
  5. Attend me, then: I went to Antioch,
  6. Where as thou know'st, against the face of death,
  7. I sought the purchase of a glorious beauty.
  8. From whence an issue I might propagate,
  9. Are arms to princes, and bring joys to subjects.
  10. Her face was to mine eye beyond all wonder;
  11. The rest — hark in thine ear — as black as incest:
  12. Which by my knowledge found, the sinful father
  13. Seem'd not to strike, but smooth: but thou
  14. know'st this,
  15. 'Tis time to fear when tyrants seem to kiss.
  16. Such fear so grew in me, I hither fled,
  17. Under the covering of a careful night,
  18. Who seem'd my good protector; and, being here,
  19. Bethought me what was past, what might succeed.
  20. I knew him tyrannous; and tyrants' fears
  21. Decrease not, but grow faster than the years:
  22. And should he doubt it, as no doubt he doth,
  23. That I should open to the listening air
  24. How many worthy princes' bloods were shed,
  25. To keep his bed of blackness unlaid ope,
  26. To lop that doubt, he'll fill this land with arms,
  27. And make pretence of wrong that I have done him:
  28. When all, for mine, if I may call offence,
  29. Must feel war's blow, who spares not innocence:
  30. Which love to all, of which thyself art one,
  31. Who now reprovest me for it —
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Alas, sir!
  1. PERICLES
  2. Drew sleep out of mine eyes, blood from my cheeks,
  3. Musings into my mind, with thousand doubts
  4. How I might stop this tempest ere it came;
  5. And finding little comfort to relieve them,
  6. I thought it princely charity to grieve them.
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Well, my lord, since you have given me leave to speak.
  3. Freely will I speak. Antiochus you fear,
  4. And justly too, I think, you fear the tyrant,
  5. Who either by public war or private treason
  6. Will take away your life.
  7. Therefore, my lord, go travel for a while,
  8. Till that his rage and anger be forgot,
  9. Or till the Destinies do cut his thread of life.
  10. Your rule direct to any; if to me.
  11. Day serves not light more faithful than I'll be.
  1. PERICLES
  2. I do not doubt thy faith;
  3. But should he wrong my liberties in my absence?
  1. HELICANUS
  2. We'll mingle our bloods together in the earth,
  3. From whence we had our being and our birth.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Tyre, I now look from thee then, and to Tarsus
  3. Intend my travel, where I'll hear from thee;
  4. And by whose letters I'll dispose myself.
  5. The care I had and have of subjects' good
  6. On thee I lay whose wisdom's strength can bear it.
  7. I'll take thy word for faith, not ask thine oath:
  8. Who shuns not to break one will sure crack both:
  9. But in our orbs we'll live so round and safe,
  10. That time of both this truth shall ne'er convince,
  11. Thou show'dst a subject's shine, I a true prince.
Exeunt

SCENE III. Tyre. An ante-chamber in the palace.

Enter THALIARD
  1. THALIARD
  2. So, this is Tyre, and this the court. Here must I
  3. kill King Pericles; and if I do it not, I am sure to
  4. be hanged at home: 'tis dangerous. Well, I perceive
  5. he was a wise fellow, and had good discretion, that,
  6. being bid to ask what he would of the king, desired
  7. he might know none of his secrets: now do I see he
  8. had some reason for't; for if a king bid a man be a
  9. villain, he's bound by the indenture of his oath to
  10. be one! Hush! here come the lords of Tyre.
Enter HELICANUS and ESCANES, with other Lords of Tyre
  1. HELICANUS
  2. You shall not need, my fellow peers of Tyre,
  3. Further to question me of your king's departure:
  4. His seal'd commission, left in trust with me,
  5. Doth speak sufficiently he's gone to travel.
  1. THALIARD
  2. Aside How! the king gone!
  1. HELICANUS
  2. If further yet you will be satisfied,
  3. Why, as it were unlicensed of your loves,
  4. He would depart, I'll give some light unto you.
  5. Being at Antioch —
  1. THALIARD
  2. Aside What from Antioch?
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Royal Antiochus — on what cause I know not —
  3. Took some displeasure at him; at least he judged so:
  4. And doubting lest that he had err'd or sinn'd,
  5. To show his sorrow, he'ld correct himself;
  6. So puts himself unto the shipman's toil,
  7. With whom each minute threatens life or death.
  1. THALIARD
  2. Aside Well, I perceive
  3. I shall not be hang'd now, although I would;
  4. But since he's gone, the king's seas must please:
  5. He 'scaped the land, to perish at the sea.
  6. I'll present myself. Peace to the lords of Tyre!
  1. HELICANUS
  2. Lord Thaliard from Antiochus is welcome.
  1. THALIARD
  2. From him I come
  3. With message unto princely Pericles;
  4. But since my landing I have understood
  5. Your lord has betook himself to unknown travels,
  6. My message must return from whence it came.
  1. HELICANUS
  2. We have no reason to desire it,
  3. Commended to our master, not to us:
  4. Yet, ere you shall depart, this we desire,
  5. As friends to Antioch, we may feast in Tyre.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. Tarsus. A room in the Governor's house.

Enter CLEON, the governor of Tarsus, with DIONYZA, and others
  1. CLEON
  2. My Dionyza, shall we rest us here,
  3. And by relating tales of others' griefs,
  4. See if 'twill teach us to forget our own?
  1. DIONYZA
  2. That were to blow at fire in hope to quench it;
  3. For who digs hills because they do aspire
  4. Throws down one mountain to cast up a higher.
  5. O my distressed lord, even such our griefs are;
  6. Here they're but felt, and seen with mischief's eyes,
  7. But like to groves, being topp'd, they higher rise.
  1. CLEON
  2. O Dionyza,
  3. Who wanteth food, and will not say he wants it,
  4. Or can conceal his hunger till he famish?
  5. Our tongues and sorrows do sound deep
  6. Our woes into the air; our eyes do weep,
  7. Till tongues fetch breath that may proclaim them louder;
  8. That, if heaven slumber while their creatures want,
  9. They may awake their helps to comfort them.
  10. I'll then discourse our woes, felt several years,
  11. And wanting breath to speak help me with tears.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. I'll do my best, sir.
  1. CLEON
  2. This Tarsus, o'er which I have the government,
  3. A city on whom plenty held full hand,
  4. For riches strew'd herself even in the streets;
  5. Whose towers bore heads so high they kiss'd the clouds,
  6. And strangers ne'er beheld but wondered at;
  7. Whose men and dames so jetted and adorn'd,
  8. Like one another's glass to trim them by:
  9. Their tables were stored full, to glad the sight,
  10. And not so much to feed on as delight;
  11. All poverty was scorn'd, and pride so great,
  12. The name of help grew odious to repeat.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. O, 'tis too true.
  1. CLEON
  2. But see what heaven can do! By this our change,
  3. These mouths, who but of late, earth, sea, and air,
  4. Were all too little to content and please,
  5. Although they gave their creatures in abundance,
  6. As houses are defiled for want of use,
  7. They are now starved for want of exercise:
  8. Those palates who, not yet two summers younger,
  9. Must have inventions to delight the taste,
  10. Would now be glad of bread, and beg for it:
  11. Those mothers who, to nousle up their babes,
  12. Thought nought too curious, are ready now
  13. To eat those little darlings whom they loved.
  14. So sharp are hunger's teeth, that man and wife
  15. Draw lots who first shall die to lengthen life:
  16. Here stands a lord, and there a lady weeping;
  17. Here many sink, yet those which see them fall
  18. Have scarce strength left to give them burial.
  19. Is not this true?
  1. DIONYZA
  2. Our cheeks and hollow eyes do witness it.
  1. CLEON
  2. O, let those cities that of plenty's cup
  3. And her prosperities so largely taste,
  4. With their superfluous riots, hear these tears!
  5. The misery of Tarsus may be theirs.
Enter a Lord
  1. Lord
  2. Where's the lord governor?
  1. CLEON
  2. Here.
  3. Speak out thy sorrows which thou bring'st in haste,
  4. For comfort is too far for us to expect.
  1. Lord
  2. We have descried, upon our neighbouring shore,
  3. A portly sail of ships make hitherward.
  1. CLEON
  2. I thought as much.
  3. One sorrow never comes but brings an heir,
  4. That may succeed as his inheritor;
  5. And so in ours: some neighbouring nation,
  6. Taking advantage of our misery,
  7. Hath stuff'd these hollow vessels with their power,
  8. To beat us down, the which are down already;
  9. And make a conquest of unhappy me,
  10. Whereas no glory's got to overcome.
  1. Lord
  2. That's the least fear; for, by the semblance
  3. Of their white flags display'd, they bring us peace,
  4. And come to us as favourers, not as foes.
  1. CLEON
  2. Thou speak'st like him's untutor'd to repeat:
  3. Who makes the fairest show means most deceit.
  4. But bring they what they will and what they can,
  5. What need we fear?
  6. The ground's the lowest, and we are half way there.
  7. Go tell their general we attend him here,
  8. To know for what he comes, and whence he comes,
  9. And what he craves.
  1. Lord
  2. I go, my lord.
Exit
  1. CLEON
  2. Welcome is peace, if he on peace consist;
  3. If wars, we are unable to resist.
Enter PERICLES with Attendants
  1. PERICLES
  2. Lord governor, for so we hear you are,
  3. Let not our ships and number of our men
  4. Be like a beacon fired to amaze your eyes.
  5. We have heard your miseries as far as Tyre,
  6. And seen the desolation of your streets:
  7. Nor come we to add sorrow to your tears,
  8. But to relieve them of their heavy load;
  9. And these our ships, you happily may think
  10. Are like the Trojan horse was stuff'd within
  11. With bloody veins, expecting overthrow,
  12. Are stored with corn to make your needy bread,
  13. And give them life whom hunger starved half dead.
  1. All
  2. The gods of Greece protect you!
  3. And we'll pray for you.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Arise, I pray you, rise:
  3. We do not look for reverence, but to love,
  4. And harbourage for ourself, our ships, and men.
  1. CLEON
  2. The which when any shall not gratify,
  3. Or pay you with unthankfulness in thought,
  4. Be it our wives, our children, or ourselves,

  2. All fortune to the good Simonides!
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. To you as much, sir! I am beholding to you
  3. For your sweet music this last night: I do
  4. Protest my ears were never better fed
  5. With such delightful pleasing harmony.
  1. PERICLES
  2. It is your grace's pleasure to commend;
  3. Not my desert.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Sir, you are music's master.
  1. PERICLES
  2. The worst of all her scholars, my good lord.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Let me ask you one thing:
  3. What do you think of my daughter, sir?
  1. PERICLES
  2. A most virtuous princess.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. And she is fair too, is she not?
  1. PERICLES
  2. As a fair day in summer, wondrous fair.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Sir, my daughter thinks very well of you;
  3. Ay, so well, that you must be her master,
  4. And she will be your scholar: therefore look to it.
  1. PERICLES
  2. I am unworthy for her schoolmaster.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. She thinks not so; peruse this writing else.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Aside What's here?
  3. A letter, that she loves the knight of Tyre!
  4. 'Tis the king's subtlety to have my life.
  5. O, seek not to entrap me, gracious lord,
  6. A stranger and distressed gentleman,
  7. That never aim'd so high to love your daughter,
  8. But bent all offices to honour her.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Thou hast bewitch'd my daughter, and thou art
  3. A villain.
  1. PERICLES
  2. By the gods, I have not:
  3. Never did thought of mine levy offence;
  4. Nor never did my actions yet commence
  5. A deed might gain her love or your displeasure.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Traitor, thou liest.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Traitor!
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Ay, traitor.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Even in his throat — unless it be the king —
  3. That calls me traitor, I return the lie.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Aside Now, by the gods, I do applaud his courage.
  1. PERICLES
  2. My actions are as noble as my thoughts,
  3. That never relish'd of a base descent.
  4. I came unto your court for honour's cause,
  5. And not to be a rebel to her state;
  6. And he that otherwise accounts of me,
  7. This sword shall prove he's honour's enemy.
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. No?
  3. Here comes my daughter, she can witness it.
Enter THAISA
  1. PERICLES
  2. Then, as you are as virtuous as fair,
  3. Resolve your angry father, if my tongue
  4. Did ere solicit, or my hand subscribe
  5. To any syllable that made love to you.
  1. THAISA
  2. Why, sir, say if you had,
  3. Who takes offence at that would make me glad?
  1. SIMONIDES
  2. Yea, mistress, are you so peremptory?
  3. Aside
  4. I am glad on't with all my heart. —
  5. I'll tame you; I'll bring you in subjection.
  6. Will you, not having my consent,
  7. Bestow your love and your affections
  8. Upon a stranger?
  9. Aside
  10. who, for aught I know,
  11. May be, nor can I think the contrary,
  12. As great in blood as I myself. —
  13. Therefore hear you, mistress; either frame
  14. Your will to mine — and you, sir, hear you,
  15. Either be ruled by me, or I will make you —
  16. Man and wife:
  17. Nay, come, your hands and lips must seal it too:
  18. And being join'd, I'll thus your hopes destroy;

  1. THAISA
  2. Yes, if you love me, sir.
  1. PERICLES

ACT III

SCENE I

Enter PERICLES, on shipboard
  1. PERICLES
  2. Thou god of this great vast, rebuke these surges,
  3. Which wash both heaven and hell; and thou, that hast
  4. Upon the winds command, bind them in brass,
  5. Having call'd them from the deep! O, still
  6. Thy deafening, dreadful thunders; gently quench
  7. Thy nimble, sulphurous flashes! O, how, Lychorida,
  8. How does my queen? Thou stormest venomously;
  9. Wilt thou spit all thyself? The seaman's whistle
  10. Is as a whisper in the ears of death,
  11. Unheard. Lychorida! — Lucina, O
  12. Divinest patroness, and midwife gentle
  13. To those that cry by night, convey thy deity
  14. Aboard our dancing boat; make swift the pangs
  15. Of my queen's travails!
  16. Enter LYCHORIDA, with an Infant
  17. Now, Lychorida!
  1. LYCHORIDA
  2. Here is a thing too young for such a place,
  3. Who, if it had conceit, would die, as I
  4. Am like to do: take in your arms this piece
  5. Of your dead queen.
  1. PERICLES
  2. How, how, Lychorida!
  1. LYCHORIDA
  2. Patience, good sir; do not assist the storm.
  3. Here's all that is left living of your queen,
  4. A little daughter: for the sake of it,
  5. Be manly, and take comfort.
  1. PERICLES
  2. O you gods!
  3. Why do you make us love your goodly gifts,
  4. And snatch them straight away? We here below
  5. Recall not what we give, and therein may
  6. Use honour with you.
  1. LYCHORIDA
  2. Patience, good sir,
  3. Even for this charge.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Now, mild may be thy life!
  3. For a more blustrous birth had never babe:
  4. Quiet and gentle thy conditions! for
  5. Thou art the rudeliest welcome to this world
  6. That ever was prince's child. Happy what follows!
  7. Thou hast as chiding a nativity
  8. As fire, air, water, earth, and heaven can make,
  9. To herald thee from the womb: even at the first
  10. Thy loss is more than can thy portage quit,
  11. With all thou canst find here. Now, the good gods
  12. Throw their best eyes upon't!
Enter two Sailors
  1. First Sailor
  2. What courage, sir? God save you!
  1. PERICLES
  2. Courage enough: I do not fear the flaw;
  3. It hath done to me the worst. Yet, for the love
  4. Of this poor infant, this fresh-new sea-farer,
  5. I would it would be quiet.
  1. First Sailor
  2. Slack the bolins there! Thou wilt not, wilt thou?
  3. Blow, and split thyself.
  1. Second Sailor
  2. But sea-room, an the brine and cloudy billow kiss
  3. the moon, I care not.
  1. First Sailor
  2. Sir, your queen must overboard: the sea works high,
  3. the wind is loud, and will not lie till the ship be
  4. cleared of the dead.
  1. PERICLES
  2. That's your superstition.
  1. First Sailor
  2. Pardon us, sir; with us at sea it hath been still
  3. observed: and we are strong in custom. Therefore
  4. briefly yield her; for she must overboard straight.
  1. PERICLES
  2. As you think meet. Most wretched queen!
  1. LYCHORIDA
  2. Here she lies, sir.
  1. PERICLES
  2. A terrible childbed hast thou had, my dear;
  3. No light, no fire: the unfriendly elements
  4. Forgot thee utterly: nor have I time
  5. To give thee hallow'd to thy grave, but straight
  6. Must cast thee, scarcely coffin'd, in the ooze;
  7. Where, for a monument upon thy bones,
  8. And e'er-remaining lamps, the belching whale
  9. And humming water must o'erwhelm thy corpse,
  10. Lying with simple shells. O Lychorida,
  11. Bid Nestor bring me spices, ink and paper,
  12. My casket and my jewels; and bid Nicander
  13. Bring me the satin coffer: lay the babe
  14. Upon the pillow: hie thee, whiles I say
  15. A priestly farewell to her: suddenly, woman.
Exit LYCHORIDA
  1. Second Sailor
  2. Sir, we have a chest beneath the hatches, caulked
  3. and bitumed ready.
  1. PERICLES
  2. I thank thee. Mariner, say what coast is this?
  1. Second Sailor
  2. We are near Tarsus.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Thither, gentle mariner.
  3. Alter thy course for Tyre. When canst thou reach it?
  1. Second Sailor
  2. By break of day, if the wind cease.
  1. PERICLES
  2. O, make for Tarsus!
  3. There will I visit Cleon, for the babe
  4. Cannot hold out to Tyrus: there I'll leave it
  5. At careful nursing. Go thy ways, good mariner:
  6. I'll bring the body presently.
Exeunt

SCENE II. Ephesus. A room in CERIMON's house.

Enter CERIMON, with a Servant, and some Persons who have been shipwrecked
  1. CERIMON
  2. Philemon, ho!
Enter PHILEMON
  1. PHILEMON
  2. Doth my lord call?
  1. CERIMON
  2. Get fire and meat for these poor men:
  3. 'T has been a turbulent and stormy night.
  1. Servant
  2. I have been in many; but such a night as this,
  3. Till now, I ne'er endured.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Your master will be dead ere you return;
  3. There's nothing can be minister'd to nature
  4. That can recover him.
  5. To PHILEMON
  6. Give this to the 'pothecary,
  7. And tell me how it works.
Exeunt all but CERIMON
Enter two Gentlemen
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Good morrow.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. Good morrow to your lordship.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Gentlemen,
  3. Why do you stir so early?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Sir,
  3. Our lodgings, standing bleak upon the sea,
  4. Shook as the earth did quake;
  5. The very principals did seem to rend,
  6. And all-to topple: pure surprise and fear
  7. Made me to quit the house.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. That is the cause we trouble you so early;
  3. 'Tis not our husbandry.
  1. CERIMON
  2. O, you say well.
  1. First Gentleman
  2. But I much marvel that your lordship, having
  3. Rich tire about you, should at these early hours
  4. Shake off the golden slumber of repose.
  5. 'Tis most strange,
  6. Nature should be so conversant with pain,
  7. Being thereto not compell'd.
  1. CERIMON
  2. I hold it ever,
  3. Virtue and cunning were endowments greater
  4. Than nobleness and riches: careless heirs
  5. May the two latter darken and expend;
  6. But immortality attends the former.
  7. Making a man a god. 'Tis known, I ever
  8. Have studied physic, through which secret art,
  9. By turning o'er authorities, I have,
  10. Together with my practise, made familiar
  11. To me and to my aid the blest infusions
  12. That dwell in vegetives, in metals, stones;
  13. And I can speak of the disturbances
  14. That nature works, and of her cures; which doth give me
  15. A more content in course of true delight
  16. Than to be thirsty after tottering honour,
  17. Or tie my treasure up in silken bags,
  18. To please the fool and death.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. Your honour has through Ephesus pour'd forth
  3. Your charity, and hundreds call themselves
  4. Your creatures, who by you have been restored:
  5. And not your knowledge, your personal pain, but even
  6. Your purse, still open, hath built Lord Cerimon
  7. Such strong renown as time shall ne'er decay.
Enter two or three Servants with a chest
  1. First Servant
  2. So; lift there.
  1. CERIMON
  2. What is that?
  1. First Servant
  2. Sir, even now
  3. Did the sea toss upon our shore this chest:
  4. 'Tis of some wreck.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Set 't down, let's look upon't.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. 'Tis like a coffin, sir.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Whate'er it be,
  3. 'Tis wondrous heavy. Wrench it open straight:
  4. If the sea's stomach be o'ercharged with gold,
  5. 'Tis a good constraint of fortune it belches upon us.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. 'Tis so, my lord.
  1. CERIMON
  2. How close 'tis caulk'd and bitumed!
  3. Did the sea cast it up?
  1. First Servant
  2. I never saw so huge a billow, sir,
  3. As toss'd it upon shore.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Wrench it open;
  3. Soft! it smells most sweetly in my sense.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. A delicate odour.
  1. CERIMON
  2. As ever hit my nostril. So, up with it.
  3. O you most potent gods! what's here? a corse!
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Most strange!
  1. CERIMON
  2. Shrouded in cloth of state; balm'd and entreasured
  3. With full bags of spices! A passport too!
  4. Apollo, perfect me in the characters!
  5. Reads from a scroll
  6. 'Here I give to understand,
  7. If e'er this coffin drive a-land,
  8. I, King Pericles, have lost
  9. This queen, worth all our mundane cost.
  10. Who finds her, give her burying;
  11. She was the daughter of a king:
  12. Besides this treasure for a fee,
  13. The gods requite his charity!'
  14. If thou livest, Pericles, thou hast a heart
  15. That even cracks for woe! This chanced tonight.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. Most likely, sir.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Nay, certainly to-night;
  3. For look how fresh she looks! They were too rough
  4. That threw her in the sea. Make a fire within:
  5. Fetch hither all my boxes in my closet.
  6. Exit a Servant
  7. Death may usurp on nature many hours,
  8. And yet the fire of life kindle again
  9. The o'erpress'd spirits. I heard of an Egyptian
  10. That had nine hours lien dead,
  11. Who was by good appliance recovered.
  12. Re-enter a Servant, with boxes, napkins, and fire
  13. Well said, well said; the fire and cloths.
  14. The rough and woeful music that we have,
  15. Cause it to sound, beseech you.
  16. The viol once more: how thou stirr'st, thou block!
  17. The music there! — I pray you, give her air.
  18. Gentlemen.
  19. This queen will live: nature awakes; a warmth
  20. Breathes out of her: she hath not been entranced
  21. Above five hours: see how she gins to blow
  22. Into life's flower again!
  1. First Gentleman
  2. The heavens,
  3. Through you, increase our wonder and set up
  4. Your fame forever.
  1. CERIMON
  2. She is alive; behold,
  3. Her eyelids, cases to those heavenly jewels
  4. Which Pericles hath lost,
  5. Begin to part their fringes of bright gold;
  6. The diamonds of a most praised water
  7. Do appear, to make the world twice rich. Live,
  8. And make us weep to hear your fate, fair creature,
  9. Rare as you seem to be.
She moves
  1. THAISA
  2. O dear Diana,
  3. Where am I? Where's my lord? What world is this?
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. Is not this strange?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Most rare.
  1. CERIMON
  2. Hush, my gentle neighbours!
  3. Lend me your hands; to the next chamber bear her.
  4. Get linen: now this matter must be look'd to,
  5. For her relapse is mortal. Come, come;
  6. And AEsculapius guide us!
Exeunt, carrying her away

SCENE III. Tarsus. A room in CLEON's house.

Enter PERICLES, CLEON, DIONYZA, and LYCHORIDA with MARINA in her arms
  1. PERICLES
  2. Most honour'd Cleon, I must needs be gone;
  3. My twelve months are expired, and Tyrus stands
  4. In a litigious peace. You, and your lady,
  5. Take from my heart all thankfulness! The gods
  6. Make up the rest upon you!
  1. CLEON
  2. Your shafts of fortune, though they hurt you mortally,
  3. Yet glance full wanderingly on us.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. O your sweet queen!
  3. That the strict fates had pleased you had brought her hither,
  4. To have bless'd mine eyes with her!
  1. PERICLES
  2. We cannot but obey
  3. The powers above us. Could I rage and roar
  4. As doth the sea she lies in, yet the end
  5. Must be as 'tis. My gentle babe Marina, whom,
  6. For she was born at sea, I have named so, here
  7. I charge your charity withal, leaving her
  8. The infant of your care; beseeching you
  9. To give her princely training, that she may be
  10. Manner'd as she is born.
  1. CLEON
  2. Fear not, my lord, but think
  3. Your grace, that fed my country with your corn,
  4. For which the people's prayers still fall upon you,
  5. Must in your child be thought on. If neglection
  6. Should therein make me vile, the common body,
  7. By you relieved, would force me to my duty:
  8. But if to that my nature need a spur,
  9. The gods revenge it upon me and mine,
  10. To the end of generation!
  1. PERICLES
  2. I believe you;
  3. Your honour and your goodness teach me to't,
  4. Without your vows. Till she be married, madam,
  5. By bright Diana, whom we honour, all
  6. Unscissor'd shall this hair of mine remain,
  7. Though I show ill in't. So I take my leave.
  8. Good madam, make me blessed in your care
  9. In bringing up my child.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. I have one myself,
  3. Who shall not be more dear to my respect
  4. Than yours, my lord.
  1. PERICLES
  2. Madam, my thanks and prayers.
  1. CLEON
  2. We'll bring your grace e'en to the edge o' the shore,
  3. Then give you up to the mask'd Neptune and
  4. The gentlest winds of heaven.
  1. PERICLES
  2. I will embrace
  3. Your offer. Come, dearest madam. O, no tears,
  4. Lychorida, no tears:
  5. Look to your little mistress, on whose grace
  6. You may depend hereafter. Come, my lord.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. Ephesus. A room in CERIMON's house.

Enter CERIMON and THAISA
  1. CERIMON
  2. Madam, this letter, and some certain jewels,
  3. Lay with you in your coffer: which are now
  4. At your command. Know you the character?
  1. THAISA
  2. It is my lord's.
  3. That I was shipp'd at sea, I well remember,
  4. Even on my eaning time; but whether there
  5. Deliver'd, by the holy gods,
  6. I cannot rightly say. But since King Pericles,
  7. My wedded lord, I ne'er shall see again,
  8. A vestal livery will I take me to,

  1. CERIMON
  2. Madam, if this you purpose as ye speak,
  3. Diana's temple is not distant far,
  4. Where you may abide till your date expire.
  5. Moreover, if you please, a niece of mine
  6. Shall there attend you.
  1. THAISA
  2. My recompense is thanks, that's all;
  3. Yet my good will is great, though the gift small.
Exeunt

ACT IV

SCENE I. Tarsus. An open place near the sea-shore.

Enter DIONYZA and LEONINE
  1. DIONYZA
  2. Thy oath remember; thou hast sworn to do't:
  3. 'Tis but a blow, which never shall be known.
  4. Thou canst not do a thing in the world so soon,
  5. To yield thee so much profit. Let not conscience,
  6. Which is but cold, inflaming love i' thy bosom,
  7. Inflame too nicely; nor let pity, which
  8. Even women have cast off, melt thee, but be
  9. A soldier to thy purpose.
  1. LEONINE
  2. I will do't; but yet she is a goodly creature.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. The fitter, then, the gods should have her. Here
  3. she comes weeping for her only mistress' death.
  4. Thou art resolved?
  1. LEONINE
  2. I am resolved.
Enter MARINA, with a basket of flowers
  1. MARINA
  2. No, I will rob Tellus of her weed,
  3. To strew thy green with flowers: the yellows, blues,
  4. The purple violets, and marigolds,
  5. Shall as a carpet hang upon thy grave,
  6. While summer-days do last. Ay me! poor maid,
  7. Born in a tempest, when my mother died,
  8. This world to me is like a lasting storm,
  9. Whirring me from my friends.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. How now, Marina! why do you keep alone?
  3. How chance my daughter is not with you? Do not
  4. Consume your blood with sorrowing: you have
  5. A nurse of me. Lord, how your favour's changed
  6. With this unprofitable woe!
  7. Come, give me your flowers, ere the sea mar it.
  8. Walk with Leonine; the air is quick there,
  9. And it pierces and sharpens the stomach. Come,
  10. Leonine, take her by the arm, walk with her.
  1. MARINA
  2. No, I pray you;
  3. I'll not bereave you of your servant.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. Come, come;
  3. I love the king your father, and yourself,
  4. With more than foreign heart. We every day
  5. Expect him here: when he shall come and find
  6. Our paragon to all reports thus blasted,
  7. He will repent the breadth of his great voyage;
  8. Blame both my lord and me, that we have taken
  9. No care to your best courses. Go, I pray you,
  10. Walk, and be cheerful once again; reserve
  11. That excellent complexion, which did steal
  12. The eyes of young and old. Care not for me
  13. I can go home alone.
  1. MARINA
  2. Well, I will go;
  3. But yet I have no desire to it.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. Come, come, I know 'tis good for you.
  3. Walk half an hour, Leonine, at the least:
  4. Remember what I have said.
  1. LEONINE
  2. I warrant you, madam.
  1. DIONYZA
  2. I'll leave you, my sweet lady, for a while:
  3. Pray, walk softly, do not heat your blood:
  4. What! I must have a care of you.
  1. MARINA
  2. My thanks, sweet madam.
  3. Exit DIONYZA
  4. Is this wind westerly that blows?
  1. LEONINE
  2. South-west.
  1. MARINA
  2. When I was born, the wind was north.
  1. LEONINE
  2. Was't so?
  1. MARINA
  2. My father, as nurse said, did never fear,
  3. But cried 'Good seaman!' to the sailors, galling
  4. His kingly hands, haling ropes;
  5. And, clasping to the mast, endured a sea
  6. That almost burst the deck.
  1. LEONINE
  2. When was this?
  1. MARINA
  2. When I was born:
  3. Never was waves nor wind more violent;
  4. And from the ladder-tackle washes off
  5. A canvas-climber. 'Ha!' says one, 'wilt out?'
  6. And with a dropping industry they skip
  7. From stem to stern: the boatswain whistles, and
  8. The master calls, and trebles their confusion.
  1. LEONINE
  2. Come, say your prayers.
  1. MARINA
  2. What mean you?
  1. LEONINE
  2. If you require a little space for prayer,
  3. I grant it: pray; but be not tedious,
  4. For the gods are quick of ear, and I am sworn
  5. To do my work with haste.
  1. MARINA
  2. Why will you kill me?
  1. LEONINE
  2. To satisfy my lady.
  1. MARINA
  2. Why would she have me kill'd?
  3. Now, as I can remember, by my troth,
  4. I never did her hurt in all my life:
  5. I never spake bad word, nor did ill turn
  6. To any living creature: believe me, la,
  7. I never kill'd a mouse, nor hurt a fly:
  8. I trod upon a worm against my will,
  9. But I wept for it. How have I offended,
  10. Wherein my death might yield her any profit,
  11. Or my life imply her any danger?
  1. LEONINE
  2. My commission
  3. Is not to reason of the deed, but do it.
  1. MARINA
  2. You will not do't for all the world, I hope.
  3. You are well favour'd, and your looks foreshow
  4. You have a gentle heart. I saw you lately,
  5. When you caught hurt in parting two that fought:
  6. Good sooth, it show'd well in you: do so now:
  7. Your lady seeks my life; come you between,
  8. And save poor me, the weaker.
  1. LEONINE
  2. I am sworn,
  3. And will dispatch.
He seizes her
Enter Pirates
  1. First Pirate
  2. Hold, villain!
LEONINE runs away
  1. Second Pirate
  2. A prize! a prize!
  1. Third Pirate
  2. Half-part, mates, half-part.
  3. Come, let's have her aboard suddenly.
Exeunt Pirates with MARINA
