ACT I

SCENE I. Rousillon. The COUNT's palace.

Enter BERTRAM, the COUNTESS of Rousillon, HELENA, and LAFEU, all in black
  1. COUNTESS
  2. In delivering my son from me, I bury a second husband.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. And I in going, madam, weep o'er my father's death
  3. anew: but I must attend his majesty's command, to
  4. whom I am now in ward, evermore in subjection.
  1. LAFEU
  2. You shall find of the king a husband, madam; you,
  3. sir, a father: he that so generally is at all times
  4. good must of necessity hold his virtue to you; whose
  5. worthiness would stir it up where it wanted rather
  6. than lack it where there is such abundance.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. What hope is there of his majesty's amendment?
  1. LAFEU
  2. He hath abandoned his physicians, madam; under whose
  3. practises he hath persecuted time with hope, and
  4. finds no other advantage in the process but only the
  5. losing of hope by time.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. This young gentlewoman had a father — O, that
  3. 'had'! how sad a passage 'tis! — whose skill was
  4. almost as great as his honesty; had it stretched so
  5. far, would have made nature immortal, and death
  6. should have play for lack of work. Would, for the
  7. king's sake, he were living! I think it would be
  8. the death of the king's disease.
  1. LAFEU
  2. How called you the man you speak of, madam?
  1. COUNTESS
  2. He was famous, sir, in his profession, and it was
  3. his great right to be so: Gerard de Narbon.
  1. LAFEU
  2. He was excellent indeed, madam: the king very
  3. lately spoke of him admiringly and mourningly: he
  4. was skilful enough to have lived still, if knowledge
  5. could be set up against mortality.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. What is it, my good lord, the king languishes of?
  1. LAFEU
  2. A fistula, my lord.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. I heard not of it before.
  1. LAFEU
  2. I would it were not notorious. Was this gentlewoman
  3. the daughter of Gerard de Narbon?
  1. COUNTESS
  2. His sole child, my lord, and bequeathed to my
  3. overlooking. I have those hopes of her good that
  4. her education promises; her dispositions she
  5. inherits, which makes fair gifts fairer; for where
  6. an unclean mind carries virtuous qualities, there
  7. commendations go with pity; they are virtues and
  8. traitors too; in her they are the better for their
  9. simpleness; she derives her honesty and achieves her goodness.
  1. LAFEU
  2. Your commendations, madam, get from her tears.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. 'Tis the best brine a maiden can season her praise
  3. in. The remembrance of her father never approaches
  4. her heart but the tyranny of her sorrows takes all
  5. livelihood from her cheek. No more of this, Helena;
  6. go to, no more; lest it be rather thought you affect
  7. a sorrow than have it.
  1. HELENA
  2. I do affect a sorrow indeed, but I have it too.
  1. LAFEU
  2. Moderate lamentation is the right of the dead,
  3. excessive grief the enemy to the living.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. If the living be enemy to the grief, the excess
  3. makes it soon mortal.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. Madam, I desire your holy wishes.
  1. LAFEU
  2. How understand we that?
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Be thou blest, Bertram, and succeed thy father
  3. In manners, as in shape! thy blood and virtue
  4. Contend for empire in thee, and thy goodness
  5. Share with thy birthright! Love all, trust a few,
  6. Do wrong to none: be able for thine enemy
  7. Rather in power than use, and keep thy friend
  8. Under thy own life's key: be cheque'd for silence,
  9. But never tax'd for speech. What heaven more will,
  10. That thee may furnish and my prayers pluck down,
  11. Fall on thy head! Farewell, my lord;
  12. 'Tis an unseason'd courtier; good my lord,
  13. Advise him.
  1. LAFEU
  2. He cannot want the best
  3. That shall attend his love.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Heaven bless him! Farewell, Bertram.
Exit
  1. BERTRAM
  2. To HELENA The best wishes that can be forged in
  3. your thoughts be servants to you! Be comfortable
  4. to my mother, your mistress, and make much of her.
  1. LAFEU
  2. Farewell, pretty lady: you must hold the credit of
  3. your father.
Exeunt BERTRAM and LAFEU
  1. HELENA
  2. O, were that all! I think not on my father;
  3. And these great tears grace his remembrance more
  4. Than those I shed for him. What was he like?
  5. I have forgot him: my imagination
  6. Carries no favour in't but Bertram's.
  7. I am undone: there is no living, none,
  8. If Bertram be away. 'Twere all one
  9. That I should love a bright particular star
  10. And think to wed it, he is so above me:
  11. In his bright radiance and collateral light
  12. Must I be comforted, not in his sphere.
  13. The ambition in my love thus plagues itself:
  14. The hind that would be mated by the lion
  15. Must die for love. 'Twas pretty, though plague,
  16. To see him every hour; to sit and draw
  17. His arched brows, his hawking eye, his curls,
  18. In our heart's table; heart too capable
  19. Of every line and trick of his sweet favour:
  20. But now he's gone, and my idolatrous fancy
  21. Must sanctify his reliques. Who comes here?
  22. Enter PAROLLES
  23. Aside
  24. One that goes with him: I love him for his sake;
  25. And yet I know him a notorious liar,
  26. Think him a great way fool, solely a coward;
  27. Yet these fixed evils sit so fit in him,
  28. That they take place, when virtue's steely bones
  29. Look bleak i' the cold wind: withal, full oft we see
  30. Cold wisdom waiting on superfluous folly.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Save you, fair queen!
  1. HELENA
  2. And you, monarch!
  1. PAROLLES
  2. No.
  1. HELENA
  2. And no.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Are you meditating on virginity?
  1. HELENA
  2. Ay. You have some stain of soldier in you: let me
  3. ask you a question. Man is enemy to virginity; how
  4. may we barricado it against him?
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Keep him out.
  1. HELENA
  2. But he assails; and our virginity, though valiant,
  3. in the defence yet is weak: unfold to us some
  4. warlike resistance.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. There is none: man, sitting down before you, will
  3. undermine you and blow you up.
  1. HELENA
  2. Bless our poor virginity from underminers and
  3. blowers up! Is there no military policy, how
  4. virgins might blow up men?
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Virginity being blown down, man will quicklier be
  3. blown up: marry, in blowing him down again, with
  4. the breach yourselves made, you lose your city. It
  5. is not politic in the commonwealth of nature to
  6. preserve virginity. Loss of virginity is rational
  7. increase and there was never virgin got till
  8. virginity was first lost. That you were made of is
  9. metal to make virgins. Virginity by being once lost
  10. may be ten times found; by being ever kept, it is
  11. ever lost: 'tis too cold a companion; away with 't!
  1. HELENA
  2. I will stand for 't a little, though therefore I die a virgin.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. There's little can be said in 't; 'tis against the
  3. rule of nature. To speak on the part of virginity,
  4. is to accuse your mothers; which is most infallible
  5. disobedience. He that hangs himself is a virgin:
  6. virginity murders itself and should be buried in
  7. highways out of all sanctified limit, as a desperate
  8. offendress against nature. Virginity breeds mites,
  9. much like a cheese; consumes itself to the very
  10. paring, and so dies with feeding his own stomach.
  11. Besides, virginity is peevish, proud, idle, made of
  12. self-love, which is the most inhibited sin in the
  13. canon. Keep it not; you cannot choose but loose
  14. by't: out with 't! within ten year it will make
  15. itself ten, which is a goodly increase; and the
  16. principal itself not much the worse: away with 't!
  1. HELENA
  2. How might one do, sir, to lose it to her own liking?
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Let me see: marry, ill, to like him that ne'er it
  3. likes. 'Tis a commodity will lose the gloss with
  4. lying; the longer kept, the less worth: off with 't
  5. while 'tis vendible; answer the time of request.
  6. Virginity, like an old courtier, wears her cap out
  7. of fashion: richly suited, but unsuitable: just
  8. like the brooch and the tooth-pick, which wear not
  9. now. Your date is better in your pie and your
  10. porridge than in your cheek; and your virginity,
  11. your old virginity, is like one of our French
  12. withered pears, it looks ill, it eats drily; marry,
  13. 'tis a withered pear; it was formerly better;
  14. marry, yet 'tis a withered pear: will you anything with it?
  1. HELENA
  2. Not my virginity yet
  3. There shall your master have a thousand loves,
  4. A mother and a mistress and a friend,
  5. A phoenix, captain and an enemy,
  6. A guide, a goddess, and a sovereign,
  7. A counsellor, a traitress, and a dear;
  8. His humble ambition, proud humility,
  9. His jarring concord, and his discord dulcet,
  10. His faith, his sweet disaster; with a world
  11. Of pretty, fond, adoptious christendoms,
  12. That blinking Cupid gossips. Now shall he —
  13. I know not what he shall. God send him well!
  14. The court's a learning place, and he is one —
  1. PAROLLES
  2. What one, i' faith?
  1. HELENA
  2. That I wish well. 'Tis pity —
  1. PAROLLES
  2. What's pity?
  1. HELENA
  2. That wishing well had not a body in't,
  3. Which might be felt; that we, the poorer born,
  4. Whose baser stars do shut us up in wishes,
  5. Might with effects of them follow our friends,
  6. And show what we alone must think, which never
  7. Return us thanks.
Enter Page
  1. Page
  2. Monsieur Parolles, my lord calls for you.
Exit
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Little Helen, farewell; if I can remember thee, I
  3. will think of thee at court.
  1. HELENA
  2. Monsieur Parolles, you were born under a charitable star.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Under Mars, I.
  1. HELENA
  2. I especially think, under Mars.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Why under Mars?
  1. HELENA
  2. The wars have so kept you under that you must needs
  3. be born under Mars.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. When he was predominant.
  1. HELENA
  2. When he was retrograde, I think, rather.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. Why think you so?
  1. HELENA
  2. You go so much backward when you fight.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. That's for advantage.
  1. HELENA
  2. So is running away, when fear proposes the safety;
  3. but the composition that your valour and fear makes
  4. in you is a virtue of a good wing, and I like the wear well.
  1. PAROLLES
  2. I am so full of businesses, I cannot answer thee
  3. acutely. I will return perfect courtier; in the
  4. which, my instruction shall serve to naturalize
  5. thee, so thou wilt be capable of a courtier's
  6. counsel and understand what advice shall thrust upon
  7. thee; else thou diest in thine unthankfulness, and
  8. thine ignorance makes thee away: farewell. When
  9. thou hast leisure, say thy prayers; when thou hast
  10. none, remember thy friends; get thee a good husband,
  11. and use him as he uses thee; so, farewell.
Exit
  1. HELENA
  2. Our remedies oft in ourselves do lie,
  3. Which we ascribe to heaven: the fated sky
  4. Gives us free scope, only doth backward pull
  5. Our slow designs when we ourselves are dull.
  6. What power is it which mounts my love so high,
  7. That makes me see, and cannot feed mine eye?
  8. The mightiest space in fortune nature brings
  9. To join like likes and kiss like native things.
  10. Impossible be strange attempts to those
  11. That weigh their pains in sense and do suppose
  12. What hath been cannot be: who ever strove
  13. So show her merit, that did miss her love?
  14. The king's disease — my project may deceive me,
  15. But my intents are fix'd and will not leave me.
Exit

SCENE II. Paris. The KING's palace.

Flourish of cornets. Enter the KING of France, with letters, and divers Attendants
  1. KING
  2. The Florentines and Senoys are by the ears;
  3. Have fought with equal fortune and continue
  4. A braving war.
  1. First Lord
  2. So 'tis reported, sir.
  1. KING
  2. Nay, 'tis most credible; we here received it
  3. A certainty, vouch'd from our cousin Austria,
  4. With caution that the Florentine will move us
  5. For speedy aid; wherein our dearest friend
  6. Prejudicates the business and would seem
  7. To have us make denial.
  1. First Lord
  2. His love and wisdom,
  3. Approved so to your majesty, may plead
  4. For amplest credence.
  1. KING
  2. He hath arm'd our answer,
  3. And Florence is denied before he comes:
  4. Yet, for our gentlemen that mean to see
  5. The Tuscan service, freely have they leave
  6. To stand on either part.
  1. Second Lord
  2. It well may serve
  3. A nursery to our gentry, who are sick
  4. For breathing and exploit.
  1. KING
  2. What's he comes here?
Enter BERTRAM, LAFEU, and PAROLLES
  1. First Lord
  2. It is the Count Rousillon, my good lord,
  3. Young Bertram.
  1. KING
  2. Youth, thou bear'st thy father's face;
  3. Frank nature, rather curious than in haste,
  4. Hath well composed thee. Thy father's moral parts
  5. Mayst thou inherit too! Welcome to Paris.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. My thanks and duty are your majesty's.
  1. KING
  2. I would I had that corporal soundness now,
  3. As when thy father and myself in friendship
  4. First tried our soldiership! He did look far
  5. Into the service of the time and was
  6. Discipled of the bravest: he lasted long;
  7. But on us both did haggish age steal on
  8. And wore us out of act. It much repairs me
  9. To talk of your good father. In his youth
  10. He had the wit which I can well observe
  11. To-day in our young lords; but they may jest
  12. Till their own scorn return to them unnoted
  13. Ere they can hide their levity in honour;
  14. So like a courtier, contempt nor bitterness
  15. Were in his pride or sharpness; if they were,
  16. His equal had awaked them, and his honour,
  17. Clock to itself, knew the true minute when
  18. Exception bid him speak, and at this time
  19. His tongue obey'd his hand: who were below him
  20. He used as creatures of another place
  21. And bow'd his eminent top to their low ranks,
  22. Making them proud of his humility,
  23. In their poor praise he humbled. Such a man
  24. Might be a copy to these younger times;
  25. Which, follow'd well, would demonstrate them now
  26. But goers backward.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. His good remembrance, sir,
  3. Lies richer in your thoughts than on his tomb;
  4. So in approof lives not his epitaph
  5. As in your royal speech.
  1. KING
  2. Would I were with him! He would always say —
  3. Methinks I hear him now; his plausive words
  4. He scatter'd not in ears, but grafted them,
  5. To grow there and to bear — 'Let me not live,' —
  6. This his good melancholy oft began,
  7. On the catastrophe and heel of pastime,
  8. When it was out — 'Let me not live,' quoth he,
  9. 'After my flame lacks oil, to be the snuff
  10. Of younger spirits, whose apprehensive senses
  11. All but new things disdain; whose judgments are
  12. Mere fathers of their garments; whose constancies
  13. Expire before their fashions.' This he wish'd;
  14. I after him do after him wish too,
  15. Since I nor wax nor honey can bring home,
  16. I quickly were dissolved from my hive,
  17. To give some labourers room.
  1. Second Lord
  2. You are loved, sir:
  3. They that least lend it you shall lack you first.
  1. KING
  2. I fill a place, I know't. How long is't, count,
  3. Since the physician at your father's died?
  4. He was much famed.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. Some six months since, my lord.
  1. KING
  2. If he were living, I would try him yet.
  3. Lend me an arm; the rest have worn me out
  4. With several applications; nature and sickness
  5. Debate it at their leisure. Welcome, count;
  6. My son's no dearer.
  1. BERTRAM
  2. Thank your majesty.
Exeunt. Flourish

SCENE III. Rousillon. The COUNT's palace.

Enter COUNTESS, Steward, and Clown
  1. COUNTESS
  2. I will now hear; what say you of this gentlewoman?
  1. Steward
  2. Madam, the care I have had to even your content, I
  3. wish might be found in the calendar of my past
  4. endeavours; for then we wound our modesty and make
  5. foul the clearness of our deservings, when of
  6. ourselves we publish them.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. What does this knave here? Get you gone, sirrah:
  3. the complaints I have heard of you I do not all
  4. believe: 'tis my slowness that I do not; for I know
  5. you lack not folly to commit them, and have ability
  6. enough to make such knaveries yours.
  1. Clown
  2. 'Tis not unknown to you, madam, I am a poor fellow.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Well, sir.
  1. Clown
  2. No, madam, 'tis not so well that I am poor, though
  3. many of the rich are damned: but, if I may have
  4. your ladyship's good will to go to the world, Isbel
  5. the woman and I will do as we may.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Wilt thou needs be a beggar?
  1. Clown
  2. I do beg your good will in this case.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. In what case?
  1. Clown
  2. In Isbel's case and mine own. Service is no
  3. heritage: and I think I shall never have the
  4. blessing of God till I have issue o' my body; for
  5. they say barnes are blessings.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Tell me thy reason why thou wilt marry.
  1. Clown
  2. My poor body, madam, requires it: I am driven on
  3. by the flesh; and he must needs go that the devil drives.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Is this all your worship's reason?
  1. Clown
  2. Faith, madam, I have other holy reasons such as they
  3. are.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. May the world know them?
  1. Clown
  2. I have been, madam, a wicked creature, as you and
  3. all flesh and blood are; and, indeed, I do marry
  4. that I may repent.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Thy marriage, sooner than thy wickedness.
  1. Clown
  2. I am out o' friends, madam; and I hope to have
  3. friends for my wife's sake.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Such friends are thine enemies, knave.
  1. Clown
  2. You're shallow, madam, in great friends; for the
  3. knaves come to do that for me which I am aweary of.
  4. He that ears my land spares my team and gives me
  5. leave to in the crop; if I be his cuckold, he's my
  6. drudge: he that comforts my wife is the cherisher
  7. of my flesh and blood; he that cherishes my flesh
  8. and blood loves my flesh and blood; he that loves my
  9. flesh and blood is my friend: ergo, he that kisses
  10. my wife is my friend. If men could be contented to
  11. be what they are, there were no fear in marriage;
  12. for young Charbon the Puritan and old Poysam the
  13. Papist, howsome'er their hearts are severed in
  14. religion, their heads are both one; they may jowl
  15. horns together, like any deer i' the herd.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Wilt thou ever be a foul-mouthed and calumnious knave?
  1. Clown
  2. A prophet I, madam; and I speak the truth the next
  3. way:
  4. For I the ballad will repeat,
  5. Which men full true shall find;
  6. Your marriage comes by destiny,
  7. Your cuckoo sings by kind.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Get you gone, sir; I'll talk with you more anon.
  1. Steward
  2. May it please you, madam, that he bid Helen come to
  3. you: of her I am to speak.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Sirrah, tell my gentlewoman I would speak with her;
  3. Helen, I mean.
  1. Clown
  2. Was this fair face the cause, quoth she,
  3. Why the Grecians sacked Troy?
  4. Fond done, done fond,
  5. Was this King Priam's joy?
  6. With that she sighed as she stood,
  7. With that she sighed as she stood,
  8. And gave this sentence then;
  9. Among nine bad if one be good,
  10. Among nine bad if one be good,
  11. There's yet one good in ten.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. What, one good in ten? you corrupt the song, sirrah.
  1. Clown
  2. One good woman in ten, madam; which is a purifying
  3. o' the song: would God would serve the world so all
  4. the year! we'ld find no fault with the tithe-woman,
  5. if I were the parson. One in ten, quoth a'! An we
  6. might have a good woman born but one every blazing
  7. star, or at an earthquake, 'twould mend the lottery
  8. well: a man may draw his heart out, ere a' pluck
  9. one.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. You'll be gone, sir knave, and do as I command you.
  1. Clown
  2. That man should be at woman's command, and yet no
  3. hurt done! Though honesty be no puritan, yet it
  4. will do no hurt; it will wear the surplice of
  5. humility over the black gown of a big heart. I am
  6. going, forsooth: the business is for Helen to come hither.
Exit
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Well, now.
  1. Steward
  2. I know, madam, you love your gentlewoman entirely.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Faith, I do: her father bequeathed her to me; and
  3. she herself, without other advantage, may lawfully
  4. make title to as much love as she finds: there is
  5. more owing her than is paid; and more shall be paid
  6. her than she'll demand.
  1. Steward
  2. Madam, I was very late more near her than I think
  3. she wished me: alone she was, and did communicate
  4. to herself her own words to her own ears; she
  5. thought, I dare vow for her, they touched not any
  6. stranger sense. Her matter was, she loved your son:
  7. Fortune, she said, was no goddess, that had put
  8. such difference betwixt their two estates; Love no
  9. god, that would not extend his might, only where
  10. qualities were level; Dian no queen of virgins, that
  11. would suffer her poor knight surprised, without
  12. rescue in the first assault or ransom afterward.
  13. This she delivered in the most bitter touch of
  14. sorrow that e'er I heard virgin exclaim in: which I
  15. held my duty speedily to acquaint you withal;
  16. sithence, in the loss that may happen, it concerns
  17. you something to know it.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. You have discharged this honestly; keep it to
  3. yourself: many likelihoods informed me of this
  4. before, which hung so tottering in the balance that
  5. I could neither believe nor misdoubt. Pray you,
  6. leave me: stall this in your bosom; and I thank you
  7. for your honest care: I will speak with you further anon.
  8. Exit Steward
  9. Enter HELENA
  10. Even so it was with me when I was young:
  11. If ever we are nature's, these are ours; this thorn
  12. Doth to our rose of youth rightly belong;
  13. Our blood to us, this to our blood is born;
  14. It is the show and seal of nature's truth,
  15. Where love's strong passion is impress'd in youth:
  16. By our remembrances of days foregone,
  17. Such were our faults, or then we thought them none.
  18. Her eye is sick on't: I observe her now.
  1. HELENA
  2. What is your pleasure, madam?
  1. COUNTESS
  2. You know, Helen,
  3. I am a mother to you.
  1. HELENA
  2. Mine honourable mistress.
  1. COUNTESS
  2. Nay, a mother:
  3. Why not a mother? When I said 'a mother,'
  4. Methought you saw a serpent: what's in 'mother,'
  5. That you start at it? I say, I am your mother;
