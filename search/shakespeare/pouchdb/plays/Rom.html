SCENE Verona: Mantua.

ACT I

SCENE I. Verona. A public place.

Enter SAMPSON and GREGORY, of the house of Capulet, armed with swords and bucklers
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Gregory, o' my word, we'll not carry coals.
  1. GREGORY
  2. No, for then we should be colliers.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. I mean, an we be in choler, we'll draw.
  1. GREGORY
  2. Ay, while you live, draw your neck out o' the collar.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. I strike quickly, being moved.
  1. GREGORY
  2. But thou art not quickly moved to strike.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. A dog of the house of Montague moves me.
  1. GREGORY
  2. To move is to stir; and to be valiant is to stand:
  3. therefore, if thou art moved, thou runn'st away.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. A dog of that house shall move me to stand: I will
  3. take the wall of any man or maid of Montague's.
  1. GREGORY
  2. That shows thee a weak slave; for the weakest goes
  3. to the wall.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. True; and therefore women, being the weaker vessels,
  3. are ever thrust to the wall: therefore I will push
  4. Montague's men from the wall, and thrust his maids
  5. to the wall.
  1. GREGORY
  2. The quarrel is between our masters and us their men.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. 'Tis all one, I will show myself a tyrant: when I
  3. have fought with the men, I will be cruel with the
  4. maids, and cut off their heads.
  1. GREGORY
  2. The heads of the maids?
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Ay, the heads of the maids, or their maidenheads;
  3. take it in what sense thou wilt.
  1. GREGORY
  2. They must take it in sense that feel it.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Me they shall feel while I am able to stand: and
  3. 'tis known I am a pretty piece of flesh.
  1. GREGORY
  2. 'Tis well thou art not fish; if thou hadst, thou
  3. hadst been poor John. Draw thy tool! here comes
  4. two of the house of the Montagues.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. My naked weapon is out: quarrel, I will back thee.
  1. GREGORY
  2. How! turn thy back and run?
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Fear me not.
  1. GREGORY
  2. No, marry; I fear thee!
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Let us take the law of our sides; let them begin.
  1. GREGORY
  2. I will frown as I pass by, and let them take it as
  3. they list.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Nay, as they dare. I will bite my thumb at them;
  3. which is a disgrace to them, if they bear it.
Enter ABRAHAM and BALTHASAR
  1. ABRAHAM
  2. Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?
  1. SAMPSON
  2. I do bite my thumb, sir.
  1. ABRAHAM
  2. Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Aside to GREGORY Is the law of our side, if I say
  3. ay?
  1. GREGORY
  2. No.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. No, sir, I do not bite my thumb at you, sir, but I
  3. bite my thumb, sir.
  1. GREGORY
  2. Do you quarrel, sir?
  1. ABRAHAM
  2. Quarrel sir! no, sir.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. If you do, sir, I am for you: I serve as good a man as you.
  1. ABRAHAM
  2. No better.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Well, sir.
  1. GREGORY
  2. Say 'better:' here comes one of my master's kinsmen.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Yes, better, sir.
  1. ABRAHAM
  2. You lie.
  1. SAMPSON
  2. Draw, if you be men. Gregory, remember thy swashing blow.
They fight
Enter BENVOLIO
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Part, fools!
  3. Put up your swords; you know not what you do.
Beats down their swords
Enter TYBALT
  1. TYBALT
  2. What, art thou drawn among these heartless hinds?
  3. Turn thee, Benvolio, look upon thy death.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. I do but keep the peace: put up thy sword,
  3. Or manage it to part these men with me.
  1. TYBALT
  2. What, drawn, and talk of peace! I hate the word,
  3. As I hate hell, all Montagues, and thee:
  4. Have at thee, coward!
They fight
Enter, several of both houses, who join the fray; then enter Citizens, with clubs
  1. First Citizen
  2. Clubs, bills, and partisans! strike! beat them down!
  3. Down with the Capulets! down with the Montagues!
Enter CAPULET in his gown, and LADY CAPULET
  1. CAPULET
  2. What noise is this? Give me my long sword, ho!
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. A crutch, a crutch! why call you for a sword?
  1. CAPULET
  2. My sword, I say! Old Montague is come,
  3. And flourishes his blade in spite of me.
Enter MONTAGUE and LADY MONTAGUE
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. Thou villain Capulet — Hold me not, let me go.
  1. LADY MONTAGUE
  2. Thou shalt not stir a foot to seek a foe.
Enter PRINCE, with Attendants
  1. PRINCE
  2. Rebellious subjects, enemies to peace,
  3. Profaners of this neighbour-stained steel —
  4. Will they not hear? What, ho! you men, you beasts,
  5. That quench the fire of your pernicious rage
  6. With purple fountains issuing from your veins,
  7. On pain of torture, from those bloody hands
  8. Throw your mistemper'd weapons to the ground,
  9. And hear the sentence of your moved prince.
  10. Three civil brawls, bred of an airy word,
  11. By thee, old Capulet, and Montague,
  12. Have thrice disturb'd the quiet of our streets,
  13. And made Verona's ancient citizens
  14. Cast by their grave beseeming ornaments,
  15. To wield old partisans, in hands as old,
  16. Canker'd with peace, to part your canker'd hate:
  17. If ever you disturb our streets again,
  18. Your lives shall pay the forfeit of the peace.
  19. For this time, all the rest depart away:
  20. You Capulet; shall go along with me:
  21. And, Montague, come you this afternoon,
  22. To know our further pleasure in this case,
  23. To old Free-town, our common judgment-place.
  24. Once more, on pain of death, all men depart.
Exeunt all but MONTAGUE, LADY MONTAGUE, and BENVOLIO
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. Who set this ancient quarrel new abroach?
  3. Speak, nephew, were you by when it began?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Here were the servants of your adversary,
  3. And yours, close fighting ere I did approach:
  4. I drew to part them: in the instant came
  5. The fiery Tybalt, with his sword prepared,
  6. Which, as he breathed defiance to my ears,
  7. He swung about his head and cut the winds,
  8. Who nothing hurt withal hiss'd him in scorn:
  9. While we were interchanging thrusts and blows,
  10. Came more and more and fought on part and part,
  11. Till the prince came, who parted either part.
  1. LADY MONTAGUE
  2. O, where is Romeo? saw you him to-day?
  3. Right glad I am he was not at this fray.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Madam, an hour before the worshipp'd sun
  3. Peer'd forth the golden window of the east,
  4. A troubled mind drave me to walk abroad;
  5. Where, underneath the grove of sycamore
  6. That westward rooteth from the city's side,
  7. So early walking did I see your son:
  8. Towards him I made, but he was ware of me
  9. And stole into the covert of the wood:
  10. I, measuring his affections by my own,
  11. That most are busied when they're most alone,
  12. Pursued my humour not pursuing his,
  13. And gladly shunn'd who gladly fled from me.
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. Many a morning hath he there been seen,
  3. With tears augmenting the fresh morning dew.
  4. Adding to clouds more clouds with his deep sighs;
  5. But all so soon as the all-cheering sun
  6. Should in the furthest east begin to draw
  7. The shady curtains from Aurora's bed,
  8. Away from the light steals home my heavy son,
  9. And private in his chamber pens himself,
  10. Shuts up his windows, locks far daylight out
  11. And makes himself an artificial night:
  12. Black and portentous must this humour prove,
  13. Unless good counsel may the cause remove.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. My noble uncle, do you know the cause?
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. I neither know it nor can learn of him.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Have you importuned him by any means?
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. Both by myself and many other friends:
  3. But he, his own affections' counsellor,
  4. Is to himself — I will not say how true —
  5. But to himself so secret and so close,
  6. So far from sounding and discovery,
  7. As is the bud bit with an envious worm,
  8. Ere he can spread his sweet leaves to the air,
  9. Or dedicate his beauty to the sun.
  10. Could we but learn from whence his sorrows grow.
  11. We would as willingly give cure as know.
Enter ROMEO
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. See, where he comes: so please you, step aside;
  3. I'll know his grievance, or be much denied.
  1. MONTAGUE
  2. I would thou wert so happy by thy stay,
  3. To hear true shrift. Come, madam, let's away.
Exeunt MONTAGUE and LADY MONTAGUE
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Good-morrow, cousin.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Is the day so young?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. But new struck nine.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Ay me! sad hours seem long.
  3. Was that my father that went hence so fast?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. It was. What sadness lengthens Romeo's hours?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Not having that, which, having, makes them short.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. In love?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Out —
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Of love?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Out of her favour, where I am in love.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Alas, that love, so gentle in his view,
  3. Should be so tyrannous and rough in proof!
  1. ROMEO
  2. Alas, that love, whose view is muffled still,
  3. Should, without eyes, see pathways to his will!
  4. Where shall we dine? O me! What fray was here?
  5. Yet tell me not, for I have heard it all.
  6. Here's much to do with hate, but more with love.
  7. Why, then, O brawling love! O loving hate!
  8. O any thing, of nothing first create!
  9. O heavy lightness! serious vanity!
  10. Mis-shapen chaos of well-seeming forms!
  11. Feather of lead, bright smoke, cold fire,
  12. sick health!
  13. Still-waking sleep, that is not what it is!
  14. This love feel I, that feel no love in this.
  15. Dost thou not laugh?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. No, coz, I rather weep.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Good heart, at what?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. At thy good heart's oppression.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Why, such is love's transgression.
  3. Griefs of mine own lie heavy in my breast,
  4. Which thou wilt propagate, to have it prest
  5. With more of thine: this love that thou hast shown
  6. Doth add more grief to too much of mine own.
  7. Love is a smoke raised with the fume of sighs;
  8. Being purged, a fire sparkling in lovers' eyes;
  9. Being vex'd a sea nourish'd with lovers' tears:
  10. What is it else? a madness most discreet,
  11. A choking gall and a preserving sweet.
  12. Farewell, my coz.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Soft! I will go along;
  3. An if you leave me so, you do me wrong.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Tut, I have lost myself; I am not here;
  3. This is not Romeo, he's some other where.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Tell me in sadness, who is that you love.
  1. ROMEO
  2. What, shall I groan and tell thee?
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Groan! why, no.
  3. But sadly tell me who.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Bid a sick man in sadness make his will:
  3. Ah, word ill urged to one that is so ill!
  4. In sadness, cousin, I do love a woman.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. I aim'd so near, when I supposed you loved.
  1. ROMEO
  2. A right good mark-man! And she's fair I love.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. A right fair mark, fair coz, is soonest hit.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Well, in that hit you miss: she'll not be hit
  3. With Cupid's arrow; she hath Dian's wit;
  4. And, in strong proof of chastity well arm'd,
  5. From love's weak childish bow she lives unharm'd.
  6. She will not stay the siege of loving terms,
  7. Nor bide the encounter of assailing eyes,
  8. Nor ope her lap to saint-seducing gold:
  9. O, she is rich in beauty, only poor,
  10. That when she dies with beauty dies her store.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Then she hath sworn that she will still live chaste?
  1. ROMEO
  2. She hath, and in that sparing makes huge waste,
  3. For beauty starved with her severity
  4. Cuts beauty off from all posterity.
  5. She is too fair, too wise, wisely too fair,
  6. To merit bliss by making me despair:
  7. She hath forsworn to love, and in that vow
  8. Do I live dead that live to tell it now.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Be ruled by me, forget to think of her.
  1. ROMEO
  2. O, teach me how I should forget to think.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. By giving liberty unto thine eyes;
  3. Examine other beauties.
  1. ROMEO
  2. 'Tis the way
  3. To call hers exquisite, in question more:
  4. These happy masks that kiss fair ladies' brows
  5. Being black put us in mind they hide the fair;
  6. He that is strucken blind cannot forget
  7. The precious treasure of his eyesight lost:
  8. Show me a mistress that is passing fair,
  9. What doth her beauty serve, but as a note
  10. Where I may read who pass'd that passing fair?
  11. Farewell: thou canst not teach me to forget.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. I'll pay that doctrine, or else die in debt.
Exeunt

SCENE II. A street.

Enter CAPULET, PARIS, and Servant
  1. CAPULET
  2. But Montague is bound as well as I,
  3. In penalty alike; and 'tis not hard, I think,
  4. For men so old as we to keep the peace.
  1. PARIS
  2. Of honourable reckoning are you both;
  3. And pity 'tis you lived at odds so long.
  4. But now, my lord, what say you to my suit?
  1. CAPULET
  2. But saying o'er what I have said before:
  3. My child is yet a stranger in the world;
  4. She hath not seen the change of fourteen years,
  5. Let two more summers wither in their pride,
  6. Ere we may think her ripe to be a bride.
  1. PARIS
  2. Younger than she are happy mothers made.
  1. CAPULET
  2. And too soon marr'd are those so early made.
  3. The earth hath swallow'd all my hopes but she,
  4. She is the hopeful lady of my earth:
  5. But woo her, gentle Paris, get her heart,
  6. My will to her consent is but a part;
  7. An she agree, within her scope of choice
  8. Lies my consent and fair according voice.
  9. This night I hold an old accustom'd feast,
  10. Whereto I have invited many a guest,
  11. Such as I love; and you, among the store,
  12. One more, most welcome, makes my number more.
  13. At my poor house look to behold this night
  14. Earth-treading stars that make dark heaven light:
  15. Such comfort as do lusty young men feel
  16. When well-apparell'd April on the heel
  17. Of limping winter treads, even such delight
  18. Among fresh female buds shall you this night
  19. Inherit at my house; hear all, all see,
  20. And like her most whose merit most shall be:
  21. Which on more view, of many mine being one
  22. May stand in number, though in reckoning none,
  23. Come, go with me.
  24. To Servant, giving a paper
  25. Go, sirrah, trudge about
  26. Through fair Verona; find those persons out
  27. Whose names are written there, and to them say,
  28. My house and welcome on their pleasure stay.
Exeunt CAPULET and PARIS
  1. Servant
  2. Find them out whose names are written here! It is
  3. written, that the shoemaker should meddle with his
  4. yard, and the tailor with his last, the fisher with
  5. his pencil, and the painter with his nets; but I am
  6. sent to find those persons whose names are here
  7. writ, and can never find what names the writing
  8. person hath here writ. I must to the learned. — In good time.
Enter BENVOLIO and ROMEO
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Tut, man, one fire burns out another's burning,
  3. One pain is lessen'd by another's anguish;
  4. Turn giddy, and be holp by backward turning;
  5. One desperate grief cures with another's languish:
  6. Take thou some new infection to thy eye,
  7. And the rank poison of the old will die.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Your plaintain-leaf is excellent for that.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. For what, I pray thee?
  1. ROMEO
  2. For your broken shin.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Why, Romeo, art thou mad?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Not mad, but bound more than a mad-man is;
  3. Shut up in prison, kept without my food,
  4. Whipp'd and tormented and — God-den, good fellow.
  1. Servant
  2. God gi' god-den. I pray, sir, can you read?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Ay, mine own fortune in my misery.
  1. Servant
  2. Perhaps you have learned it without book: but, I
  3. pray, can you read any thing you see?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Ay, if I know the letters and the language.
  1. Servant
  2. Ye say honestly: rest you merry!
  1. ROMEO
  2. Stay, fellow; I can read.
  3. Reads
  4. 'Signior Martino and his wife and daughters;
  5. County Anselme and his beauteous sisters; the lady
  6. widow of Vitravio; Signior Placentio and his lovely
  7. nieces; Mercutio and his brother Valentine; mine
  8. uncle Capulet, his wife and daughters; my fair niece
  9. Rosaline; Livia; Signior Valentio and his cousin
  10. Tybalt, Lucio and the lively Helena.' A fair
  11. assembly: whither should they come?
  1. Servant
  2. Up.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Whither?
  1. Servant
  2. To supper; to our house.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Whose house?
  1. Servant
  2. My master's.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Indeed, I should have ask'd you that before.
  1. Servant
  2. Now I'll tell you without asking: my master is the
  3. great rich Capulet; and if you be not of the house
  4. of Montagues, I pray, come and crush a cup of wine.
  5. Rest you merry!
Exit
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. At this same ancient feast of Capulet's
  3. Sups the fair Rosaline whom thou so lovest,
  4. With all the admired beauties of Verona:
  5. Go thither; and, with unattainted eye,
  6. Compare her face with some that I shall show,
  7. And I will make thee think thy swan a crow.
  1. ROMEO
  2. When the devout religion of mine eye
  3. Maintains such falsehood, then turn tears to fires;
  4. And these, who often drown'd could never die,
  5. Transparent heretics, be burnt for liars!
  6. One fairer than my love! the all-seeing sun
  7. Ne'er saw her match since first the world begun.
  1. BENVOLIO
  2. Tut, you saw her fair, none else being by,
  3. Herself poised with herself in either eye:
  4. But in that crystal scales let there be weigh'd
  5. Your lady's love against some other maid
  6. That I will show you shining at this feast,
  7. And she shall scant show well that now shows best.
  1. ROMEO
  2. I'll go along, no such sight to be shown,
  3. But to rejoice in splendor of mine own.
Exeunt

SCENE III. A room in Capulet's house.

Enter LADY CAPULET and Nurse
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Nurse, where's my daughter? call her forth to me.
  1. Nurse
  2. Now, by my maidenhead, at twelve year old,
  3. I bade her come. What, lamb! what, ladybird!
  4. God forbid! Where's this girl? What, Juliet!
Enter JULIET
  1. JULIET
  2. How now! who calls?
  1. Nurse
  2. Your mother.
  1. JULIET
  2. Madam, I am here.
  3. What is your will?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. This is the matter: — Nurse, give leave awhile,
  3. We must talk in secret: — nurse, come back again;
  4. I have remember'd me, thou's hear our counsel.
  5. Thou know'st my daughter's of a pretty age.
  1. Nurse
  2. Faith, I can tell her age unto an hour.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. She's not fourteen.
  1. Nurse
  2. I'll lay fourteen of my teeth —
  3. And yet, to my teeth be it spoken, I have but four —
  4. She is not fourteen. How long is it now
  5. To Lammas-tide?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. A fortnight and odd days.
  1. Nurse
  2. Even or odd, of all days in the year,
  3. Come Lammas-eve at night shall she be fourteen.
  4. Susan and she — God rest all Christian souls! —
  5. Were of an age: well, Susan is with God;
  6. She was too good for me: but, as I said,
  7. On Lammas-eve at night shall she be fourteen;
  8. That shall she, marry; I remember it well.
  9. 'Tis since the earthquake now eleven years;
  10. And she was wean'd — I never shall forget it —
  11. Of all the days of the year, upon that day:
  12. For I had then laid wormwood to my dug,
  13. Sitting in the sun under the dove-house wall;
  14. My lord and you were then at Mantua: —
  15. Nay, I do bear a brain: — but, as I said,
  16. When it did taste the wormwood on the nipple
  17. Of my dug and felt it bitter, pretty fool,
  18. To see it tetchy and fall out with the dug!
  19. Shake quoth the dove-house: 'twas no need, I trow,
  20. To bid me trudge:
  21. And since that time it is eleven years;
  22. For then she could stand alone; nay, by the rood,
  23. She could have run and waddled all about;
  24. For even the day before, she broke her brow:
  25. And then my husband — God be with his soul!
  26. A' was a merry man — took up the child:
  27. 'Yea,' quoth he, 'dost thou fall upon thy face?
  28. Thou wilt fall backward when thou hast more wit;
  29. Wilt thou not, Jule?' and, by my holidame,
  30. The pretty wretch left crying and said 'Ay.'
  31. To see, now, how a jest shall come about!
  32. I warrant, an I should live a thousand years,
  33. I never should forget it: 'Wilt thou not, Jule?' quoth he;
  34. And, pretty fool, it stinted and said 'Ay.'
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Enough of this; I pray thee, hold thy peace.
  1. Nurse
  2. Yes, madam: yet I cannot choose but laugh,
  3. To think it should leave crying and say 'Ay.'
  4. And yet, I warrant, it had upon its brow
  5. A bump as big as a young cockerel's stone;
  6. A parlous knock; and it cried bitterly:
  7. 'Yea,' quoth my husband,'fall'st upon thy face?
  8. Thou wilt fall backward when thou comest to age;
  9. Wilt thou not, Jule?' it stinted and said 'Ay.'
  1. JULIET
  2. And stint thou too, I pray thee, nurse, say I.
  1. Nurse
  2. Peace, I have done. God mark thee to his grace!
  3. Thou wast the prettiest babe that e'er I nursed:
  4. An I might live to see thee married once,

  17. In herbs, plants, stones, and their true qualities:
  18. For nought so vile that on the earth doth live

  20. Nor aught so good but strain'd from that fair use
  21. Revolts from true birth, stumbling on abuse:
  22. Virtue itself turns vice, being misapplied;
  23. And vice sometimes by action dignified.
  24. Within the infant rind of this small flower
  25. Poison hath residence and medicine power:
  26. For this, being smelt, with that part cheers each part;
  27. Being tasted, slays all senses with the heart.
  28. Two such opposed kings encamp them still
  29. In man as well as herbs, grace and rude will;
  30. And where the worser is predominant,

  5. But, wherefore, villain, didst thou kill my cousin?
  6. That villain cousin would have kill'd my husband:
  7. Back, foolish tears, back to your native spring;
  8. Your tributary drops belong to woe,
  9. Which you, mistaking, offer up to joy.
  10. My husband lives, that Tybalt would have slain;
  11. And Tybalt's dead, that would have slain my husband:
  12. All this is comfort; wherefore weep I then?
  13. Some word there was, worser than Tybalt's death,
  14. That murder'd me: I would forget it fain;
  15. But, O, it presses to my memory,
  16. Like damned guilty deeds to sinners' minds:
  17. 'Tybalt is dead, and Romeo — banished;'
  18. That 'banished,' that one word 'banished,'
  19. Hath slain ten thousand Tybalts. Tybalt's death
  20. Was woe enough, if it had ended there:
  21. Or, if sour woe delights in fellowship
  22. And needly will be rank'd with other griefs,
  23. Why follow'd not, when she said 'Tybalt's dead,'
  24. Thy father, or thy mother, nay, or both,
  25. Which modern lamentations might have moved?
  26. But with a rear-ward following Tybalt's death,
  27. 'Romeo is banished,' to speak that word,
  28. Is father, mother, Tybalt, Romeo, Juliet,
  29. All slain, all dead. 'Romeo is banished!'
  30. There is no end, no limit, measure, bound,
  31. In that word's death; no words can that woe sound.
  32. Where is my father, and my mother, nurse?
  1. Nurse
  2. Weeping and wailing over Tybalt's corse:
  3. Will you go to them? I will bring you thither.
  1. JULIET
  2. Wash they his wounds with tears: mine shall be spent,
  3. When theirs are dry, for Romeo's banishment.
  4. Take up those cords: poor ropes, you are beguiled,
  5. Both you and I; for Romeo is exiled:
  6. He made you for a highway to my bed;
  7. But I, a maid, die maiden-widowed.
  8. Come, cords, come, nurse; I'll to my wedding-bed;
  9. And death, not Romeo, take my maidenhead!
  1. Nurse
  2. Hie to your chamber: I'll find Romeo
  3. To comfort you: I wot well where he is.
  4. Hark ye, your Romeo will be here at night:
  5. I'll to him; he is hid at Laurence' cell.
  1. JULIET
  2. O, find him! give this ring to my true knight,
  3. And bid him come to take his last farewell.
Exeunt

SCENE III. Friar Laurence's cell.

Enter FRIAR LAURENCE
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Romeo, come forth; come forth, thou fearful man:
  3. Affliction is enamour'd of thy parts,
  4. And thou art wedded to calamity.
Enter ROMEO
  1. ROMEO
  2. Father, what news? what is the prince's doom?
  3. What sorrow craves acquaintance at my hand,
  4. That I yet know not?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Too familiar
  3. Is my dear son with such sour company:
  4. I bring thee tidings of the prince's doom.
  1. ROMEO
  2. What less than dooms-day is the prince's doom?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. A gentler judgment vanish'd from his lips,
  3. Not body's death, but body's banishment.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Ha, banishment! be merciful, say 'death;'
  3. For exile hath more terror in his look,
  4. Much more than death: do not say 'banishment.'
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hence from Verona art thou banished:
  3. Be patient, for the world is broad and wide.
  1. ROMEO
  2. There is no world without Verona walls,
  3. But purgatory, torture, hell itself.
  4. Hence-banished is banish'd from the world,
  5. And world's exile is death: then banished,
  6. Is death mis-term'd: calling death banishment,
  7. Thou cutt'st my head off with a golden axe,
  8. And smilest upon the stroke that murders me.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. O deadly sin! O rude unthankfulness!
  3. Thy fault our law calls death; but the kind prince,
  4. Taking thy part, hath rush'd aside the law,
  5. And turn'd that black word death to banishment:
  6. This is dear mercy, and thou seest it not.
  1. ROMEO
  2. 'Tis torture, and not mercy: heaven is here,
  3. Where Juliet lives; and every cat and dog
  4. And little mouse, every unworthy thing,
  5. Live here in heaven and may look on her;
  6. But Romeo may not: more validity,
  7. More honourable state, more courtship lives
  8. In carrion-flies than Romeo: they my seize
  9. On the white wonder of dear Juliet's hand
  10. And steal immortal blessing from her lips,
  11. Who even in pure and vestal modesty,
  12. Still blush, as thinking their own kisses sin;
  13. But Romeo may not; he is banished:
  14. Flies may do this, but I from this must fly:
  15. They are free men, but I am banished.
  16. And say'st thou yet that exile is not death?
  17. Hadst thou no poison mix'd, no sharp-ground knife,
  18. No sudden mean of death, though ne'er so mean,
  19. But 'banished' to kill me? — 'banished'?
  20. O friar, the damned use that word in hell;
  21. Howlings attend it: how hast thou the heart,
  22. Being a divine, a ghostly confessor,
  23. A sin-absolver, and my friend profess'd,
  24. To mangle me with that word 'banished'?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Thou fond mad man, hear me but speak a word.
  1. ROMEO
  2. O, thou wilt speak again of banishment.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. I'll give thee armour to keep off that word:
  3. Adversity's sweet milk, philosophy,
  4. To comfort thee, though thou art banished.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Yet 'banished'? Hang up philosophy!
  3. Unless philosophy can make a Juliet,
  4. Displant a town, reverse a prince's doom,
  5. It helps not, it prevails not: talk no more.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. O, then I see that madmen have no ears.
  1. ROMEO
  2. How should they, when that wise men have no eyes?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Let me dispute with thee of thy estate.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Thou canst not speak of that thou dost not feel:
  3. Wert thou as young as I, Juliet thy love,
  4. An hour but married, Tybalt murdered,
  5. Doting like me and like me banished,
  6. Then mightst thou speak, then mightst thou tear thy hair,
  7. And fall upon the ground, as I do now,
  8. Taking the measure of an unmade grave.
Knocking within
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Arise; one knocks; good Romeo, hide thyself.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Not I; unless the breath of heartsick groans,
  3. Mist-like, infold me from the search of eyes.
Knocking
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hark, how they knock! Who's there? Romeo, arise;
  3. Thou wilt be taken. Stay awhile! Stand up;
  4. Knocking
  5. Run to my study. By and by! God's will,
  6. What simpleness is this! I come, I come!
  7. Knocking
  8. Who knocks so hard? whence come you? what's your will?
  1. Nurse
  2. Within Let me come in, and you shall know
  3. my errand;
  4. I come from Lady Juliet.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Welcome, then.
Enter Nurse
  1. Nurse
  2. O holy friar, O, tell me, holy friar,
  3. Where is my lady's lord, where's Romeo?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. There on the ground, with his own tears made drunk.
  1. Nurse
  2. O, he is even in my mistress' case,
  3. Just in her case! O woful sympathy!
  4. Piteous predicament! Even so lies she,
  5. Blubbering and weeping, weeping and blubbering.
  6. Stand up, stand up; stand, and you be a man:
  7. For Juliet's sake, for her sake, rise and stand;
  8. Why should you fall into so deep an O?
  1. ROMEO
  2. Nurse!
  1. Nurse
  2. Ah sir! ah sir! Well, death's the end of all.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Spakest thou of Juliet? how is it with her?
  3. Doth she not think me an old murderer,
  4. Now I have stain'd the childhood of our joy
  5. With blood removed but little from her own?
  6. Where is she? and how doth she? and what says
  7. My conceal'd lady to our cancell'd love?
  1. Nurse
  2. O, she says nothing, sir, but weeps and weeps;
  3. And now falls on her bed; and then starts up,
  4. And Tybalt calls; and then on Romeo cries,
  5. And then down falls again.
  1. ROMEO
  2. As if that name,
  3. Shot from the deadly level of a gun,
  4. Did murder her; as that name's cursed hand
  5. Murder'd her kinsman. O, tell me, friar, tell me,
  6. In what vile part of this anatomy
  7. Doth my name lodge? tell me, that I may sack
  8. The hateful mansion.
Drawing his sword
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hold thy desperate hand:
  3. Art thou a man? thy form cries out thou art:
  4. Thy tears are womanish; thy wild acts denote
  5. The unreasonable fury of a beast:
  6. Unseemly woman in a seeming man!
  7. Or ill-beseeming beast in seeming both!
  8. Thou hast amazed me: by my holy order,
  9. I thought thy disposition better temper'd.
  10. Hast thou slain Tybalt? wilt thou slay thyself?
  11. And stay thy lady too that lives in thee,
  12. By doing damned hate upon thyself?
  13. Why rail'st thou on thy birth, the heaven, and earth?
  14. Since birth, and heaven, and earth, all three do meet
  15. In thee at once; which thou at once wouldst lose.
  16. Fie, fie, thou shamest thy shape, thy love, thy wit;
  17. Which, like a usurer, abound'st in all,
  18. And usest none in that true use indeed
  19. Which should bedeck thy shape, thy love, thy wit:
  20. Thy noble shape is but a form of wax,
  21. Digressing from the valour of a man;
  22. Thy dear love sworn but hollow perjury,
  23. Killing that love which thou hast vow'd to cherish;
  24. Thy wit, that ornament to shape and love,
  25. Misshapen in the conduct of them both,
  26. Like powder in a skitless soldier's flask,
  27. Is set afire by thine own ignorance,
  28. And thou dismember'd with thine own defence.
  29. What, rouse thee, man! thy Juliet is alive,
  30. For whose dear sake thou wast but lately dead;
  31. There art thou happy: Tybalt would kill thee,
  32. But thou slew'st Tybalt; there are thou happy too:
  33. The law that threaten'd death becomes thy friend
  34. And turns it to exile; there art thou happy:
  35. A pack of blessings lights up upon thy back;
  36. Happiness courts thee in her best array;
  37. But, like a misbehaved and sullen wench,
  38. Thou pout'st upon thy fortune and thy love:
  39. Take heed, take heed, for such die miserable.
  40. Go, get thee to thy love, as was decreed,
  41. Ascend her chamber, hence and comfort her:
  42. But look thou stay not till the watch be set,
  43. For then thou canst not pass to Mantua;
  44. Where thou shalt live, till we can find a time
  45. To blaze your marriage, reconcile your friends,
  46. Beg pardon of the prince, and call thee back
  47. With twenty hundred thousand times more joy
  48. Than thou went'st forth in lamentation.
  49. Go before, nurse: commend me to thy lady;
  50. And bid her hasten all the house to bed,
  51. Which heavy sorrow makes them apt unto:
  52. Romeo is coming.
  1. Nurse
  2. O Lord, I could have stay'd here all the night
  3. To hear good counsel: O, what learning is!
  4. My lord, I'll tell my lady you will come.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Do so, and bid my sweet prepare to chide.
  1. Nurse
  2. Here, sir, a ring she bid me give you, sir:
  3. Hie you, make haste, for it grows very late.
Exit
  1. ROMEO
  2. How well my comfort is revived by this!
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Go hence; good night; and here stands all your state:
  3. Either be gone before the watch be set,
  4. Or by the break of day disguised from hence:
  5. Sojourn in Mantua; I'll find out your man,
  6. And he shall signify from time to time
  7. Every good hap to you that chances here:
  8. Give me thy hand; 'tis late: farewell; good night.
  1. ROMEO
  2. But that a joy past joy calls out on me,
  3. It were a grief, so brief to part with thee: Farewell.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. A room in Capulet's house.

Enter CAPULET, LADY CAPULET, and PARIS
  1. CAPULET
  2. Things have fall'n out, sir, so unluckily,
  3. That we have had no time to move our daughter:
  4. Look you, she loved her kinsman Tybalt dearly,
  5. And so did I: — Well, we were born to die.
  6. 'Tis very late, she'll not come down to-night:
  7. I promise you, but for your company,
  8. I would have been a-bed an hour ago.
  1. PARIS
  2. These times of woe afford no time to woo.
  3. Madam, good night: commend me to your daughter.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. I will, and know her mind early to-morrow;
  3. To-night she is mew'd up to her heaviness.
  1. CAPULET
  2. Sir Paris, I will make a desperate tender
  3. Of my child's love: I think she will be ruled
  4. In all respects by me; nay, more, I doubt it not.
  5. Wife, go you to her ere you go to bed;
  6. Acquaint her here of my son Paris' love;
  7. And bid her, mark you me, on Wednesday next —
  8. But, soft! what day is this?
  1. PARIS
  2. Monday, my lord,
  1. CAPULET
  2. Monday! ha, ha! Well, Wednesday is too soon,
  3. O' Thursday let it be: o' Thursday, tell her,
  4. She shall be married to this noble earl.
  5. Will you be ready? do you like this haste?
  6. We'll keep no great ado — a friend or two;
  7. For, hark you, Tybalt being slain so late,
  8. It may be thought we held him carelessly,
  9. Being our kinsman, if we revel much:
  10. Therefore we'll have some half a dozen friends,
  11. And there an end. But what say you to Thursday?
  1. PARIS
  2. My lord, I would that Thursday were to-morrow.
  1. CAPULET
  2. Well get you gone: o' Thursday be it, then.
  3. Go you to Juliet ere you go to bed,
  4. Prepare her, wife, against this wedding-day.
  5. Farewell, my lord. Light to my chamber, ho!
  6. Afore me! it is so very very late,
  7. That we may call it early by and by.
  8. Good night.
Exeunt

SCENE V. Capulet's orchard.

Enter ROMEO and JULIET above, at the window
  1. JULIET
  2. Wilt thou be gone? it is not yet near day:
  3. It was the nightingale, and not the lark,
  4. That pierced the fearful hollow of thine ear;
  5. Nightly she sings on yon pomegranate-tree:
  6. Believe me, love, it was the nightingale.
  1. ROMEO
  2. It was the lark, the herald of the morn,
  3. No nightingale: look, love, what envious streaks
  4. Do lace the severing clouds in yonder east:
  5. Night's candles are burnt out, and jocund day
  6. Stands tiptoe on the misty mountain tops.
  7. I must be gone and live, or stay and die.
  1. JULIET
  2. Yon light is not day-light, I know it, I:
  3. It is some meteor that the sun exhales,
  4. To be to thee this night a torch-bearer,
  5. And light thee on thy way to Mantua:
  6. Therefore stay yet; thou need'st not to be gone.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Let me be ta'en, let me be put to death;
  3. I am content, so thou wilt have it so.
  4. I'll say yon grey is not the morning's eye,
  5. 'Tis but the pale reflex of Cynthia's brow;
  6. Nor that is not the lark, whose notes do beat
  7. The vaulty heaven so high above our heads:
  8. I have more care to stay than will to go:
  9. Come, death, and welcome! Juliet wills it so.
  10. How is't, my soul? let's talk; it is not day.
  1. JULIET
  2. It is, it is: hie hence, be gone, away!
  3. It is the lark that sings so out of tune,
  4. Straining harsh discords and unpleasing sharps.
  5. Some say the lark makes sweet division;
  6. This doth not so, for she divideth us:
  7. Some say the lark and loathed toad change eyes,
  8. O, now I would they had changed voices too!
  9. Since arm from arm that voice doth us affray,
  10. Hunting thee hence with hunt's-up to the day,
  11. O, now be gone; more light and light it grows.
  1. ROMEO
  2. More light and light; more dark and dark our woes!
Enter Nurse, to the chamber
  1. Nurse
  2. Madam!
  1. JULIET
  2. Nurse?
  1. Nurse
  2. Your lady mother is coming to your chamber:
  3. The day is broke; be wary, look about.
Exit
  1. JULIET
  2. Then, window, let day in, and let life out.
  1. ROMEO
  2. Farewell, farewell! one kiss, and I'll descend.
He goeth down
  1. JULIET
  2. Art thou gone so? love, lord, ay, husband, friend!
  3. I must hear from thee every day in the hour,
  4. For in a minute there are many days:
  5. O, by this count I shall be much in years
  6. Ere I again behold my Romeo!
  1. ROMEO
  2. Farewell!
  3. I will omit no opportunity
  4. That may convey my greetings, love, to thee.
  1. JULIET
  2. O think'st thou we shall ever meet again?
  1. ROMEO
  2. I doubt it not; and all these woes shall serve
  3. For sweet discourses in our time to come.
  1. JULIET
  2. O God, I have an ill-divining soul!
  3. Methinks I see thee, now thou art below,
  4. As one dead in the bottom of a tomb:
  5. Either my eyesight fails, or thou look'st pale.
  1. ROMEO
  2. And trust me, love, in my eye so do you:
  3. Dry sorrow drinks our blood. Adieu, adieu!
Exit
  1. JULIET
  2. O fortune, fortune! all men call thee fickle:
  3. If thou art fickle, what dost thou with him.
  4. That is renown'd for faith? Be fickle, fortune;
  5. For then, I hope, thou wilt not keep him long,
  6. But send him back.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Within Ho, daughter! are you up?
  1. JULIET
  2. Who is't that calls? is it my lady mother?
  3. Is she not down so late, or up so early?
  4. What unaccustom'd cause procures her hither?
Enter LADY CAPULET
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Why, how now, Juliet!
  1. JULIET
  2. Madam, I am not well.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Evermore weeping for your cousin's death?
  3. What, wilt thou wash him from his grave with tears?
  4. An if thou couldst, thou couldst not make him live;
  5. Therefore, have done: some grief shows much of love;
  6. But much of grief shows still some want of wit.
  1. JULIET
  2. Yet let me weep for such a feeling loss.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. So shall you feel the loss, but not the friend
  3. Which you weep for.
  1. JULIET
  2. Feeling so the loss,
  3. Cannot choose but ever weep the friend.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Well, girl, thou weep'st not so much for his death,
  3. As that the villain lives which slaughter'd him.
  1. JULIET
  2. What villain madam?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. That same villain, Romeo.
  1. JULIET
  2. Aside Villain and he be many miles asunder. —
  3. God Pardon him! I do, with all my heart;
  4. And yet no man like he doth grieve my heart.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. That is, because the traitor murderer lives.
  1. JULIET
  2. Ay, madam, from the reach of these my hands:
  3. Would none but I might venge my cousin's death!
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. We will have vengeance for it, fear thou not:
  3. Then weep no more. I'll send to one in Mantua,
  4. Where that same banish'd runagate doth live,
  5. Shall give him such an unaccustom'd dram,
  6. That he shall soon keep Tybalt company:
  7. And then, I hope, thou wilt be satisfied.
  1. JULIET
  2. Indeed, I never shall be satisfied
  3. With Romeo, till I behold him — dead —
  4. Is my poor heart for a kinsman vex'd.
  5. Madam, if you could find out but a man
  6. To bear a poison, I would temper it;
  7. That Romeo should, upon receipt thereof,
  8. Soon sleep in quiet. O, how my heart abhors
  9. To hear him named, and cannot come to him.
  10. To wreak the love I bore my cousin
  11. Upon his body that slaughter'd him!
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Find thou the means, and I'll find such a man.
  3. But now I'll tell thee joyful tidings, girl.
  1. JULIET
  2. And joy comes well in such a needy time:
  3. What are they, I beseech your ladyship?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Well, well, thou hast a careful father, child;
  3. One who, to put thee from thy heaviness,
  4. Hath sorted out a sudden day of joy,
  5. That thou expect'st not nor I look'd not for.
  1. JULIET
  2. Madam, in happy time, what day is that?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Marry, my child, early next Thursday morn,
  3. The gallant, young and noble gentleman,
  4. The County Paris, at Saint Peter's Church,
  5. Shall happily make thee there a joyful bride.
  1. JULIET
  2. Now, by Saint Peter's Church and Peter too,
  3. He shall not make me there a joyful bride.
  4. I wonder at this haste; that I must wed
  5. Ere he, that should be husband, comes to woo.
  6. I pray you, tell my lord and father, madam,
  7. I will not marry yet; and, when I do, I swear,
  8. It shall be Romeo, whom you know I hate,
  9. Rather than Paris. These are news indeed!
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Here comes your father; tell him so yourself,
  3. And see how he will take it at your hands.
Enter CAPULET and Nurse
  1. CAPULET
  2. When the sun sets, the air doth drizzle dew;
  3. But for the sunset of my brother's son
  4. It rains downright.
  5. How now! a conduit, girl? what, still in tears?
  6. Evermore showering? In one little body
  7. Thou counterfeit'st a bark, a sea, a wind;
  8. For still thy eyes, which I may call the sea,
  9. Do ebb and flow with tears; the bark thy body is,
  10. Sailing in this salt flood; the winds, thy sighs;
  11. Who, raging with thy tears, and they with them,
  12. Without a sudden calm, will overset
  13. Thy tempest-tossed body. How now, wife!
  14. Have you deliver'd to her our decree?
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Ay, sir; but she will none, she gives you thanks.
  3. I would the fool were married to her grave!
  1. CAPULET
  2. Soft! take me with you, take me with you, wife.
  3. How! will she none? doth she not give us thanks?
  4. Is she not proud? doth she not count her blest,
  5. Unworthy as she is, that we have wrought
  6. So worthy a gentleman to be her bridegroom?
  1. JULIET
  2. Not proud, you have; but thankful, that you have:
  3. Proud can I never be of what I hate;
  4. But thankful even for hate, that is meant love.
  1. CAPULET
  2. How now, how now, chop-logic! What is this?
  3. 'Proud,' and 'I thank you,' and 'I thank you not;'
  4. And yet 'not proud,' mistress minion, you,
  5. Thank me no thankings, nor, proud me no prouds,
  6. But fettle your fine joints 'gainst Thursday next,
  7. To go with Paris to Saint Peter's Church,
  8. Or I will drag thee on a hurdle thither.
  9. Out, you green-sickness carrion! out, you baggage!
  10. You tallow-face!
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Fie, fie! what, are you mad?
  1. JULIET
  2. Good father, I beseech you on my knees,
  3. Hear me with patience but to speak a word.
  1. CAPULET
  2. Hang thee, young baggage! disobedient wretch!
  3. I tell thee what: get thee to church o' Thursday,
  4. Or never after look me in the face:
  5. Speak not, reply not, do not answer me;
  6. My fingers itch. Wife, we scarce thought us blest
  7. That God had lent us but this only child;
  8. But now I see this one is one too much,
  9. And that we have a curse in having her:
  10. Out on her, hilding!
  1. Nurse
  2. God in heaven bless her!
  3. You are to blame, my lord, to rate her so.
  1. CAPULET
  2. And why, my lady wisdom? hold your tongue,

ACT IV

SCENE I. Friar Laurence's cell.

Enter FRIAR LAURENCE and PARIS
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. On Thursday, sir? the time is very short.
  1. PARIS
  2. My father Capulet will have it so;
  3. And I am nothing slow to slack his haste.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. You say you do not know the lady's mind:
  3. Uneven is the course, I like it not.
  1. PARIS
  2. Immoderately she weeps for Tybalt's death,
  3. And therefore have I little talk'd of love;
  4. For Venus smiles not in a house of tears.
  5. Now, sir, her father counts it dangerous
  6. That she doth give her sorrow so much sway,
  7. And in his wisdom hastes our marriage,
  8. To stop the inundation of her tears;
  9. Which, too much minded by herself alone,
  10. May be put from her by society:
  11. Now do you know the reason of this haste.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Aside I would I knew not why it should be slow'd.
  3. Look, sir, here comes the lady towards my cell.
Enter JULIET
  1. PARIS
  2. Happily met, my lady and my wife!
  1. JULIET
  2. That may be, sir, when I may be a wife.
  1. PARIS
  2. That may be must be, love, on Thursday next.
  1. JULIET
  2. What must be shall be.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. That's a certain text.
  1. PARIS
  2. Come you to make confession to this father?
  1. JULIET
  2. To answer that, I should confess to you.
  1. PARIS
  2. Do not deny to him that you love me.
  1. JULIET
  2. I will confess to you that I love him.
  1. PARIS
  2. So will ye, I am sure, that you love me.
  1. JULIET
  2. If I do so, it will be of more price,
  3. Being spoke behind your back, than to your face.
  1. PARIS
  2. Poor soul, thy face is much abused with tears.
  1. JULIET
  2. The tears have got small victory by that;
  3. For it was bad enough before their spite.
  1. PARIS
  2. Thou wrong'st it, more than tears, with that report.
  1. JULIET
  2. That is no slander, sir, which is a truth;
  3. And what I spake, I spake it to my face.
  1. PARIS
  2. Thy face is mine, and thou hast slander'd it.
  1. JULIET
  2. It may be so, for it is not mine own.
  3. Are you at leisure, holy father, now;
  4. Or shall I come to you at evening mass?
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. My leisure serves me, pensive daughter, now.
  3. My lord, we must entreat the time alone.
  1. PARIS
  2. God shield I should disturb devotion!
  3. Juliet, on Thursday early will I rouse ye:
  4. Till then, adieu; and keep this holy kiss.
Exit
  1. JULIET
  2. O shut the door! and when thou hast done so,
  3. Come weep with me; past hope, past cure, past help!
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Ah, Juliet, I already know thy grief;
  3. It strains me past the compass of my wits:
  4. I hear thou must, and nothing may prorogue it,
  5. On Thursday next be married to this county.
  1. JULIET
  2. Tell me not, friar, that thou hear'st of this,
  3. Unless thou tell me how I may prevent it:
  4. If, in thy wisdom, thou canst give no help,
  5. Do thou but call my resolution wise,
  6. And with this knife I'll help it presently.
  7. God join'd my heart and Romeo's, thou our hands;
  8. And ere this hand, by thee to Romeo seal'd,
  9. Shall be the label to another deed,
  10. Or my true heart with treacherous revolt
  11. Turn to another, this shall slay them both:
  12. Therefore, out of thy long-experienced time,
  13. Give me some present counsel, or, behold,
  14. 'Twixt my extremes and me this bloody knife
  15. Shall play the umpire, arbitrating that
  16. Which the commission of thy years and art
  17. Could to no issue of true honour bring.
  18. Be not so long to speak; I long to die,
  19. If what thou speak'st speak not of remedy.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hold, daughter: I do spy a kind of hope,
  3. Which craves as desperate an execution.
  4. As that is desperate which we would prevent.
  5. If, rather than to marry County Paris,
  6. Thou hast the strength of will to slay thyself,
  7. Then is it likely thou wilt undertake
  8. A thing like death to chide away this shame,
  9. That copest with death himself to scape from it:
  10. And, if thou darest, I'll give thee remedy.
  1. JULIET
  2. O, bid me leap, rather than marry Paris,
  3. From off the battlements of yonder tower;
  4. Or walk in thievish ways; or bid me lurk
  5. Where serpents are; chain me with roaring bears;
  6. Or shut me nightly in a charnel-house,
  7. O'er-cover'd quite with dead men's rattling bones,
  8. With reeky shanks and yellow chapless skulls;
  9. Or bid me go into a new-made grave
  10. And hide me with a dead man in his shroud;
  11. Things that, to hear them told, have made me tremble;
  12. And I will do it without fear or doubt,
  13. To live an unstain'd wife to my sweet love.
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hold, then; go home, be merry, give consent
  3. To marry Paris: Wednesday is to-morrow:
  4. To-morrow night look that thou lie alone;
  5. Let not thy nurse lie with thee in thy chamber:
  6. Take thou this vial, being then in bed,
  7. And this distilled liquor drink thou off;
  8. When presently through all thy veins shall run
  9. A cold and drowsy humour, for no pulse
  10. Shall keep his native progress, but surcease:
  11. No warmth, no breath, shall testify thou livest;
  12. The roses in thy lips and cheeks shall fade
  13. To paly ashes, thy eyes' windows fall,
  14. Like death, when he shuts up the day of life;
  15. Each part, deprived of supple government,
  16. Shall, stiff and stark and cold, appear like death:
  17. And in this borrow'd likeness of shrunk death
  18. Thou shalt continue two and forty hours,
  19. And then awake as from a pleasant sleep.
  20. Now, when the bridegroom in the morning comes
  21. To rouse thee from thy bed, there art thou dead:
  22. Then, as the manner of our country is,
  23. In thy best robes uncover'd on the bier
  24. Thou shalt be borne to that same ancient vault
  25. Where all the kindred of the Capulets lie.
  26. In the mean time, against thou shalt awake,
  27. Shall Romeo by my letters know our drift,
  28. And hither shall he come: and he and I
  29. Will watch thy waking, and that very night
  30. Shall Romeo bear thee hence to Mantua.
  31. And this shall free thee from this present shame;
  32. If no inconstant toy, nor womanish fear,
  33. Abate thy valour in the acting it.
  1. JULIET
  2. Give me, give me! O, tell not me of fear!
  1. FRIAR LAURENCE
  2. Hold; get you gone, be strong and prosperous
  3. In this resolve: I'll send a friar with speed
  4. To Mantua, with my letters to thy lord.
  1. JULIET
  2. Love give me strength! and strength shall help afford.
  3. Farewell, dear father!
Exeunt

  5. To move the heavens to smile upon my state,
  6. Which, well thou know'st, is cross, and full of sin.

  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. What, are you busy, ho? need you my help?
  1. JULIET
  2. No, madam; we have cull'd such necessaries
  3. As are behoveful for our state to-morrow:
  4. So please you, let me now be left alone,
  5. And let the nurse this night sit up with you;
  6. For, I am sure, you have your hands full all,
  7. In this so sudden business.
  1. LADY CAPULET
  2. Good night:
  3. Get thee to bed, and rest; for thou hast need.
Exeunt LADY CAPULET and Nurse
  1. JULIET
  2. Farewell! God knows when we shall meet again.
  3. I have a faint cold fear thrills through my veins,
  4. That almost freezes up the heat of life:
  5. I'll call them back again to comfort me:
  6. Nurse! What should she do here?
  7. My dismal scene I needs must act alone.
  8. Come, vial.
  9. What if this mixture do not work at all?
  10. Shall I be married then to-morrow morning?
  11. No, no: this shall forbid it: lie thou there.
  12. Laying down her dagger
  13. What if it be a poison, which the friar
  14. Subtly hath minister'd to have me dead,
  15. Lest in this marriage he should be dishonour'd,
  16. Because he married me before to Romeo?
  17. I fear it is: and yet, methinks, it should not,
  18. For he hath still been tried a holy man.
  19. How if, when I am laid into the tomb,
  20. I wake before the time that Romeo
  21. Come to redeem me? there's a fearful point!
  22. Shall I not, then, be stifled in the vault,
  23. To whose foul mouth no healthsome air breathes in,
  24. And there die strangled ere my Romeo comes?
  25. Or, if I live, is it not very like,
  26. The horrible conceit of death and night,
  27. Together with the terror of the place —
  28. As in a vault, an ancient receptacle,
  29. Where, for these many hundred years, the bones
  30. Of all my buried ancestors are packed:
  31. Where bloody Tybalt, yet but green in earth,
  32. Lies festering in his shroud; where, as they say,
  33. At some hours in the night spirits resort; —
  34. Alack, alack, is it not like that I,
  35. So early waking, what with loathsome smells,
  36. And shrieks like mandrakes' torn out of the earth,
  37. That living mortals, hearing them, run mad: —
  38. O, if I wake, shall I not be distraught,
  39. Environed with all these hideous fears?
  40. And madly play with my forefather's joints?
  41. And pluck the mangled Tybalt from his shroud?
  42. And, in this rage, with some great kinsman's bone,
  43. As with a club, dash out my desperate brains?
  44. O, look! methinks I see my cousin's ghost
  45. Seeking out Romeo, that did spit his body
  46. Upon a rapier's point: stay, Tybalt, stay!
  47. Romeo, I come! this do I drink to thee.
