ACT I

SCENE I. Britain. The garden of Cymbeline's palace.

Enter two Gentlemen
  1. First Gentleman
  2. You do not meet a man but frowns: our bloods
  3. No more obey the heavens than our courtiers
  4. Still seem as does the king.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. But what's the matter?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. His daughter, and the heir of's kingdom, whom
  3. He purposed to his wife's sole son — a widow
  4. That late he married — hath referr'd herself
  5. Unto a poor but worthy gentleman: she's wedded;
  6. Her husband banish'd; she imprison'd: all
  7. Is outward sorrow; though I think the king
  8. Be touch'd at very heart.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. None but the king?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. He that hath lost her too; so is the queen,
  3. That most desired the match; but not a courtier,
  4. Although they wear their faces to the bent
  5. Of the king's look's, hath a heart that is not
  6. Glad at the thing they scowl at.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. And why so?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. He that hath miss'd the princess is a thing
  3. Too bad for bad report: and he that hath her —
  4. I mean, that married her, alack, good man!
  5. And therefore banish'd — is a creature such
  6. As, to seek through the regions of the earth
  7. For one his like, there would be something failing
  8. In him that should compare. I do not think
  9. So fair an outward and such stuff within
  10. Endows a man but he.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. You speak him far.
  1. First Gentleman
  2. I do extend him, sir, within himself,
  3. Crush him together rather than unfold
  4. His measure duly.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. What's his name and birth?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. I cannot delve him to the root: his father
  3. Was call'd Sicilius, who did join his honour
  4. Against the Romans with Cassibelan,
  5. But had his titles by Tenantius whom
  6. He served with glory and admired success,
  7. So gain'd the sur-addition Leonatus;
  8. And had, besides this gentleman in question,
  9. Two other sons, who in the wars o' the time
  10. Died with their swords in hand; for which
  11. their father,
  12. Then old and fond of issue, took such sorrow
  13. That he quit being, and his gentle lady,
  14. Big of this gentleman our theme, deceased
  15. As he was born. The king he takes the babe
  16. To his protection, calls him Posthumus Leonatus,
  17. Breeds him and makes him of his bed-chamber,
  18. Puts to him all the learnings that his time
  19. Could make him the receiver of; which he took,
  20. As we do air, fast as 'twas minister'd,
  21. And in's spring became a harvest, lived in court —
  22. Which rare it is to do — most praised, most loved,
  23. A sample to the youngest, to the more mature
  24. A glass that feated them, and to the graver
  25. A child that guided dotards; to his mistress,
  26. For whom he now is banish'd, her own price
  27. Proclaims how she esteem'd him and his virtue;
  28. By her election may be truly read
  29. What kind of man he is.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. I honour him
  3. Even out of your report. But, pray you, tell me,
  4. Is she sole child to the king?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. His only child.
  3. He had two sons: if this be worth your hearing,
  4. Mark it: the eldest of them at three years old,
  5. I' the swathing-clothes the other, from their nursery
  6. Were stol'n, and to this hour no guess in knowledge
  7. Which way they went.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. How long is this ago?
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Some twenty years.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. That a king's children should be so convey'd,
  3. So slackly guarded, and the search so slow,
  4. That could not trace them!
  1. First Gentleman
  2. Howsoe'er 'tis strange,
  3. Or that the negligence may well be laugh'd at,
  4. Yet is it true, sir.
  1. Second Gentleman
  2. I do well believe you.
  1. First Gentleman
  2. We must forbear: here comes the gentleman,
  3. The queen, and princess.
Exeunt
Enter the QUEEN, POSTHUMUS LEONATUS, and IMOGEN
  1. QUEEN
  2. No, be assured you shall not find me, daughter,
  3. After the slander of most stepmothers,
  4. Evil-eyed unto you: you're my prisoner, but
  5. Your gaoler shall deliver you the keys
  6. That lock up your restraint. For you, Posthumus,
  7. So soon as I can win the offended king,
  8. I will be known your advocate: marry, yet
  9. The fire of rage is in him, and 'twere good
  10. You lean'd unto his sentence with what patience
  11. Your wisdom may inform you.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Please your highness,
  3. I will from hence to-day.
  1. QUEEN
  2. You know the peril.
  3. I'll fetch a turn about the garden, pitying
  4. The pangs of barr'd affections, though the king
  5. Hath charged you should not speak together.
Exit
  1. IMOGEN
  2. O
  3. Dissembling courtesy! How fine this tyrant
  4. Can tickle where she wounds! My dearest husband,
  5. I something fear my father's wrath; but nothing —
  6. Always reserved my holy duty — what
  7. His rage can do on me: you must be gone;
  8. And I shall here abide the hourly shot
  9. Of angry eyes, not comforted to live,
  10. But that there is this jewel in the world
  11. That I may see again.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. My queen! my mistress!
  3. O lady, weep no more, lest I give cause
  4. To be suspected of more tenderness
  5. Than doth become a man. I will remain
  6. The loyal'st husband that did e'er plight troth:
  7. My residence in Rome at one Philario's,
  8. Who to my father was a friend, to me
  9. Known but by letter: thither write, my queen,
  10. And with mine eyes I'll drink the words you send,
  11. Though ink be made of gall.
Re-enter QUEEN
  1. QUEEN
  2. Be brief, I pray you:
  3. If the king come, I shall incur I know not
  4. How much of his displeasure.
  5. Aside
  6. Yet I'll move him
  7. To walk this way: I never do him wrong,
  8. But he does buy my injuries, to be friends;
  9. Pays dear for my offences.
Exit
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Should we be taking leave
  3. As long a term as yet we have to live,
  4. The loathness to depart would grow. Adieu!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Nay, stay a little:
  3. Were you but riding forth to air yourself,
  4. Such parting were too petty. Look here, love;
  5. This diamond was my mother's: take it, heart;
  6. But keep it till you woo another wife,
  7. When Imogen is dead.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. How, how! another?
  3. You gentle gods, give me but this I have,
  4. And sear up my embracements from a next
  5. With bonds of death!
  6. Putting on the ring
  7. Remain, remain thou here
  8. While sense can keep it on. And, sweetest, fairest,
  9. As I my poor self did exchange for you,
  10. To your so infinite loss, so in our trifles
  11. I still win of you: for my sake wear this;
  12. It is a manacle of love; I'll place it
  13. Upon this fairest prisoner.
Putting a bracelet upon her arm
  1. IMOGEN
  2. O the gods!
  3. When shall we see again?
Enter CYMBELINE and Lords
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Alack, the king!
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Thou basest thing, avoid! hence, from my sight!
  3. If after this command thou fraught the court
  4. With thy unworthiness, thou diest: away!
  5. Thou'rt poison to my blood.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. The gods protect you!
  3. And bless the good remainders of the court! I am gone.
Exit
  1. IMOGEN
  2. There cannot be a pinch in death
  3. More sharp than this is.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. O disloyal thing,
  3. That shouldst repair my youth, thou heap'st
  4. A year's age on me.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. I beseech you, sir,
  3. Harm not yourself with your vexation
  4. I am senseless of your wrath; a touch more rare
  5. Subdues all pangs, all fears.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Past grace? obedience?
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Past hope, and in despair; that way, past grace.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. That mightst have had the sole son of my queen!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. O blest, that I might not! I chose an eagle,
  3. And did avoid a puttock.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Thou took'st a beggar; wouldst have made my throne
  3. A seat for baseness.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. No; I rather added
  3. A lustre to it.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. O thou vile one!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Sir,
  3. It is your fault that I have loved Posthumus:
  4. You bred him as my playfellow, and he is
  5. A man worth any woman, overbuys me
  6. Almost the sum he pays.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. What, art thou mad?
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Almost, sir: heaven restore me! Would I were
  3. A neat-herd's daughter, and my Leonatus
  4. Our neighbour shepherd's son!
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Thou foolish thing!
  3. Re-enter QUEEN
  4. They were again together: you have done
  5. Not after our command. Away with her,
  6. And pen her up.
  1. QUEEN
  2. Beseech your patience. Peace,
  3. Dear lady daughter, peace! Sweet sovereign,
  4. Leave us to ourselves; and make yourself some comfort
  5. Out of your best advice.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Nay, let her languish
  3. A drop of blood a day; and, being aged,
  4. Die of this folly!
Exeunt CYMBELINE and Lords
  1. QUEEN
  2. Fie! you must give way.
  3. Enter PISANIO
  4. Here is your servant. How now, sir! What news?
  1. PISANIO
  2. My lord your son drew on my master.
  1. QUEEN
  2. Ha!
  3. No harm, I trust, is done?
  1. PISANIO
  2. There might have been,
  3. But that my master rather play'd than fought
  4. And had no help of anger: they were parted
  5. By gentlemen at hand.
  1. QUEEN
  2. I am very glad on't.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Your son's my father's friend; he takes his part.
  3. To draw upon an exile! O brave sir!
  4. I would they were in Afric both together;
  5. Myself by with a needle, that I might prick
  6. The goer-back. Why came you from your master?
  1. PISANIO
  2. On his command: he would not suffer me
  3. To bring him to the haven; left these notes
  4. Of what commands I should be subject to,
  5. When 't pleased you to employ me.
  1. QUEEN
  2. This hath been
  3. Your faithful servant: I dare lay mine honour
  4. He will remain so.
  1. PISANIO
  2. I humbly thank your highness.
  1. QUEEN
  2. Pray, walk awhile.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. About some half-hour hence,
  3. I pray you, speak with me: you shall at least
  4. Go see my lord aboard: for this time leave me.
Exeunt

SCENE II. The same. A public place.

Enter CLOTEN and two Lords
  1. First Lord
  2. Sir, I would advise you to shift a shirt; the
  3. violence of action hath made you reek as a
  4. sacrifice: where air comes out, air comes in:
  5. there's none abroad so wholesome as that you vent.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. If my shirt were bloody, then to shift it. Have I hurt him?
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside No, 'faith; not so much as his patience.
  1. First Lord
  2. Hurt him! his body's a passable carcass, if he be
  3. not hurt: it is a thoroughfare for steel, if it be not hurt.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside His steel was in debt; it went o' the
  3. backside the town.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. The villain would not stand me.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside No; but he fled forward still, toward your face.
  1. First Lord
  2. Stand you! You have land enough of your own: but
  3. he added to your having; gave you some ground.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside As many inches as you have oceans. Puppies!
  1. CLOTEN
  2. I would they had not come between us.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside So would I, till you had measured how long
  3. a fool you were upon the ground.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. And that she should love this fellow and refuse me!
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside If it be a sin to make a true election, she
  3. is damned.
  1. First Lord
  2. Sir, as I told you always, her beauty and her brain
  3. go not together: she's a good sign, but I have seen
  4. small reflection of her wit.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside She shines not upon fools, lest the
  3. reflection should hurt her.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. Come, I'll to my chamber. Would there had been some
  3. hurt done!
  1. Second Lord
  2. Aside I wish not so; unless it had been the fall
  3. of an ass, which is no great hurt.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. You'll go with us?
  1. First Lord
  2. I'll attend your lordship.
  1. CLOTEN
  2. Nay, come, let's go together.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Well, my lord.
Exeunt

SCENE III. A room in Cymbeline's palace.

Enter IMOGEN and PISANIO
  1. IMOGEN
  2. I would thou grew'st unto the shores o' the haven,
  3. And question'dst every sail: if he should write
  4. And not have it, 'twere a paper lost,
  5. As offer'd mercy is. What was the last
  6. That he spake to thee?
  1. PISANIO
  2. It was his queen, his queen!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Then waved his handkerchief?
  1. PISANIO
  2. And kiss'd it, madam.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Senseless Linen! happier therein than I!
  3. And that was all?
  1. PISANIO
  2. No, madam; for so long
  3. As he could make me with this eye or ear
  4. Distinguish him from others, he did keep
  5. The deck, with glove, or hat, or handkerchief,
  6. Still waving, as the fits and stirs of 's mind
  7. Could best express how slow his soul sail'd on,
  8. How swift his ship.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Thou shouldst have made him
  3. As little as a crow, or less, ere left
  4. To after-eye him.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Madam, so I did.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. I would have broke mine eye-strings; crack'd them, but
  3. To look upon him, till the diminution
  4. Of space had pointed him sharp as my needle,
  5. Nay, follow'd him, till he had melted from
  6. The smallness of a gnat to air, and then
  7. Have turn'd mine eye and wept. But, good Pisanio,
  8. When shall we hear from him?
  1. PISANIO
  2. Be assured, madam,
  3. With his next vantage.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. I did not take my leave of him, but had
  3. Most pretty things to say: ere I could tell him
  4. How I would think on him at certain hours
  5. Such thoughts and such, or I could make him swear
  6. The shes of Italy should not betray
  7. Mine interest and his honour, or have charged him,
  8. At the sixth hour of morn, at noon, at midnight,
  9. To encounter me with orisons, for then
  10. I am in heaven for him; or ere I could
  11. Give him that parting kiss which I had set
  12. Betwixt two charming words, comes in my father
  13. And like the tyrannous breathing of the north
  14. Shakes all our buds from growing.
Enter a Lady
  1. Lady
  2. The queen, madam,
  3. Desires your highness' company.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Those things I bid you do, get them dispatch'd.
  3. I will attend the queen.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Madam, I shall.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. Rome. Philario's house.

Enter PHILARIO, IACHIMO, a Frenchman, a Dutchman, and a Spaniard
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Believe it, sir, I have seen him in Britain: he was
  3. then of a crescent note, expected to prove so worthy
  4. as since he hath been allowed the name of; but I
  5. could then have looked on him without the help of
  6. admiration, though the catalogue of his endowments
  7. had been tabled by his side and I to peruse him by items.
  1. PHILARIO
  2. You speak of him when he was less furnished than now
  3. he is with that which makes him both without and within.
  1. Frenchman
  2. I have seen him in France: we had very many there
  3. could behold the sun with as firm eyes as he.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. This matter of marrying his king's daughter, wherein
  3. he must be weighed rather by her value than his own,
  4. words him, I doubt not, a great deal from the matter.
  1. Frenchman
  2. And then his banishment.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Ay, and the approbation of those that weep this
  3. lamentable divorce under her colours are wonderfully
  4. to extend him; be it but to fortify her judgment,
  5. which else an easy battery might lay flat, for
  6. taking a beggar without less quality. But how comes
  7. it he is to sojourn with you? How creeps
  8. acquaintance?
  1. PHILARIO
  2. His father and I were soldiers together; to whom I
  3. have been often bound for no less than my life.
  4. Here comes the Briton: let him be so entertained
  5. amongst you as suits, with gentlemen of your
  6. knowing, to a stranger of his quality.
  7. Enter POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  8. I beseech you all, be better known to this
  9. gentleman; whom I commend to you as a noble friend
  10. of mine: how worthy he is I will leave to appear
  11. hereafter, rather than story him in his own hearing.
  1. Frenchman
  2. Sir, we have known together in Orleans.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Since when I have been debtor to you for courtesies,
  3. which I will be ever to pay and yet pay still.
  1. Frenchman
  2. Sir, you o'er-rate my poor kindness: I was glad I
  3. did atone my countryman and you; it had been pity
  4. you should have been put together with so mortal a
  5. purpose as then each bore, upon importance of so
  6. slight and trivial a nature.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. By your pardon, sir, I was then a young traveller;
  3. rather shunned to go even with what I heard than in
  4. my every action to be guided by others' experiences:
  5. but upon my mended judgment — if I offend not to say
  6. it is mended — my quarrel was not altogether slight.
  1. Frenchman
  2. 'Faith, yes, to be put to the arbitrement of swords,
  3. and by such two that would by all likelihood have
  4. confounded one the other, or have fallen both.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Can we, with manners, ask what was the difference?
  1. Frenchman
  2. Safely, I think: 'twas a contention in public,
  3. which may, without contradiction, suffer the report.
  4. It was much like an argument that fell out last
  5. night, where each of us fell in praise of our
  6. country mistresses; this gentleman at that time
  7. vouching — and upon warrant of bloody
  8. affirmation — his to be more fair, virtuous, wise,
  9. chaste, constant-qualified and less attemptable
  10. than any the rarest of our ladies in France.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. That lady is not now living, or this gentleman's
  3. opinion by this worn out.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. She holds her virtue still and I my mind.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. You must not so far prefer her 'fore ours of Italy.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Being so far provoked as I was in France, I would
  3. abate her nothing, though I profess myself her
  4. adorer, not her friend.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. As fair and as good — a kind of hand-in-hand
  3. comparison — had been something too fair and too good
  4. for any lady in Britain. If she went before others
  5. I have seen, as that diamond of yours outlustres
  6. many I have beheld. I could not but believe she
  7. excelled many: but I have not seen the most
  8. precious diamond that is, nor you the lady.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. I praised her as I rated her: so do I my stone.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. What do you esteem it at?
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. More than the world enjoys.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Either your unparagoned mistress is dead, or she's
  3. outprized by a trifle.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. You are mistaken: the one may be sold, or given, if
  3. there were wealth enough for the purchase, or merit
  4. for the gift: the other is not a thing for sale,
  5. and only the gift of the gods.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Which the gods have given you?
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Which, by their graces, I will keep.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. You may wear her in title yours: but, you know,
  3. strange fowl light upon neighbouring ponds. Your
  4. ring may be stolen too: so your brace of unprizable
  5. estimations; the one is but frail and the other
  6. casual; a cunning thief, or a that way accomplished
  7. courtier, would hazard the winning both of first and last.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Your Italy contains none so accomplished a courtier
  3. to convince the honour of my mistress, if, in the
  4. holding or loss of that, you term her frail. I do
  5. nothing doubt you have store of thieves;
  6. notwithstanding, I fear not my ring.
  1. PHILARIO
  2. Let us leave here, gentlemen.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. Sir, with all my heart. This worthy signior, I
  3. thank him, makes no stranger of me; we are familiar at first.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. With five times so much conversation, I should get
  3. ground of your fair mistress, make her go back, even
  4. to the yielding, had I admittance and opportunity to friend.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. No, no.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. I dare thereupon pawn the moiety of my estate to
  3. your ring; which, in my opinion, o'ervalues it
  4. something: but I make my wager rather against your
  5. confidence than her reputation: and, to bar your
  6. offence herein too, I durst attempt it against any
  7. lady in the world.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. You are a great deal abused in too bold a
  3. persuasion; and I doubt not you sustain what you're
  4. worthy of by your attempt.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. What's that?
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. A repulse: though your attempt, as you call it,
  3. deserve more; a punishment too.
  1. PHILARIO
  2. Gentlemen, enough of this: it came in too suddenly;
  3. let it die as it was born, and, I pray you, be
  4. better acquainted.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Would I had put my estate and my neighbour's on the
  3. approbation of what I have spoke!
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. What lady would you choose to assail?
  1. IACHIMO
  2. Yours; whom in constancy you think stands so safe.
  3. I will lay you ten thousand ducats to your ring,
  4. that, commend me to the court where your lady is,
  5. with no more advantage than the opportunity of a
  6. second conference, and I will bring from thence
  7. that honour of hers which you imagine so reserved.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. I will wage against your gold, gold to it: my ring
  3. I hold dear as my finger; 'tis part of it.
  1. IACHIMO
  2. You are afraid, and therein the wiser. If you buy
  3. ladies' flesh at a million a dram, you cannot
  4. preserve it from tainting: but I see you have some
  5. religion in you, that you fear.
  1. POSTHUMUS LEONATUS
  2. This is but a custom in your tongue; you bear a
  3. graver purpose, I hope.
  1. IACHIMO

SCENE III. Wales: a mountainous country with a cave.

Enter, from the cave, BELARIUS; GUIDERIUS, and ARVIRAGUS following
  1. BELARIUS
  2. A goodly day not to keep house, with such
  3. Whose roof's as low as ours! Stoop, boys; this gate
  4. Instructs you how to adore the heavens and bows you
  5. To a morning's holy office: the gates of monarchs
  6. Are arch'd so high that giants may jet through
  7. And keep their impious turbans on, without
  8. Good morrow to the sun. Hail, thou fair heaven!
  9. We house i' the rock, yet use thee not so hardly
  10. As prouder livers do.
  1. GUIDERIUS
  2. Hail, heaven!
  1. ARVIRAGUS
  2. Hail, heaven!
  1. BELARIUS
  2. Now for our mountain sport: up to yond hill;
  3. Your legs are young; I'll tread these flats. Consider,
  4. When you above perceive me like a crow,
  5. That it is place which lessens and sets off;
  6. And you may then revolve what tales I have told you
  7. Of courts, of princes, of the tricks in war:
  8. This service is not service, so being done,
  9. But being so allow'd: to apprehend thus,
  10. Draws us a profit from all things we see;
  11. And often, to our comfort, shall we find
  12. The sharded beetle in a safer hold
  13. Than is the full-wing'd eagle. O, this life
  14. Is nobler than attending for a cheque,
  15. Richer than doing nothing for a bauble,
  16. Prouder than rustling in unpaid-for silk:
  17. Such gain the cap of him that makes 'em fine,
  18. Yet keeps his book uncross'd: no life to ours.
  1. GUIDERIUS
  2. Out of your proof you speak: we, poor unfledged,
  3. Have never wing'd from view o' the nest, nor know not
  4. What air's from home. Haply this life is best,
  5. If quiet life be best; sweeter to you
  6. That have a sharper known; well corresponding
  7. With your stiff age: but unto us it is
  8. A cell of ignorance; travelling a-bed;
  9. A prison for a debtor, that not dares
  10. To stride a limit.
  1. ARVIRAGUS
  2. What should we speak of
  3. When we are old as you? when we shall hear
  4. The rain and wind beat dark December, how,
  5. In this our pinching cave, shall we discourse
  6. The freezing hours away? We have seen nothing;
  7. We are beastly, subtle as the fox for prey,
  8. Like warlike as the wolf for what we eat;
  9. Our valour is to chase what flies; our cage
  10. We make a quire, as doth the prison'd bird,
  11. And sing our bondage freely.
  1. BELARIUS
  2. How you speak!
  3. Did you but know the city's usuries
  4. And felt them knowingly; the art o' the court
  5. As hard to leave as keep; whose top to climb
  6. Is certain falling, or so slippery that
  7. The fear's as bad as falling; the toil o' the war,
  8. A pain that only seems to seek out danger
  9. I' the name of fame and honour; which dies i'
  10. the search,
  11. And hath as oft a slanderous epitaph
  12. As record of fair act; nay, many times,
  13. Doth ill deserve by doing well; what's worse,
  14. Must court'sy at the censure: — O boys, this story
  15. The world may read in me: my body's mark'd
  16. With Roman swords, and my report was once
  17. First with the best of note: Cymbeline loved me,
  18. And when a soldier was the theme, my name
  19. Was not far off: then was I as a tree
  20. Whose boughs did bend with fruit: but in one night,
  21. A storm or robbery, call it what you will,
  22. Shook down my mellow hangings, nay, my leaves,
  23. And left me bare to weather.
  1. GUIDERIUS
  2. Uncertain favour!
  1. BELARIUS
  2. My fault being nothing — as I have told you oft —
  3. But that two villains, whose false oaths prevail'd
  4. Before my perfect honour, swore to Cymbeline
  5. I was confederate with the Romans: so
  6. Follow'd my banishment, and this twenty years
  7. This rock and these demesnes have been my world;
  8. Where I have lived at honest freedom, paid
  9. More pious debts to heaven than in all
  10. The fore-end of my time. But up to the mountains!
  11. This is not hunters' language: he that strikes
  12. The venison first shall be the lord o' the feast;
  13. To him the other two shall minister;
  14. And we will fear no poison, which attends
  15. In place of greater state. I'll meet you in the valleys.
  16. Exeunt GUIDERIUS and ARVIRAGUS
  17. How hard it is to hide the sparks of nature!
  18. These boys know little they are sons to the king;
  19. Nor Cymbeline dreams that they are alive.
  20. They think they are mine; and though train'd
  21. up thus meanly
  22. I' the cave wherein they bow, their thoughts do hit
  23. The roofs of palaces, and nature prompts them
  24. In simple and low things to prince it much
  25. Beyond the trick of others. This Polydore,
  26. The heir of Cymbeline and Britain, who
  27. The king his father call'd Guiderius — Jove!
  28. When on my three-foot stool I sit and tell
  29. The warlike feats I have done, his spirits fly out
  30. Into my story: say 'Thus, mine enemy fell,
  31. And thus I set my foot on 's neck;' even then
  32. The princely blood flows in his cheek, he sweats,
  33. Strains his young nerves and puts himself in posture
  34. That acts my words. The younger brother, Cadwal,
  35. Once Arviragus, in as like a figure,
  36. Strikes life into my speech and shows much more
  37. His own conceiving. — Hark, the game is roused!
  38. O Cymbeline! heaven and my conscience knows
  39. Thou didst unjustly banish me: whereon,
  40. At three and two years old, I stole these babes;
  41. Thinking to bar thee of succession, as
  42. Thou reft'st me of my lands. Euriphile,
  43. Thou wast their nurse; they took thee for
  44. their mother,
  45. And every day do honour to her grave:
  46. Myself, Belarius, that am Morgan call'd,
  47. They take for natural father. The game is up.
Exit

SCENE IV. Country near Milford-Haven.

Enter PISANIO and IMOGEN
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Thou told'st me, when we came from horse, the place
  3. Was near at hand: ne'er long'd my mother so
  4. To see me first, as I have now. Pisanio! man!
  5. Where is Posthumus? What is in thy mind,
  6. That makes thee stare thus? Wherefore breaks that sigh
  7. From the inward of thee? One, but painted thus,
  8. Would be interpreted a thing perplex'd
  9. Beyond self-explication: put thyself
  10. Into a havior of less fear, ere wildness
  11. Vanquish my staider senses. What's the matter?
  12. Why tender'st thou that paper to me, with
  13. A look untender? If't be summer news,
  14. Smile to't before; if winterly, thou need'st
  15. But keep that countenance still. My husband's hand!
  16. That drug-damn'd Italy hath out-craftied him,
  17. And he's at some hard point. Speak, man: thy tongue
  18. May take off some extremity, which to read
  19. Would be even mortal to me.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Please you, read;
  3. And you shall find me, wretched man, a thing
  4. The most disdain'd of fortune.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Reads 'Thy mistress, Pisanio, hath played the
  3. strumpet in my bed; the testimonies whereof lie
  4. bleeding in me. I speak not out of weak surmises,
  5. but from proof as strong as my grief and as certain
  6. as I expect my revenge. That part thou, Pisanio,
  7. must act for me, if thy faith be not tainted with
  8. the breach of hers. Let thine own hands take away
  9. her life: I shall give thee opportunity at
  10. Milford-Haven. She hath my letter for the purpose
  11. where, if thou fear to strike and to make me certain
  12. it is done, thou art the pandar to her dishonour and
  13. equally to me disloyal.'
  1. PISANIO
  2. What shall I need to draw my sword? the paper
  3. Hath cut her throat already. No, 'tis slander,
  4. Whose edge is sharper than the sword, whose tongue
  5. Outvenoms all the worms of Nile, whose breath
  6. Rides on the posting winds and doth belie
  7. All corners of the world: kings, queens and states,
  8. Maids, matrons, nay, the secrets of the grave
  9. This viperous slander enters. What cheer, madam?
  1. IMOGEN
  2. False to his bed! What is it to be false?
  3. To lie in watch there and to think on him?
  4. To weep 'twixt clock and clock? if sleep
  5. charge nature,
  6. To break it with a fearful dream of him
  7. And cry myself awake? that's false to's bed, is it?
  1. PISANIO
  2. Alas, good lady!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. I false! Thy conscience witness: Iachimo,
  3. Thou didst accuse him of incontinency;
  4. Thou then look'dst like a villain; now methinks
  5. Thy favour's good enough. Some jay of Italy
  6. Whose mother was her painting, hath betray'd him:
  7. Poor I am stale, a garment out of fashion;
  8. And, for I am richer than to hang by the walls,
  9. I must be ripp'd: — to pieces with me! — O,
  10. Men's vows are women's traitors! All good seeming,
  11. By thy revolt, O husband, shall be thought
  12. Put on for villany; not born where't grows,
  13. But worn a bait for ladies.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Good madam, hear me.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. True honest men being heard, like false Aeneas,
  3. Were in his time thought false, and Sinon's weeping
  4. Did scandal many a holy tear, took pity
  5. From most true wretchedness: so thou, Posthumus,
  6. Wilt lay the leaven on all proper men;
  7. Goodly and gallant shall be false and perjured
  8. From thy great fall. Come, fellow, be thou honest:
  9. Do thou thy master's bidding: when thou see'st him,
  10. A little witness my obedience: look!
  11. I draw the sword myself: take it, and hit
  12. The innocent mansion of my love, my heart;
  13. Fear not; 'tis empty of all things but grief;
  14. Thy master is not there, who was indeed
  15. The riches of it: do his bidding; strike
  16. Thou mayst be valiant in a better cause;
  17. But now thou seem'st a coward.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Hence, vile instrument!
  3. Thou shalt not damn my hand.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Why, I must die;
  3. And if I do not by thy hand, thou art
  4. No servant of thy master's. Against self-slaughter
  5. There is a prohibition so divine
  6. That cravens my weak hand. Come, here's my heart.
  7. Something's afore't. Soft, soft! we'll no defence;
  8. Obedient as the scabbard. What is here?
  9. The scriptures of the loyal Leonatus,
  10. All turn'd to heresy? Away, away,
  11. Corrupters of my faith! you shall no more
  12. Be stomachers to my heart. Thus may poor fools
  13. Believe false teachers: though those that
  14. are betray'd
  15. Do feel the treason sharply, yet the traitor
  16. Stands in worse case of woe.
  17. And thou, Posthumus, thou that didst set up
  18. My disobedience 'gainst the king my father
  19. And make me put into contempt the suits
  20. Of princely fellows, shalt hereafter find
  21. It is no act of common passage, but
  22. A strain of rareness: and I grieve myself
  23. To think, when thou shalt be disedged by her
  24. That now thou tirest on, how thy memory
  25. Will then be pang'd by me. Prithee, dispatch:
  26. The lamb entreats the butcher: where's thy knife?
  27. Thou art too slow to do thy master's bidding,
  28. When I desire it too.
  1. PISANIO
  2. O gracious lady,
  3. Since I received command to do this business
  4. I have not slept one wink.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Do't, and to bed then.
  1. PISANIO
  2. I'll wake mine eye-balls blind first.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Wherefore then
  3. Didst undertake it? Why hast thou abused
  4. So many miles with a pretence? this place?
  5. Mine action and thine own? our horses' labour?
  6. The time inviting thee? the perturb'd court,
  7. For my being absent? whereunto I never
  8. Purpose return. Why hast thou gone so far,
  9. To be unbent when thou hast ta'en thy stand,
  10. The elected deer before thee?
  1. PISANIO
  2. But to win time
  3. To lose so bad employment; in the which
  4. I have consider'd of a course. Good lady,
  5. Hear me with patience.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Talk thy tongue weary; speak
  3. I have heard I am a strumpet; and mine ear
  4. Therein false struck, can take no greater wound,
  5. Nor tent to bottom that. But speak.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Then, madam,
  3. I thought you would not back again.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Most like;
  3. Bringing me here to kill me.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Not so, neither:
  3. But if I were as wise as honest, then
  4. My purpose would prove well. It cannot be
  5. But that my master is abused:
  6. Some villain, ay, and singular in his art.
  7. Hath done you both this cursed injury.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Some Roman courtezan.
  1. PISANIO
  2. No, on my life.
  3. I'll give but notice you are dead and send him
  4. Some bloody sign of it; for 'tis commanded
  5. I should do so: you shall be miss'd at court,
  6. And that will well confirm it.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Why good fellow,
  3. What shall I do the where? where bide? how live?
  4. Or in my life what comfort, when I am
  5. Dead to my husband?
  1. PISANIO
  2. If you'll back to the court —
  1. IMOGEN
  2. No court, no father; nor no more ado
  3. With that harsh, noble, simple nothing,
  4. That Cloten, whose love-suit hath been to me
  5. As fearful as a siege.
  1. PISANIO
  2. If not at court,
  3. Then not in Britain must you bide.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Where then
  3. Hath Britain all the sun that shines? Day, night,
  4. Are they not but in Britain? I' the world's volume
  5. Our Britain seems as of it, but not in 't;
  6. In a great pool a swan's nest: prithee, think
  7. There's livers out of Britain.
  1. PISANIO
  2. I am most glad
  3. You think of other place. The ambassador,
  4. Lucius the Roman, comes to Milford-Haven
  5. To-morrow: now, if you could wear a mind
  6. Dark as your fortune is, and but disguise
  7. That which, to appear itself, must not yet be
  8. But by self-danger, you should tread a course
  9. Pretty and full of view; yea, haply, near
  10. The residence of Posthumus; so nigh at least
  11. That though his actions were not visible, yet
  12. Report should render him hourly to your ear
  13. As truly as he moves.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. O, for such means!
  3. Though peril to my modesty, not death on't,
  4. I would adventure.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Well, then, here's the point:
  3. You must forget to be a woman; change
  4. Command into obedience: fear and niceness —
  5. The handmaids of all women, or, more truly,
  6. Woman its pretty self — into a waggish courage:
  7. Ready in gibes, quick-answer'd, saucy and
  8. As quarrelous as the weasel; nay, you must
  9. Forget that rarest treasure of your cheek,
  10. Exposing it — but, O, the harder heart!
  11. Alack, no remedy! — to the greedy touch
  12. Of common-kissing Titan, and forget
  13. Your laboursome and dainty trims, wherein
  14. You made great Juno angry.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Nay, be brief
  3. I see into thy end, and am almost
  4. A man already.
  1. PISANIO
  2. First, make yourself but like one.
  3. Fore-thinking this, I have already fit —
  4. 'Tis in my cloak-bag — doublet, hat, hose, all
  5. That answer to them: would you in their serving,
  6. And with what imitation you can borrow
  7. From youth of such a season, 'fore noble Lucius
  8. Present yourself, desire his service, tell him
  9. wherein you're happy — which you'll make him know,
  10. If that his head have ear in music — doubtless
  11. With joy he will embrace you, for he's honourable
  12. And doubling that, most holy. Your means abroad,
  13. You have me, rich; and I will never fail
  14. Beginning nor supplyment.
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Thou art all the comfort
  3. The gods will diet me with. Prithee, away:
  4. There's more to be consider'd; but we'll even
  5. All that good time will give us: this attempt
  6. I am soldier to, and will abide it with
  7. A prince's courage. Away, I prithee.
  1. PISANIO
  2. Well, madam, we must take a short farewell,
  3. Lest, being miss'd, I be suspected of
  4. Your carriage from the court. My noble mistress,
  5. Here is a box; I had it from the queen:
  6. What's in't is precious; if you are sick at sea,
  7. Or stomach-qualm'd at land, a dram of this
  8. Will drive away distemper. To some shade,
  9. And fit you to your manhood. May the gods
  10. Direct you to the best!
  1. IMOGEN
  2. Amen: I thank thee.
Exeunt, severally

  1. CAIUS LUCIUS
  2. Thanks, royal sir.

  14. The piece of tender air, thy virtuous daughter,
  15. Which we call 'mollis aer;' and 'mollis aer'
  16. We term it 'mulier:' which 'mulier' I divine
  17. Is this most constant wife; who, even now,
  18. Answering the letter of the oracle,
  19. Unknown to you, unsought, were clipp'd about
  20. With this most tender air.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. This hath some seeming.
  1. Soothsayer
  2. The lofty cedar, royal Cymbeline,
  3. Personates thee: and thy lopp'd branches point
  4. Thy two sons forth; who, by Belarius stol'n,
  5. For many years thought dead, are now revived,
  6. To the majestic cedar join'd, whose issue
  7. Promises Britain peace and plenty.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Well
  3. My peace we will begin. And, Caius Lucius,
  4. Although the victor, we submit to Caesar,
  5. And to the Roman empire; promising
  6. To pay our wonted tribute, from the which
  7. We were dissuaded by our wicked queen;
  8. Whom heavens, in justice, both on her and hers,
  9. Have laid most heavy hand.
  1. Soothsayer
  2. The fingers of the powers above do tune
  3. The harmony of this peace. The vision
  4. Which I made known to Lucius, ere the stroke
  5. Of this yet scarce-cold battle, at this instant
  6. Is full accomplish'd; for the Roman eagle,
  7. From south to west on wing soaring aloft,
  8. Lessen'd herself, and in the beams o' the sun
  9. So vanish'd: which foreshow'd our princely eagle,
  10. The imperial Caesar, should again unite
  11. His favour with the radiant Cymbeline,
  12. Which shines here in the west.
  1. CYMBELINE
  2. Laud we the gods;
  3. And let our crooked smokes climb to their nostrils
  4. From our blest altars. Publish we this peace
  5. To all our subjects. Set we forward: let
  6. A Roman and a British ensign wave
  7. Friendly together: so through Lud's-town march:
  8. And in the temple of great Jupiter
  9. Our peace we'll ratify; seal it with feasts.
  10. Set on there! Never was a war did cease,
  11. Ere bloody hands were wash'd, with such a peace.
