SCENE Partly at Venice, and partly at Belmont, the seat of PORTIA, on the Continent.

ACT I

SCENE I. Venice. A street.

Enter ANTONIO, SALARINO, and SALANIO
  1. ANTONIO
  2. In sooth, I know not why I am so sad:
  3. It wearies me; you say it wearies you;
  4. But how I caught it, found it, or came by it,
  5. What stuff 'tis made of, whereof it is born,
  6. I am to learn;
  7. And such a want-wit sadness makes of me,
  8. That I have much ado to know myself.
  1. SALARINO
  2. Your mind is tossing on the ocean;
  3. There, where your argosies with portly sail,
  4. Like signiors and rich burghers on the flood,
  5. Or, as it were, the pageants of the sea,
  6. Do overpeer the petty traffickers,
  7. That curtsy to them, do them reverence,
  8. As they fly by them with their woven wings.
  1. SALANIO
  2. Believe me, sir, had I such venture forth,
  3. The better part of my affections would
  4. Be with my hopes abroad. I should be still
  5. Plucking the grass, to know where sits the wind,
  6. Peering in maps for ports and piers and roads;
  7. And every object that might make me fear
  8. Misfortune to my ventures, out of doubt
  9. Would make me sad.
  1. SALARINO
  2. My wind cooling my broth
  3. Would blow me to an ague, when I thought
  4. What harm a wind too great at sea might do.
  5. I should not see the sandy hour-glass run,
  6. But I should think of shallows and of flats,
  7. And see my wealthy Andrew dock'd in sand,
  8. Vailing her high-top lower than her ribs
  9. To kiss her burial. Should I go to church
  10. And see the holy edifice of stone,
  11. And not bethink me straight of dangerous rocks,
  12. Which touching but my gentle vessel's side,
  13. Would scatter all her spices on the stream,
  14. Enrobe the roaring waters with my silks,
  15. And, in a word, but even now worth this,
  16. And now worth nothing? Shall I have the thought
  17. To think on this, and shall I lack the thought
  18. That such a thing bechanced would make me sad?
  19. But tell not me; I know, Antonio
  20. Is sad to think upon his merchandise.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Believe me, no: I thank my fortune for it,
  3. My ventures are not in one bottom trusted,
  4. Nor to one place; nor is my whole estate
  5. Upon the fortune of this present year:
  6. Therefore my merchandise makes me not sad.
  1. SALARINO
  2. Why, then you are in love.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Fie, fie!
  1. SALARINO
  2. Not in love neither? Then let us say you are sad,
  3. Because you are not merry: and 'twere as easy
  4. For you to laugh and leap and say you are merry,
  5. Because you are not sad. Now, by two-headed Janus,
  6. Nature hath framed strange fellows in her time:
  7. Some that will evermore peep through their eyes
  8. And laugh like parrots at a bag-piper,
  9. And other of such vinegar aspect
  10. That they'll not show their teeth in way of smile,
  11. Though Nestor swear the jest be laughable.
Enter BASSANIO, LORENZO, and GRATIANO
  1. SALANIO
  2. Here comes Bassanio, your most noble kinsman,
  3. Gratiano and Lorenzo. Fare ye well:
  4. We leave you now with better company.
  1. SALARINO
  2. I would have stay'd till I had made you merry,
  3. If worthier friends had not prevented me.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Your worth is very dear in my regard.
  3. I take it, your own business calls on you
  4. And you embrace the occasion to depart.
  1. SALARINO
  2. Good morrow, my good lords.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Good signiors both, when shall we laugh? say, when?
  3. You grow exceeding strange: must it be so?
  1. SALARINO
  2. We'll make our leisures to attend on yours.
Exeunt Salarino and Salanio
  1. LORENZO
  2. My Lord Bassanio, since you have found Antonio,
  3. We two will leave you: but at dinner-time,
  4. I pray you, have in mind where we must meet.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. I will not fail you.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. You look not well, Signior Antonio;
  3. You have too much respect upon the world:
  4. They lose it that do buy it with much care:
  5. Believe me, you are marvellously changed.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I hold the world but as the world, Gratiano;
  3. A stage where every man must play a part,
  4. And mine a sad one.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Let me play the fool:
  3. With mirth and laughter let old wrinkles come,
  4. And let my liver rather heat with wine
  5. Than my heart cool with mortifying groans.
  6. Why should a man, whose blood is warm within,
  7. Sit like his grandsire cut in alabaster?
  8. Sleep when he wakes and creep into the jaundice
  9. By being peevish? I tell thee what, Antonio —
  10. I love thee, and it is my love that speaks —
  11. There are a sort of men whose visages
  12. Do cream and mantle like a standing pond,
  13. And do a wilful stillness entertain,
  14. With purpose to be dress'd in an opinion
  15. Of wisdom, gravity, profound conceit,
  16. As who should say 'I am Sir Oracle,
  17. And when I ope my lips let no dog bark!'
  18. O my Antonio, I do know of these
  19. That therefore only are reputed wise
  20. For saying nothing; when, I am very sure,
  21. If they should speak, would almost damn those ears,
  22. Which, hearing them, would call their brothers fools.
  23. I'll tell thee more of this another time:
  24. But fish not, with this melancholy bait,
  25. For this fool gudgeon, this opinion.
  26. Come, good Lorenzo. Fare ye well awhile:
  27. I'll end my exhortation after dinner.
  1. LORENZO
  2. Well, we will leave you then till dinner-time:
  3. I must be one of these same dumb wise men,
  4. For Gratiano never lets me speak.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Well, keep me company but two years moe,
  3. Thou shalt not know the sound of thine own tongue.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Farewell: I'll grow a talker for this gear.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Thanks, i' faith, for silence is only commendable
  3. In a neat's tongue dried and a maid not vendible.
Exeunt GRATIANO and LORENZO
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Is that any thing now?
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Gratiano speaks an infinite deal of nothing, more
  3. than any man in all Venice. His reasons are as two
  4. grains of wheat hid in two bushels of chaff: you
  5. shall seek all day ere you find them, and when you
  6. have them, they are not worth the search.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Well, tell me now what lady is the same
  3. To whom you swore a secret pilgrimage,
  4. That you to-day promised to tell me of?
  1. BASSANIO
  2. 'Tis not unknown to you, Antonio,
  3. How much I have disabled mine estate,
  4. By something showing a more swelling port
  5. Than my faint means would grant continuance:
  6. Nor do I now make moan to be abridged
  7. From such a noble rate; but my chief care
  8. Is to come fairly off from the great debts
  9. Wherein my time something too prodigal
  10. Hath left me gaged. To you, Antonio,
  11. I owe the most, in money and in love,
  12. And from your love I have a warranty
  13. To unburden all my plots and purposes
  14. How to get clear of all the debts I owe.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I pray you, good Bassanio, let me know it;
  3. And if it stand, as you yourself still do,
  4. Within the eye of honour, be assured,
  5. My purse, my person, my extremest means,
  6. Lie all unlock'd to your occasions.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. In my school-days, when I had lost one shaft,
  3. I shot his fellow of the self-same flight
  4. The self-same way with more advised watch,
  5. To find the other forth, and by adventuring both
  6. I oft found both: I urge this childhood proof,
  7. Because what follows is pure innocence.
  8. I owe you much, and, like a wilful youth,
  9. That which I owe is lost; but if you please
  10. To shoot another arrow that self way
  11. Which you did shoot the first, I do not doubt,
  12. As I will watch the aim, or to find both
  13. Or bring your latter hazard back again
  14. And thankfully rest debtor for the first.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. You know me well, and herein spend but time
  3. To wind about my love with circumstance;
  4. And out of doubt you do me now more wrong
  5. In making question of my uttermost
  6. Than if you had made waste of all I have:
  7. Then do but say to me what I should do
  8. That in your knowledge may by me be done,
  9. And I am prest unto it: therefore, speak.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. In Belmont is a lady richly left;
  3. And she is fair, and, fairer than that word,
  4. Of wondrous virtues: sometimes from her eyes
  5. I did receive fair speechless messages:
  6. Her name is Portia, nothing undervalued
  7. To Cato's daughter, Brutus' Portia:
  8. Nor is the wide world ignorant of her worth,
  9. For the four winds blow in from every coast
  10. Renowned suitors, and her sunny locks
  11. Hang on her temples like a golden fleece;
  12. Which makes her seat of Belmont Colchos' strand,
  13. And many Jasons come in quest of her.
  14. O my Antonio, had I but the means
  15. To hold a rival place with one of them,
  16. I have a mind presages me such thrift,
  17. That I should questionless be fortunate!
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Thou know'st that all my fortunes are at sea;
  3. Neither have I money nor commodity
  4. To raise a present sum: therefore go forth;
  5. Try what my credit can in Venice do:
  6. That shall be rack'd, even to the uttermost,
  7. To furnish thee to Belmont, to fair Portia.
  8. Go, presently inquire, and so will I,
  9. Where money is, and I no question make
  10. To have it of my trust or for my sake.
Exeunt

SCENE II: Belmont. A room in PORTIA'S house.

Enter PORTIA and NERISSA
  1. PORTIA
  2. By my troth, Nerissa, my little body is aweary of
  3. this great world.
  1. NERISSA
  2. You would be, sweet madam, if your miseries were in
  3. the same abundance as your good fortunes are: and
  4. yet, for aught I see, they are as sick that surfeit
  5. with too much as they that starve with nothing. It
  6. is no mean happiness therefore, to be seated in the
  7. mean: superfluity comes sooner by white hairs, but
  8. competency lives longer.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Good sentences and well pronounced.
  1. NERISSA
  2. They would be better, if well followed.
  1. PORTIA
  2. If to do were as easy as to know what were good to
  3. do, chapels had been churches and poor men's
  4. cottages princes' palaces. It is a good divine that
  5. follows his own instructions: I can easier teach
  6. twenty what were good to be done, than be one of the
  7. twenty to follow mine own teaching. The brain may
  8. devise laws for the blood, but a hot temper leaps
  9. o'er a cold decree: such a hare is madness the
  10. youth, to skip o'er the meshes of good counsel the
  11. cripple. But this reasoning is not in the fashion to
  12. choose me a husband. O me, the word 'choose!' I may
  13. neither choose whom I would nor refuse whom I
  14. dislike; so is the will of a living daughter curbed
  15. by the will of a dead father. Is it not hard,
  16. Nerissa, that I cannot choose one nor refuse none?
  1. NERISSA
  2. Your father was ever virtuous; and holy men at their
  3. death have good inspirations: therefore the lottery,
  4. that he hath devised in these three chests of gold,
  5. silver and lead, whereof who chooses his meaning
  6. chooses you, will, no doubt, never be chosen by any
  7. rightly but one who shall rightly love. But what
  8. warmth is there in your affection towards any of
  9. these princely suitors that are already come?
  1. PORTIA
  2. I pray thee, over-name them; and as thou namest
  3. them, I will describe them; and, according to my
  4. description, level at my affection.
  1. NERISSA
  2. First, there is the Neapolitan prince.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Ay, that's a colt indeed, for he doth nothing but
  3. talk of his horse; and he makes it a great
  4. appropriation to his own good parts, that he can
  5. shoe him himself. I am much afeard my lady his
  6. mother played false with a smith.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Then there is the County Palatine.
  1. PORTIA
  2. He doth nothing but frown, as who should say 'If you
  3. will not have me, choose:' he hears merry tales and
  4. smiles not: I fear he will prove the weeping
  5. philosopher when he grows old, being so full of
  6. unmannerly sadness in his youth. I had rather be
  7. married to a death's-head with a bone in his mouth
  8. than to either of these. God defend me from these
  9. two!
  1. NERISSA
  2. How say you by the French lord, Monsieur Le Bon?
  1. PORTIA
  2. God made him, and therefore let him pass for a man.
  3. In truth, I know it is a sin to be a mocker: but,
  4. he! why, he hath a horse better than the
  5. Neapolitan's, a better bad habit of frowning than
  6. the Count Palatine; he is every man in no man; if a
  7. throstle sing, he falls straight a capering: he will
  8. fence with his own shadow: if I should marry him, I
  9. should marry twenty husbands. If he would despise me
  10. I would forgive him, for if he love me to madness, I
  11. shall never requite him.
  1. NERISSA
  2. What say you, then, to Falconbridge, the young baron
  3. of England?
  1. PORTIA
  2. You know I say nothing to him, for he understands
  3. not me, nor I him: he hath neither Latin, French,
  4. nor Italian, and you will come into the court and
  5. swear that I have a poor pennyworth in the English.
  6. He is a proper man's picture, but, alas, who can
  7. converse with a dumb-show? How oddly he is suited!
  8. I think he bought his doublet in Italy, his round
  9. hose in France, his bonnet in Germany and his
  10. behavior every where.
  1. NERISSA
  2. What think you of the Scottish lord, his neighbour?
  1. PORTIA
  2. That he hath a neighbourly charity in him, for he
  3. borrowed a box of the ear of the Englishman and
  4. swore he would pay him again when he was able: I
  5. think the Frenchman became his surety and sealed
  6. under for another.
  1. NERISSA
  2. How like you the young German, the Duke of Saxony's nephew?
  1. PORTIA
  2. Very vilely in the morning, when he is sober, and
  3. most vilely in the afternoon, when he is drunk: when
  4. he is best, he is a little worse than a man, and
  5. when he is worst, he is little better than a beast:
  6. and the worst fall that ever fell, I hope I shall
  7. make shift to go without him.
  1. NERISSA
  2. If he should offer to choose, and choose the right
  3. casket, you should refuse to perform your father's
  4. will, if you should refuse to accept him.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Therefore, for fear of the worst, I pray thee, set a
  3. deep glass of rhenish wine on the contrary casket,
  4. for if the devil be within and that temptation
  5. without, I know he will choose it. I will do any
  6. thing, Nerissa, ere I'll be married to a sponge.
  1. NERISSA
  2. You need not fear, lady, the having any of these
  3. lords: they have acquainted me with their
  4. determinations; which is, indeed, to return to their
  5. home and to trouble you with no more suit, unless
  6. you may be won by some other sort than your father's
  7. imposition depending on the caskets.
  1. PORTIA
  2. If I live to be as old as Sibylla, I will die as
  3. chaste as Diana, unless I be obtained by the manner
  4. of my father's will. I am glad this parcel of wooers
  5. are so reasonable, for there is not one among them
  6. but I dote on his very absence, and I pray God grant
  7. them a fair departure.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Do you not remember, lady, in your father's time, a
  3. Venetian, a scholar and a soldier, that came hither
  4. in company of the Marquis of Montferrat?
  1. PORTIA
  2. Yes, yes, it was Bassanio; as I think, he was so called.
  1. NERISSA
  2. True, madam: he, of all the men that ever my foolish
  3. eyes looked upon, was the best deserving a fair lady.
  1. PORTIA
  2. I remember him well, and I remember him worthy of
  3. thy praise.
  4. Enter a Serving-man
  5. How now! what news?
  1. Servant
  2. The four strangers seek for you, madam, to take
  3. their leave: and there is a forerunner come from a
  4. fifth, the Prince of Morocco, who brings word the
  5. prince his master will be here to-night.
  1. PORTIA
  2. If I could bid the fifth welcome with so good a
  3. heart as I can bid the other four farewell, I should
  4. be glad of his approach: if he have the condition
  5. of a saint and the complexion of a devil, I had
  6. rather he should shrive me than wive me. Come,
  7. Nerissa. Sirrah, go before.
  8. Whiles we shut the gates
  9. upon one wooer, another knocks at the door.
Exeunt

SCENE III. Venice. A public place.

Enter BASSANIO and SHYLOCK
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Three thousand ducats; well.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Ay, sir, for three months.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. For three months; well.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. For the which, as I told you, Antonio shall be bound.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Antonio shall become bound; well.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. May you stead me? will you pleasure me? shall I
  3. know your answer?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Three thousand ducats for three months and Antonio bound.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Your answer to that.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Antonio is a good man.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Have you heard any imputation to the contrary?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Oh, no, no, no, no: my meaning in saying he is a
  3. good man is to have you understand me that he is
  4. sufficient. Yet his means are in supposition: he
  5. hath an argosy bound to Tripolis, another to the
  6. Indies; I understand moreover, upon the Rialto, he
  7. hath a third at Mexico, a fourth for England, and
  8. other ventures he hath, squandered abroad. But ships
  9. are but boards, sailors but men: there be land-rats
  10. and water-rats, water-thieves and land-thieves, I
  11. mean pirates, and then there is the peril of waters,
  12. winds and rocks. The man is, notwithstanding,
  13. sufficient. Three thousand ducats; I think I may
  14. take his bond.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Be assured you may.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. I will be assured I may; and, that I may be assured,
  3. I will bethink me. May I speak with Antonio?
  1. BASSANIO
  2. If it please you to dine with us.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Yes, to smell pork; to eat of the habitation which
  3. your prophet the Nazarite conjured the devil into. I
  4. will buy with you, sell with you, talk with you,
  5. walk with you, and so following, but I will not eat
  6. with you, drink with you, nor pray with you. What
  7. news on the Rialto? Who is he comes here?
Enter ANTONIO
  1. BASSANIO
  2. This is Signior Antonio.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Aside How like a fawning publican he looks!
  3. I hate him for he is a Christian,
  4. But more for that in low simplicity
  5. He lends out money gratis and brings down
  6. The rate of usance here with us in Venice.
  7. If I can catch him once upon the hip,
  8. I will feed fat the ancient grudge I bear him.
  9. He hates our sacred nation, and he rails,
  10. Even there where merchants most do congregate,
  11. On me, my bargains and my well-won thrift,
  12. Which he calls interest. Cursed be my tribe,
  13. If I forgive him!
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Shylock, do you hear?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. I am debating of my present store,
  3. And, by the near guess of my memory,
  4. I cannot instantly raise up the gross
  5. Of full three thousand ducats. What of that?
  6. Tubal, a wealthy Hebrew of my tribe,
  7. Will furnish me. But soft! how many months
  8. Do you desire?
  9. To ANTONIO
  10. Rest you fair, good signior;
  11. Your worship was the last man in our mouths.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Shylock, although I neither lend nor borrow
  3. By taking nor by giving of excess,
  4. Yet, to supply the ripe wants of my friend,
  5. I'll break a custom. Is he yet possess'd
  6. How much ye would?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Ay, ay, three thousand ducats.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. And for three months.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. I had forgot; three months; you told me so.
  3. Well then, your bond; and let me see; but hear you;
  4. Methought you said you neither lend nor borrow
  5. Upon advantage.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I do never use it.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. When Jacob grazed his uncle Laban's sheep —
  3. This Jacob from our holy Abram was,
  4. As his wise mother wrought in his behalf,
  5. The third possessor; ay, he was the third —
  1. ANTONIO
  2. And what of him? did he take interest?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. No, not take interest, not, as you would say,
  3. Directly interest: mark what Jacob did.
  4. When Laban and himself were compromised
  5. That all the eanlings which were streak'd and pied
  6. Should fall as Jacob's hire, the ewes, being rank,
  7. In the end of autumn turned to the rams,
  8. And, when the work of generation was
  9. Between these woolly breeders in the act,
  10. The skilful shepherd peel'd me certain wands,
  11. And, in the doing of the deed of kind,
  12. He stuck them up before the fulsome ewes,
  13. Who then conceiving did in eaning time
  14. Fall parti-colour'd lambs, and those were Jacob's.
  15. This was a way to thrive, and he was blest:
  16. And thrift is blessing, if men steal it not.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. This was a venture, sir, that Jacob served for;
  3. A thing not in his power to bring to pass,
  4. But sway'd and fashion'd by the hand of heaven.
  5. Was this inserted to make interest good?
  6. Or is your gold and silver ewes and rams?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. I cannot tell; I make it breed as fast:
  3. But note me, signior.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Mark you this, Bassanio,
  3. The devil can cite Scripture for his purpose.
  4. An evil soul producing holy witness
  5. Is like a villain with a smiling cheek,
  6. A goodly apple rotten at the heart:
  7. O, what a goodly outside falsehood hath!
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Three thousand ducats; 'tis a good round sum.
  3. Three months from twelve; then, let me see; the rate —
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Well, Shylock, shall we be beholding to you?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. Signior Antonio, many a time and oft
  3. In the Rialto you have rated me
  4. About my moneys and my usances:
  5. Still have I borne it with a patient shrug,
  6. For sufferance is the badge of all our tribe.
  7. You call me misbeliever, cut-throat dog,
  8. And spit upon my Jewish gaberdine,
  9. And all for use of that which is mine own.
  10. Well then, it now appears you need my help:
  11. Go to, then; you come to me, and you say
  12. 'Shylock, we would have moneys:' you say so;
  13. You, that did void your rheum upon my beard
  14. And foot me as you spurn a stranger cur
  15. Over your threshold: moneys is your suit
  16. What should I say to you? Should I not say
  17. 'Hath a dog money? is it possible
  18. A cur can lend three thousand ducats?' Or
  19. Shall I bend low and in a bondman's key,
  20. With bated breath and whispering humbleness, Say this;
  21. 'Fair sir, you spit on me on Wednesday last;
  22. You spurn'd me such a day; another time

  4. the Goodwins, I think they call the place; a very
  5. dangerous flat and fatal, where the carcasses of many
  6. a tall ship lie buried, as they say, if my gossip
  7. Report be an honest woman of her word.
  1. SALANIO
  2. I would she were as lying a gossip in that as ever
  3. knapped ginger or made her neighbours believe she
  4. wept for the death of a third husband. But it is
  5. true, without any slips of prolixity or crossing the
  6. plain highway of talk, that the good Antonio, the
  7. honest Antonio — O that I had a title good enough
  8. to keep his name company! —
  1. SALARINO
  2. Come, the full stop.
  1. SALANIO
  2. Ha! what sayest thou? Why, the end is, he hath
  3. lost a ship.
  1. SALARINO
  2. I would it might prove the end of his losses.
  1. SALANIO
  2. Let me say 'amen' betimes, lest the devil cross my
  3. prayer, for here he comes in the likeness of a Jew.

  5. How now, Shylock! what news among the merchants?

  2. You know, none so well, none so well as you, of my
  3. daughter's flight.
  1. SALARINO
  2. That's certain: I, for my part, knew the tailor
  3. that made the wings she flew withal.
  1. SALANIO
  2. And Shylock, for his own part, knew the bird was
  3. fledged; and then it is the complexion of them all
  4. to leave the dam.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. She is damned for it.
  1. SALANIO
  2. That's certain, if the devil may be her judge.
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. My own flesh and blood to rebel!
  1. SALANIO
  2. Out upon it, old carrion! rebels it at these years?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. I say, my daughter is my flesh and blood.

  2. There is more difference between thy flesh and hers
  3. than between jet and ivory; more between your bloods
  4. than there is between red wine and rhenish. But
  5. tell us, do you hear whether Antonio have had any
  6. loss at sea or no?
  1. SHYLOCK
  2. There I have another bad match: a bankrupt, a

  6. call me usurer; let him look to his bond: he was

  1. SHYLOCK

  4. hindered me half a million; laughed at my losses,
  5. mocked at my gains, scorned my nation, thwarted my

  19. wrong a Jew, what should his sufferance be by

  21. teach me, I will execute, and it shall go hard but I

Enter BASSANIO, PORTIA, GRATIANO, NERISSA, and Attendants
  1. PORTIA
  2. I pray you, tarry: pause a day or two
  3. Before you hazard; for, in choosing wrong,
  4. I lose your company: therefore forbear awhile.
  5. There's something tells me, but it is not love,
  6. I would not lose you; and you know yourself,
  7. Hate counsels not in such a quality.
  8. But lest you should not understand me well —
  9. And yet a maiden hath no tongue but thought —
  10. I would detain you here some month or two
  11. Before you venture for me. I could teach you
  12. How to choose right, but I am then forsworn;
  13. So will I never be: so may you miss me;
  14. But if you do, you'll make me wish a sin,
  15. That I had been forsworn. Beshrew your eyes,
  16. They have o'erlook'd me and divided me;
  17. One half of me is yours, the other half yours,
  18. Mine own, I would say; but if mine, then yours,
  19. And so all yours. O, these naughty times
  20. Put bars between the owners and their rights!
  21. And so, though yours, not yours. Prove it so,
  22. Let fortune go to hell for it, not I.
  23. I speak too long; but 'tis to peize the time,

  3. I must away this night toward Padua,
  4. And it is meet I presently set forth.
  1. DUKE
  2. I am sorry that your leisure serves you not.
  3. Antonio, gratify this gentleman,
  4. For, in my mind, you are much bound to him.
Exeunt Duke and his train
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Most worthy gentleman, I and my friend
  3. Have by your wisdom been this day acquitted
  4. Of grievous penalties; in lieu whereof,
  5. Three thousand ducats, due unto the Jew,
  6. We freely cope your courteous pains withal.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. And stand indebted, over and above,
  3. In love and service to you evermore.
  1. PORTIA
  2. He is well paid that is well satisfied;
  3. And I, delivering you, am satisfied
  4. And therein do account myself well paid:
  5. My mind was never yet more mercenary.
  6. I pray you, know me when we meet again:
  7. I wish you well, and so I take my leave.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Dear sir, of force I must attempt you further:
  3. Take some remembrance of us, as a tribute,
  4. Not as a fee: grant me two things, I pray you,
  5. Not to deny me, and to pardon me.
  1. PORTIA
  2. You press me far, and therefore I will yield.
  3. To ANTONIO
  4. Give me your gloves, I'll wear them for your sake;
  5. To BASSANIO
  6. And, for your love, I'll take this ring from you:
  7. Do not draw back your hand; I'll take no more;
  8. And you in love shall not deny me this.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. This ring, good sir, alas, it is a trifle!
  3. I will not shame myself to give you this.
  1. PORTIA
  2. I will have nothing else but only this;
  3. And now methinks I have a mind to it.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. There's more depends on this than on the value.
  3. The dearest ring in Venice will I give you,
  4. And find it out by proclamation:
  5. Only for this, I pray you, pardon me.
  1. PORTIA
  2. I see, sir, you are liberal in offers
  3. You taught me first to beg; and now methinks
  4. You teach me how a beggar should be answer'd.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Good sir, this ring was given me by my wife;
  3. And when she put it on, she made me vow
  4. That I should neither sell nor give nor lose it.
  1. PORTIA
  2. That 'scuse serves many men to save their gifts.
  3. An if your wife be not a mad-woman,
  4. And know how well I have deserved the ring,
  5. She would not hold out enemy for ever,
  6. For giving it to me. Well, peace be with you!
Exeunt Portia and Nerissa
  1. ANTONIO
  2. My Lord Bassanio, let him have the ring:
  3. Let his deservings and my love withal
  4. Be valued against your wife's commandment.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Go, Gratiano, run and overtake him;
  3. Give him the ring, and bring him, if thou canst,
  4. Unto Antonio's house: away! make haste.
  5. Exit Gratiano
  6. Come, you and I will thither presently;
  7. And in the morning early will we both
  8. Fly toward Belmont: come, Antonio.

ACT V

SCENE I. Belmont. Avenue to PORTIA'S house.

Enter LORENZO and JESSICA
  1. LORENZO
  2. The moon shines bright: in such a night as this,
  3. When the sweet wind did gently kiss the trees
  4. And they did make no noise, in such a night
  5. Troilus methinks mounted the Troyan walls
  6. And sigh'd his soul toward the Grecian tents,
  7. Where Cressid lay that night.
  1. JESSICA
  2. In such a night
  3. Did Thisbe fearfully o'ertrip the dew
  4. And saw the lion's shadow ere himself
  5. And ran dismay'd away.
  1. LORENZO
  2. In such a night
  3. Stood Dido with a willow in her hand
  4. Upon the wild sea banks and waft her love
  5. To come again to Carthage.
  1. JESSICA
  2. In such a night
  3. Medea gather'd the enchanted herbs
  4. That did renew old AEson.
  1. LORENZO
  2. In such a night
  3. Did Jessica steal from the wealthy Jew
  4. And with an unthrift love did run from Venice
  5. As far as Belmont.
  1. JESSICA
  2. In such a night
  3. Did young Lorenzo swear he loved her well,
  4. Stealing her soul with many vows of faith
  5. And ne'er a true one.
  1. LORENZO
  2. In such a night
  3. Did pretty Jessica, like a little shrew,
  4. Slander her love, and he forgave it her.
  1. JESSICA
  2. I would out-night you, did no body come;
  3. But, hark, I hear the footing of a man.
Enter STEPHANO
  1. LORENZO
  2. Who comes so fast in silence of the night?
  1. STEPHANO
  2. A friend.
  1. LORENZO
  2. A friend! what friend? your name, I pray you, friend?
  1. STEPHANO
  2. Stephano is my name; and I bring word
  3. My mistress will before the break of day
  4. Be here at Belmont; she doth stray about
  5. By holy crosses, where she kneels and prays
  6. For happy wedlock hours.
  1. LORENZO
  2. Who comes with her?
  1. STEPHANO
  2. None but a holy hermit and her maid.
  3. I pray you, is my master yet return'd?
  1. LORENZO
  2. He is not, nor we have not heard from him.
  3. But go we in, I pray thee, Jessica,
  4. And ceremoniously let us prepare
  5. Some welcome for the mistress of the house.
Enter LAUNCELOT
  1. LAUNCELOT
  2. Sola, sola! wo ha, ho! sola, sola!
  1. LORENZO
  2. Who calls?
  1. LAUNCELOT
  2. Sola! did you see Master Lorenzo?
  3. Master Lorenzo, sola, sola!
  1. LORENZO
  2. Leave hollaing, man: here.
  1. LAUNCELOT
  2. Sola! where? where?
  1. LORENZO
  2. Here.
  1. LAUNCELOT
  2. Tell him there's a post come from my master, with
  3. his horn full of good news: my master will be here
  4. ere morning.
Exit
  1. LORENZO
  2. Sweet soul, let's in, and there expect their coming.
  3. And yet no matter: why should we go in?
  4. My friend Stephano, signify, I pray you,
  5. Within the house, your mistress is at hand;
  6. And bring your music forth into the air.
  7. Exit Stephano
  8. How sweet the moonlight sleeps upon this bank!
  9. Here will we sit and let the sounds of music
  10. Creep in our ears: soft stillness and the night
  11. Become the touches of sweet harmony.
  12. Sit, Jessica. Look how the floor of heaven
  13. Is thick inlaid with patines of bright gold:
  14. There's not the smallest orb which thou behold'st
  15. But in his motion like an angel sings,
  16. Still quiring to the young-eyed cherubins;
  17. Such harmony is in immortal souls;
  18. But whilst this muddy vesture of decay
  19. Doth grossly close it in, we cannot hear it.
  20. Enter Musicians
  21. Come, ho! and wake Diana with a hymn!
  22. With sweetest touches pierce your mistress' ear,
  23. And draw her home with music.
Music
  1. JESSICA
  2. I am never merry when I hear sweet music.
  1. LORENZO
  2. The reason is, your spirits are attentive:
  3. For do but note a wild and wanton herd,
  4. Or race of youthful and unhandled colts,
  5. Fetching mad bounds, bellowing and neighing loud,
  6. Which is the hot condition of their blood;
  7. If they but hear perchance a trumpet sound,
  8. Or any air of music touch their ears,
  9. You shall perceive them make a mutual stand,
  10. Their savage eyes turn'd to a modest gaze
  11. By the sweet power of music: therefore the poet
  12. Did feign that Orpheus drew trees, stones and floods;
  13. Since nought so stockish, hard and full of rage,
  14. But music for the time doth change his nature.
  15. The man that hath no music in himself,
  16. Nor is not moved with concord of sweet sounds,
  17. Is fit for treasons, stratagems and spoils;
  18. The motions of his spirit are dull as night
  19. And his affections dark as Erebus:
  20. Let no such man be trusted. Mark the music.
Enter PORTIA and NERISSA
  1. PORTIA
  2. That light we see is burning in my hall.
  3. How far that little candle throws his beams!
  4. So shines a good deed in a naughty world.
  1. NERISSA
  2. When the moon shone, we did not see the candle.
  1. PORTIA
  2. So doth the greater glory dim the less:
  3. A substitute shines brightly as a king
  4. Unto the king be by, and then his state
  5. Empties itself, as doth an inland brook
  6. Into the main of waters. Music! hark!
  1. NERISSA
  2. It is your music, madam, of the house.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Nothing is good, I see, without respect:
  3. Methinks it sounds much sweeter than by day.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Silence bestows that virtue on it, madam.
  1. PORTIA
  2. The crow doth sing as sweetly as the lark,
  3. When neither is attended, and I think
  4. The nightingale, if she should sing by day,
  5. When every goose is cackling, would be thought
  6. No better a musician than the wren.
  7. How many things by season season'd are
  8. To their right praise and true perfection!
  9. Peace, ho! the moon sleeps with Endymion
  10. And would not be awaked.
Music ceases
  1. LORENZO
  2. That is the voice,
  3. Or I am much deceived, of Portia.
  1. PORTIA
  2. He knows me as the blind man knows the cuckoo,
  3. By the bad voice.
  1. LORENZO
  2. Dear lady, welcome home.
  1. PORTIA
  2. We have been praying for our husbands' healths,
  3. Which speed, we hope, the better for our words.
  4. Are they return'd?
  1. LORENZO
  2. Madam, they are not yet;
  3. But there is come a messenger before,
  4. To signify their coming.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Go in, Nerissa;
  3. Give order to my servants that they take
  4. No note at all of our being absent hence;
  5. Nor you, Lorenzo; Jessica, nor you.
A tucket sounds
  1. LORENZO
  2. Your husband is at hand; I hear his trumpet:
  3. We are no tell-tales, madam; fear you not.
  1. PORTIA
  2. This night methinks is but the daylight sick;
  3. It looks a little paler: 'tis a day,
  4. Such as the day is when the sun is hid.
Enter BASSANIO, ANTONIO, GRATIANO, and their followers
  1. BASSANIO
  2. We should hold day with the Antipodes,
  3. If you would walk in absence of the sun.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Let me give light, but let me not be light;
  3. For a light wife doth make a heavy husband,
  4. And never be Bassanio so for me:
  5. But God sort all! You are welcome home, my lord.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. I thank you, madam. Give welcome to my friend.
  3. This is the man, this is Antonio,
  4. To whom I am so infinitely bound.
  1. PORTIA
  2. You should in all sense be much bound to him.
  3. For, as I hear, he was much bound for you.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. No more than I am well acquitted of.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Sir, you are very welcome to our house:
  3. It must appear in other ways than words,
  4. Therefore I scant this breathing courtesy.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. To NERISSA By yonder moon I swear you do me wrong;
  3. In faith, I gave it to the judge's clerk:
  4. Would he were gelt that had it, for my part,
  5. Since you do take it, love, so much at heart.
  1. PORTIA
  2. A quarrel, ho, already! what's the matter?
  1. GRATIANO
  2. About a hoop of gold, a paltry ring
  3. That she did give me, whose posy was
  4. For all the world like cutler's poetry
  5. Upon a knife, 'Love me, and leave me not.'
  1. NERISSA
  2. What talk you of the posy or the value?
  3. You swore to me, when I did give it you,
  4. That you would wear it till your hour of death
  5. And that it should lie with you in your grave:
  6. Though not for me, yet for your vehement oaths,
  7. You should have been respective and have kept it.
  8. Gave it a judge's clerk! no, God's my judge,
  9. The clerk will ne'er wear hair on's face that had it.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. He will, an if he live to be a man.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Ay, if a woman live to be a man.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Now, by this hand, I gave it to a youth,
  3. A kind of boy, a little scrubbed boy,
  4. No higher than thyself; the judge's clerk,
  5. A prating boy, that begg'd it as a fee:
  6. I could not for my heart deny it him.
  1. PORTIA
  2. You were to blame, I must be plain with you,
  3. To part so slightly with your wife's first gift:
  4. A thing stuck on with oaths upon your finger
  5. And so riveted with faith unto your flesh.
  6. I gave my love a ring and made him swear
  7. Never to part with it; and here he stands;
  8. I dare be sworn for him he would not leave it
  9. Nor pluck it from his finger, for the wealth
  10. That the world masters. Now, in faith, Gratiano,
  11. You give your wife too unkind a cause of grief:
  12. An 'twere to me, I should be mad at it.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Aside Why, I were best to cut my left hand off
  3. And swear I lost the ring defending it.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. My Lord Bassanio gave his ring away
  3. Unto the judge that begg'd it and indeed
  4. Deserved it too; and then the boy, his clerk,
  5. That took some pains in writing, he begg'd mine;
  6. And neither man nor master would take aught
  7. But the two rings.
  1. PORTIA
  2. What ring gave you my lord?
  3. Not that, I hope, which you received of me.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. If I could add a lie unto a fault,
  3. I would deny it; but you see my finger
  4. Hath not the ring upon it; it is gone.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Even so void is your false heart of truth.
  3. By heaven, I will ne'er come in your bed
  4. Until I see the ring.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Nor I in yours
  3. Till I again see mine.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Sweet Portia,
  3. If you did know to whom I gave the ring,
  4. If you did know for whom I gave the ring
  5. And would conceive for what I gave the ring
  6. And how unwillingly I left the ring,
  7. When nought would be accepted but the ring,
  8. You would abate the strength of your displeasure.
  1. PORTIA
  2. If you had known the virtue of the ring,
  3. Or half her worthiness that gave the ring,
  4. Or your own honour to contain the ring,
  5. You would not then have parted with the ring.
  6. What man is there so much unreasonable,
  7. If you had pleased to have defended it
  8. With any terms of zeal, wanted the modesty
  9. To urge the thing held as a ceremony?
  10. Nerissa teaches me what to believe:
  11. I'll die for't but some woman had the ring.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. No, by my honour, madam, by my soul,
  3. No woman had it, but a civil doctor,
  4. Which did refuse three thousand ducats of me
  5. And begg'd the ring; the which I did deny him
  6. And suffer'd him to go displeased away;
  7. Even he that did uphold the very life
  8. Of my dear friend. What should I say, sweet lady?
  9. I was enforced to send it after him;
  10. I was beset with shame and courtesy;
  11. My honour would not let ingratitude
  12. So much besmear it. Pardon me, good lady;
  13. For, by these blessed candles of the night,
  14. Had you been there, I think you would have begg'd
  15. The ring of me to give the worthy doctor.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Let not that doctor e'er come near my house:
  3. Since he hath got the jewel that I loved,
  4. And that which you did swear to keep for me,
  5. I will become as liberal as you;
  6. I'll not deny him any thing I have,
  7. No, not my body nor my husband's bed:
  8. Know him I shall, I am well sure of it:
  9. Lie not a night from home; watch me like Argus:
  10. If you do not, if I be left alone,
  11. Now, by mine honour, which is yet mine own,
  12. I'll have that doctor for my bedfellow.
  1. NERISSA
  2. And I his clerk; therefore be well advised
  3. How you do leave me to mine own protection.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Well, do you so; let not me take him, then;
  3. For if I do, I'll mar the young clerk's pen.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I am the unhappy subject of these quarrels.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Sir, grieve not you; you are welcome notwithstanding.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Portia, forgive me this enforced wrong;
  3. And, in the hearing of these many friends,
  4. I swear to thee, even by thine own fair eyes,
  5. Wherein I see myself —
  1. PORTIA
  2. Mark you but that!
  3. In both my eyes he doubly sees himself;
  4. In each eye, one: swear by your double self,
  5. And there's an oath of credit.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Nay, but hear me:
  3. Pardon this fault, and by my soul I swear
  4. I never more will break an oath with thee.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I once did lend my body for his wealth;
  3. Which, but for him that had your husband's ring,
  4. Had quite miscarried: I dare be bound again,
  5. My soul upon the forfeit, that your lord
  6. Will never more break faith advisedly.
  1. PORTIA
  2. Then you shall be his surety. Give him this
  3. And bid him keep it better than the other.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Here, Lord Bassanio; swear to keep this ring.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. By heaven, it is the same I gave the doctor!
  1. PORTIA
  2. I had it of him: pardon me, Bassanio;
  3. For, by this ring, the doctor lay with me.
  1. NERISSA
  2. And pardon me, my gentle Gratiano;
  3. For that same scrubbed boy, the doctor's clerk,
  4. In lieu of this last night did lie with me.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Why, this is like the mending of highways
  3. In summer, where the ways are fair enough:
  4. What, are we cuckolds ere we have deserved it?
  1. PORTIA
  2. Speak not so grossly. You are all amazed:
  3. Here is a letter; read it at your leisure;
  4. It comes from Padua, from Bellario:
  5. There you shall find that Portia was the doctor,
  6. Nerissa there her clerk: Lorenzo here
  7. Shall witness I set forth as soon as you
  8. And even but now return'd; I have not yet
  9. Enter'd my house. Antonio, you are welcome;
  10. And I have better news in store for you
  11. Than you expect: unseal this letter soon;
  12. There you shall find three of your argosies
  13. Are richly come to harbour suddenly:
  14. You shall not know by what strange accident
  15. I chanced on this letter.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. I am dumb.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Were you the doctor and I knew you not?
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Were you the clerk that is to make me cuckold?
  1. NERISSA
  2. Ay, but the clerk that never means to do it,
  3. Unless he live until he be a man.
  1. BASSANIO
  2. Sweet doctor, you shall be my bed-fellow:
  3. When I am absent, then lie with my wife.
  1. ANTONIO
  2. Sweet lady, you have given me life and living;
  3. For here I read for certain that my ships
  4. Are safely come to road.
  1. PORTIA
  2. How now, Lorenzo!
  3. My clerk hath some good comforts too for you.
  1. NERISSA
  2. Ay, and I'll give them him without a fee.
  3. There do I give to you and Jessica,
  4. From the rich Jew, a special deed of gift,
  5. After his death, of all he dies possess'd of.
  1. LORENZO
  2. Fair ladies, you drop manna in the way
  3. Of starved people.
  1. PORTIA
  2. It is almost morning,
  3. And yet I am sure you are not satisfied
  4. Of these events at full. Let us go in;
  5. And charge us there upon inter'gatories,
  6. And we will answer all things faithfully.
  1. GRATIANO
  2. Let it be so: the first inter'gatory
  3. That my Nerissa shall be sworn on is,
  4. Whether till the next night she had rather stay,
  5. Or go to bed now, being two hours to day:
  6. But were the day come, I should wish it dark,
  7. That I were couching with the doctor's clerk.
  8. Well, while I live I'll fear no other thing
  9. So sore as keeping safe Nerissa's ring.
Exeunt
