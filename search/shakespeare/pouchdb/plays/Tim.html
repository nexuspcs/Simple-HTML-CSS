ACT I

SCENE I. Athens. A hall in Timon's house.

Enter Poet, Painter, Jeweller, Merchant, and others, at several doors
  1. Poet
  2. Good day, sir.
  1. Painter
  2. I am glad you're well.
  1. Poet
  2. I have not seen you long: how goes the world?
  1. Painter
  2. It wears, sir, as it grows.
  1. Poet
  2. Ay, that's well known:
  3. But what particular rarity? what strange,
  4. Which manifold record not matches? See,
  5. Magic of bounty! all these spirits thy power
  6. Hath conjured to attend. I know the merchant.
  1. Painter
  2. I know them both; th' other's a jeweller.
  1. Merchant
  2. O, 'tis a worthy lord.
  1. Jeweller
  2. Nay, that's most fix'd.
  1. Merchant
  2. A most incomparable man, breathed, as it were,
  3. To an untirable and continuate goodness:
  4. He passes.
  1. Jeweller:
  2. I have a jewel here —
  1. Merchant
  2. O, pray, let's see't: for the Lord Timon, sir?
  1. Jeweller:
  2. If he will touch the estimate: but, for that —
  1. Poet
  2. Reciting to himself 'When we for recompense have
  3. praised the vile,
  4. It stains the glory in that happy verse
  5. Which aptly sings the good.'
  1. Merchant
  2. 'Tis a good form.
Looking at the jewel
  1. Jeweller
  2. And rich: here is a water, look ye.
  1. Painter
  2. You are rapt, sir, in some work, some dedication
  3. To the great lord.
  1. Poet
  2. A thing slipp'd idly from me.
  3. Our poesy is as a gum, which oozes
  4. From whence 'tis nourish'd: the fire i' the flint
  5. Shows not till it be struck; our gentle flame
  6. Provokes itself and like the current flies
  7. Each bound it chafes. What have you there?
  1. Painter
  2. A picture, sir. When comes your book forth?
  1. Poet
  2. Upon the heels of my presentment, sir.
  3. Let's see your piece.
  1. Painter
  2. 'Tis a good piece.
  1. Poet
  2. So 'tis: this comes off well and excellent.
  1. Painter
  2. Indifferent.
  1. Poet
  2. Admirable: how this grace
  3. Speaks his own standing! what a mental power
  4. This eye shoots forth! how big imagination
  5. Moves in this lip! to the dumbness of the gesture
  6. One might interpret.
  1. Painter
  2. It is a pretty mocking of the life.
  3. Here is a touch; is't good?
  1. Poet
  2. I will say of it,
  3. It tutors nature: artificial strife
  4. Lives in these touches, livelier than life.
Enter certain Senators, and pass over
  1. Painter
  2. How this lord is follow'd!
  1. Poet
  2. The senators of Athens: happy man!
  1. Painter
  2. Look, more!
  1. Poet
  2. You see this confluence, this great flood
  3. of visitors.
  4. I have, in this rough work, shaped out a man,
  5. Whom this beneath world doth embrace and hug
  6. With amplest entertainment: my free drift
  7. Halts not particularly, but moves itself
  8. In a wide sea of wax: no levell'd malice
  9. Infects one comma in the course I hold;
  10. But flies an eagle flight, bold and forth on,
  11. Leaving no tract behind.
  1. Painter
  2. How shall I understand you?
  1. Poet
  2. I will unbolt to you.
  3. You see how all conditions, how all minds,
  4. As well of glib and slippery creatures as
  5. Of grave and austere quality, tender down
  6. Their services to Lord Timon: his large fortune
  7. Upon his good and gracious nature hanging
  8. Subdues and properties to his love and tendance
  9. All sorts of hearts; yea, from the glass-faced flatterer
  10. To Apemantus, that few things loves better
  11. Than to abhor himself: even he drops down
  12. The knee before him, and returns in peace
  13. Most rich in Timon's nod.
  1. Painter
  2. I saw them speak together.
  1. Poet
  2. Sir, I have upon a high and pleasant hill
  3. Feign'd Fortune to be throned: the base o' the mount
  4. Is rank'd with all deserts, all kind of natures,
  5. That labour on the bosom of this sphere
  6. To propagate their states: amongst them all,
  7. Whose eyes are on this sovereign lady fix'd,
  8. One do I personate of Lord Timon's frame,
  9. Whom Fortune with her ivory hand wafts to her;
  10. Whose present grace to present slaves and servants
  11. Translates his rivals.
  1. Painter
  2. 'Tis conceived to scope.
  3. This throne, this Fortune, and this hill, methinks,
  4. With one man beckon'd from the rest below,
  5. Bowing his head against the sleepy mount
  6. To climb his happiness, would be well express'd
  7. In our condition.
  1. Poet
  2. Nay, sir, but hear me on.
  3. All those which were his fellows but of late,
  4. Some better than his value, on the moment
  5. Follow his strides, his lobbies fill with tendance,
  6. Rain sacrificial whisperings in his ear,
  7. Make sacred even his stirrup, and through him
  8. Drink the free air.
  1. Painter
  2. Ay, marry, what of these?
  1. Poet
  2. When Fortune in her shift and change of mood
  3. Spurns down her late beloved, all his dependants
  4. Which labour'd after him to the mountain's top
  5. Even on their knees and hands, let him slip down,
  6. Not one accompanying his declining foot.
  1. Painter
  2. 'Tis common:
  3. A thousand moral paintings I can show
  4. That shall demonstrate these quick blows of Fortune's
  5. More pregnantly than words. Yet you do well
  6. To show Lord Timon that mean eyes have seen
  7. The foot above the head.
Trumpets sound. Enter TIMON, addressing himself courteously to every suitor; a Messenger from VENTIDIUS talking with him; LUCILIUS and other servants following
  1. TIMON
  2. Imprison'd is he, say you?
  1. Messenger
  2. Ay, my good lord: five talents is his debt,
  3. His means most short, his creditors most strait:
  4. Your honourable letter he desires
  5. To those have shut him up; which failing,
  6. Periods his comfort.
  1. TIMON
  2. Noble Ventidius! Well;
  3. I am not of that feather to shake off
  4. My friend when he must need me. I do know him
  5. A gentleman that well deserves a help:
  6. Which he shall have: I'll pay the debt,
  7. and free him.
  1. Messenger
  2. Your lordship ever binds him.
  1. TIMON
  2. Commend me to him: I will send his ransom;
  3. And being enfranchised, bid him come to me.
  4. 'Tis not enough to help the feeble up,
  5. But to support him after. Fare you well.
  1. Messenger
  2. All happiness to your honour!
Exit
Enter an old Athenian
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Lord Timon, hear me speak.
  1. TIMON
  2. Freely, good father.
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Thou hast a servant named Lucilius.
  1. TIMON
  2. I have so: what of him?
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Most noble Timon, call the man before thee.
  1. TIMON
  2. Attends he here, or no? Lucilius!
  1. LUCILIUS
  2. Here, at your lordship's service.
  1. Old Athenian
  2. This fellow here, Lord Timon, this thy creature,
  3. By night frequents my house. I am a man
  4. That from my first have been inclined to thrift;
  5. And my estate deserves an heir more raised
  6. Than one which holds a trencher.
  1. TIMON
  2. Well; what further?
  1. Old Athenian
  2. One only daughter have I, no kin else,
  3. On whom I may confer what I have got:
  4. The maid is fair, o' the youngest for a bride,
  5. And I have bred her at my dearest cost
  6. In qualities of the best. This man of thine
  7. Attempts her love: I prithee, noble lord,
  8. Join with me to forbid him her resort;
  9. Myself have spoke in vain.
  1. TIMON
  2. The man is honest.
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Therefore he will be, Timon:
  3. His honesty rewards him in itself;
  4. It must not bear my daughter.
  1. TIMON
  2. Does she love him?
  1. Old Athenian
  2. She is young and apt:
  3. Our own precedent passions do instruct us
  4. What levity's in youth.
  1. TIMON
  2. To LUCILIUS Love you the maid?
  1. LUCILIUS
  2. Ay, my good lord, and she accepts of it.
  1. Old Athenian
  2. If in her marriage my consent be missing,
  3. I call the gods to witness, I will choose
  4. Mine heir from forth the beggars of the world,
  5. And dispossess her all.
  1. TIMON
  2. How shall she be endow'd,
  3. if she be mated with an equal husband?
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Three talents on the present; in future, all.
  1. TIMON
  2. This gentleman of mine hath served me long:
  3. To build his fortune I will strain a little,
  4. For 'tis a bond in men. Give him thy daughter:
  5. What you bestow, in him I'll counterpoise,
  6. And make him weigh with her.
  1. Old Athenian
  2. Most noble lord,
  3. Pawn me to this your honour, she is his.
  1. TIMON
  2. My hand to thee; mine honour on my promise.
  1. LUCILIUS
  2. Humbly I thank your lordship: never may
  3. The state or fortune fall into my keeping,
  4. Which is not owed to you!
Exeunt LUCILIUS and Old Athenian
  1. Poet
  2. Vouchsafe my labour, and long live your lordship!
  1. TIMON
  2. I thank you; you shall hear from me anon:
  3. Go not away. What have you there, my friend?
  1. Painter
  2. A piece of painting, which I do beseech
  3. Your lordship to accept.
  1. TIMON
  2. Painting is welcome.
  3. The painting is almost the natural man;
  4. or since dishonour traffics with man's nature,
  5. He is but outside: these pencill'd figures are
  6. Even such as they give out. I like your work;
  7. And you shall find I like it: wait attendance
  8. Till you hear further from me.
  1. Painter
  2. The gods preserve ye!
  1. TIMON
  2. Well fare you, gentleman: give me your hand;
  3. We must needs dine together. Sir, your jewel
  4. Hath suffer'd under praise.
  1. Jeweller
  2. What, my lord! dispraise?
  1. TIMON
  2. A more satiety of commendations.
  3. If I should pay you for't as 'tis extoll'd,
  4. It would unclew me quite.
  1. Jeweller
  2. My lord, 'tis rated
  3. As those which sell would give: but you well know,
  4. Things of like value differing in the owners
  5. Are prized by their masters: believe't, dear lord,
  6. You mend the jewel by the wearing it.
  1. TIMON
  2. Well mock'd.
  1. Merchant
  2. No, my good lord; he speaks the common tongue,
  3. Which all men speak with him.
  1. TIMON
  2. Look, who comes here: will you be chid?
Enter APEMANTUS
  1. Jeweller
  2. We'll bear, with your lordship.
  1. Merchant
  2. He'll spare none.
  1. TIMON
  2. Good morrow to thee, gentle Apemantus!
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Till I be gentle, stay thou for thy good morrow;
  3. When thou art Timon's dog, and these knaves honest.
  1. TIMON
  2. Why dost thou call them knaves? thou know'st them not.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Are they not Athenians?
  1. TIMON
  2. Yes.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Then I repent not.
  1. Jeweller
  2. You know me, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Thou know'st I do: I call'd thee by thy name.
  1. TIMON
  2. Thou art proud, Apemantus.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Of nothing so much as that I am not like Timon.
  1. TIMON
  2. Whither art going?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. To knock out an honest Athenian's brains.
  1. TIMON
  2. That's a deed thou'lt die for.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Right, if doing nothing be death by the law.
  1. TIMON
  2. How likest thou this picture, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. The best, for the innocence.
  1. TIMON
  2. Wrought he not well that painted it?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. He wrought better that made the painter; and yet
  3. he's but a filthy piece of work.
  1. Painter
  2. You're a dog.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Thy mother's of my generation: what's she, if I be a dog?
  1. TIMON
  2. Wilt dine with me, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. No; I eat not lords.
  1. TIMON
  2. An thou shouldst, thou 'ldst anger ladies.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. O, they eat lords; so they come by great bellies.
  1. TIMON
  2. That's a lascivious apprehension.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. So thou apprehendest it: take it for thy labour.
  1. TIMON
  2. How dost thou like this jewel, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Not so well as plain-dealing, which will not cost a
  3. man a doit.
  1. TIMON
  2. What dost thou think 'tis worth?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Not worth my thinking. How now, poet!
  1. Poet
  2. How now, philosopher!
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Thou liest.
  1. Poet
  2. Art not one?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Yes.
  1. Poet
  2. Then I lie not.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Art not a poet?
  1. Poet
  2. Yes.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Then thou liest: look in thy last work, where thou
  3. hast feigned him a worthy fellow.
  1. Poet
  2. That's not feigned; he is so.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Yes, he is worthy of thee, and to pay thee for thy
  3. labour: he that loves to be flattered is worthy o'
  4. the flatterer. Heavens, that I were a lord!
  1. TIMON
  2. What wouldst do then, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. E'en as Apemantus does now; hate a lord with my heart.
  1. TIMON
  2. What, thyself?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Ay.
  1. TIMON
  2. Wherefore?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. That I had no angry wit to be a lord.
  3. Art not thou a merchant?
  1. Merchant
  2. Ay, Apemantus.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Traffic confound thee, if the gods will not!
  1. Merchant
  2. If traffic do it, the gods do it.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Traffic's thy god; and thy god confound thee!
Trumpet sounds. Enter a Messenger
  1. TIMON
  2. What trumpet's that?
  1. Messenger
  2. 'Tis Alcibiades, and some twenty horse,
  3. All of companionship.
  1. TIMON
  2. Pray, entertain them; give them guide to us.
  3. Exeunt some Attendants
  4. You must needs dine with me: go not you hence
  5. Till I have thank'd you: when dinner's done,
  6. Show me this piece. I am joyful of your sights.
  7. Enter ALCIBIADES, with the rest
  8. Most welcome, sir!
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. So, so, there!
  3. Aches contract and starve your supple joints!
  4. That there should be small love 'mongst these
  5. sweet knaves,
  6. And all this courtesy! The strain of man's bred out
  7. Into baboon and monkey.
  1. ALCIBIADES
  2. Sir, you have saved my longing, and I feed
  3. Most hungerly on your sight.
  1. TIMON
  2. Right welcome, sir!
  3. Ere we depart, we'll share a bounteous time
  4. In different pleasures. Pray you, let us in.
Exeunt all except APEMANTUS
Enter two Lords
  1. First Lord
  2. What time o' day is't, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Time to be honest.
  1. First Lord
  2. That time serves still.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. The more accursed thou, that still omitt'st it.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Thou art going to Lord Timon's feast?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Ay, to see meat fill knaves and wine heat fools.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Fare thee well, fare thee well.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Thou art a fool to bid me farewell twice.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Why, Apemantus?
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Shouldst have kept one to thyself, for I mean to
  3. give thee none.
  1. First Lord
  2. Hang thyself!
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. No, I will do nothing at thy bidding: make thy

  1. Second Lord
  2. Away, unpeaceable dog, or I'll spurn thee hence!
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. I will fly, like a dog, the heels o' the ass.

  1. First Lord
  2. He's opposite to humanity. Come, shall we in,
  3. And taste Lord Timon's bounty? he outgoes
  4. The very heart of kindness.
  1. Second Lord
  2. He pours it out; Plutus, the god of gold,

  1. First Lord

SCENE II. A banqueting-room in Timon's house.

Hautboys playing loud music. A great banquet served in; FLAVIUS and others attending; then enter TIMON, ALCIBIADES, Lords, Senators, and VENTIDIUS. Then comes, dropping, after all, APEMANTUS, discontentedly, like himself
  1. VENTIDIUS
  2. Most honour'd Timon,
  3. It hath pleased the gods to remember my father's age,
  4. And call him to long peace.
  5. He is gone happy, and has left me rich:
  6. Then, as in grateful virtue I am bound
  7. To your free heart, I do return those talents,
  8. Doubled with thanks and service, from whose help
  9. I derived liberty.
  1. TIMON
  2. O, by no means,
  3. Honest Ventidius; you mistake my love:
  4. I gave it freely ever; and there's none
  5. Can truly say he gives, if he receives:
  6. If our betters play at that game, we must not dare
  7. To imitate them; faults that are rich are fair.
  1. VENTIDIUS
  2. A noble spirit!
  1. TIMON
  2. Nay, my lords,
  3. They all stand ceremoniously looking on TIMON
  4. Ceremony was but devised at first
  5. To set a gloss on faint deeds, hollow welcomes,
  6. Recanting goodness, sorry ere 'tis shown;
  7. But where there is true friendship, there needs none.
  8. Pray, sit; more welcome are ye to my fortunes
  9. Than my fortunes to me.
They sit
  1. First Lord
  2. My lord, we always have confess'd it.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Ho, ho, confess'd it! hang'd it, have you not?
  1. TIMON
  2. O, Apemantus, you are welcome.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. No;
  3. You shall not make me welcome:
  4. I come to have thee thrust me out of doors.
  1. TIMON
  2. Fie, thou'rt a churl; ye've got a humour there
  3. Does not become a man: 'tis much to blame.
  4. They say, my lords, 'ira furor brevis est;' but yond
  5. man is ever angry. Go, let him have a table by
  6. himself, for he does neither affect company, nor is
  7. he fit for't, indeed.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Let me stay at thine apperil, Timon: I come to
  3. observe; I give thee warning on't.
  1. TIMON
  2. I take no heed of thee; thou'rt an Athenian,
  3. therefore welcome: I myself would have no power;
  4. prithee, let my meat make thee silent.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. I scorn thy meat; 'twould choke me, for I should
  3. ne'er flatter thee. O you gods, what a number of
  4. men eat Timon, and he sees 'em not! It grieves me
  5. to see so many dip their meat in one man's blood;
  6. and all the madness is, he cheers them up too.
  7. I wonder men dare trust themselves with men:
  8. Methinks they should invite them without knives;
  9. Good for their meat, and safer for their lives.
  10. There's much example for't; the fellow that sits
  11. next him now, parts bread with him, pledges the
  12. breath of him in a divided draught, is the readiest
  13. man to kill him: 't has been proved. If I were a
  14. huge man, I should fear to drink at meals;
  15. Lest they should spy my windpipe's dangerous notes:
  16. Great men should drink with harness on their throats.
  1. TIMON
  2. My lord, in heart; and let the health go round.
  1. Second Lord
  2. Let it flow this way, my good lord.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Flow this way! A brave fellow! he keeps his tides
  3. well. Those healths will make thee and thy state
  4. look ill, Timon. Here's that which is too weak to
  5. be a sinner, honest water, which ne'er left man i' the mire:
  6. This and my food are equals; there's no odds:
  7. Feasts are too proud to give thanks to the gods.
  8. Apemantus' grace.
  9. Immortal gods, I crave no pelf;
  10. I pray for no man but myself:
  11. Grant I may never prove so fond,
  12. To trust man on his oath or bond;
  13. Or a harlot, for her weeping;
  14. Or a dog, that seems a-sleeping:
  15. Or a keeper with my freedom;
  16. Or my friends, if I should need 'em.
  17. Amen. So fall to't:
  18. Rich men sin, and I eat root.
  19. Eats and drinks
  20. Much good dich thy good heart, Apemantus!
  1. TIMON
  2. Captain Alcibiades, your heart's in the field now.
  1. ALCIBIADES
  2. My heart is ever at your service, my lord.
  1. TIMON
  2. You had rather be at a breakfast of enemies than a
  3. dinner of friends.
  1. ALCIBIADES
  2. So the were bleeding-new, my lord, there's no meat
  3. like 'em: I could wish my best friend at such a feast.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Would all those fatterers were thine enemies then,
  3. that then thou mightst kill 'em and bid me to 'em!
  1. First Lord
  2. Might we but have that happiness, my lord, that you
  3. would once use our hearts, whereby we might express
  4. some part of our zeals, we should think ourselves
  5. for ever perfect.
  1. TIMON
  2. O, no doubt, my good friends, but the gods
  3. themselves have provided that I shall have much help
  4. from you: how had you been my friends else? why
  5. have you that charitable title from thousands, did
  6. not you chiefly belong to my heart? I have told
  7. more of you to myself than you can with modesty
  8. speak in your own behalf; and thus far I confirm
  9. you. O you gods, think I, what need we have any
  10. friends, if we should ne'er have need of 'em? they
  11. were the most needless creatures living, should we
  12. ne'er have use for 'em, and would most resemble
  13. sweet instruments hung up in cases that keep their
  14. sounds to themselves. Why, I have often wished
  15. myself poorer, that I might come nearer to you. We
  16. are born to do benefits: and what better or
  17. properer can we can our own than the riches of our
  18. friends? O, what a precious comfort 'tis, to have
  19. so many, like brothers, commanding one another's
  20. fortunes! O joy, e'en made away ere 't can be born!
  21. Mine eyes cannot hold out water, methinks: to
  22. forget their faults, I drink to you.
  1. APEMANTUS
  2. Thou weepest to make them drink, Timon.

ACT IV

SCENE I. Without the walls of Athens.

Enter TIMON
  1. TIMON
  2. Let me look back upon thee. O thou wall,
  3. That girdlest in those wolves, dive in the earth,
  4. And fence not Athens! Matrons, turn incontinent!
  5. Obedience fail in children! slaves and fools,
  6. Pluck the grave wrinkled senate from the bench,
  7. And minister in their steads! to general filths
  8. Convert o' the instant, green virginity,
  9. Do 't in your parents' eyes! bankrupts, hold fast;
  10. Rather than render back, out with your knives,
  11. And cut your trusters' throats! bound servants, steal!
  12. Large-handed robbers your grave masters are,
  13. And pill by law. Maid, to thy master's bed;
  14. Thy mistress is o' the brothel! Son of sixteen,
  15. pluck the lined crutch from thy old limping sire,
  16. With it beat out his brains! Piety, and fear,
  17. Religion to the gods, peace, justice, truth,
  18. Domestic awe, night-rest, and neighbourhood,
  19. Instruction, manners, mysteries, and trades,
  20. Degrees, observances, customs, and laws,
  21. Decline to your confounding contraries,
  22. And let confusion live! Plagues, incident to men,
  23. Your potent and infectious fevers heap
  24. On Athens, ripe for stroke! Thou cold sciatica,
  25. Cripple our senators, that their limbs may halt
  26. As lamely as their manners. Lust and liberty
  27. Creep in the minds and marrows of our youth,
  28. That 'gainst the stream of virtue they may strive,
  29. And drown themselves in riot! Itches, blains,
  30. Sow all the Athenian bosoms; and their crop
  31. Be general leprosy! Breath infect breath,
  32. at their society, as their friendship, may
  33. merely poison! Nothing I'll bear from thee,
  34. But nakedness, thou detestable town!
  35. Take thou that too, with multiplying bans!
  36. Timon will to the woods; where he shall find
  37. The unkindest beast more kinder than mankind.
  38. The gods confound — hear me, you good gods all —
  39. The Athenians both within and out that wall!
  40. And grant, as Timon grows, his hate may grow
  41. To the whole race of mankind, high and low! Amen.
Exit

  15. But I do prize it at my love before

  2. That's well spoke.

  4. A parley sounded

  8. The scope of justice; till now myself and such
  9. As slept within the shadow of your power

  11. Our sufferance vainly: now the time is flush,
  12. When crouching marrow in the bearer strong
  13. Cries of itself 'No more:' now breathless wrong
  14. Shall sit and pant in your great chairs of ease,
  15. And pursy insolence shall break his wind
  16. With fear and horrid flight.

  2. Noble and young,

  4. By humble message and by promised means:

  2. These walls of ours

  7. For private faults in them.

  9. Which nature loathes — take thou the destined tenth,
  10. And by the hazard of the spotted die
  11. Let die the spotted.
  1. First Senator
  2. All have not offended;

  4. On those that are, revenges: crimes, like lands,

  6. Bring in thy ranks, but leave without thy rage:
  7. Spare thy Athenian cradle and those kin
  8. Which in the bluster of thy wrath must fall
  9. With those that have offended: like a shepherd,
  10. Approach the fold and cull the infected forth,
  11. But kill not all together.
  1. Second Senator
  2. What thou wilt,

  4. Than hew to't with thy sword.
  1. First Senator
  2. Set but thy foot

  1. Second Senator
  2. Throw thy glove,

  11. At heaviest answer.

  2. 'Tis most nobly spoken.
  1. ALCIBIADES
  2. Descend, and keep your words.
