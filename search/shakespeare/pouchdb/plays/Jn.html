SCENE Partly in England, and partly in France.

ACT I

SCENE I. KING JOHN'S palace.

Enter KING JOHN, QUEEN ELINOR, PEMBROKE, ESSEX, SALISBURY, and others, with CHATILLON
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Now, say, Chatillon, what would France with us?
  1. CHATILLON
  2. Thus, after greeting, speaks the King of France
  3. In my behavior to the majesty,
  4. The borrow'd majesty, of England here.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. A strange beginning: 'borrow'd majesty!'
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Silence, good mother; hear the embassy.
  1. CHATILLON
  2. Philip of France, in right and true behalf
  3. Of thy deceased brother Geffrey's son,
  4. Arthur Plantagenet, lays most lawful claim
  5. To this fair island and the territories,
  6. To Ireland, Poictiers, Anjou, Touraine, Maine,
  7. Desiring thee to lay aside the sword
  8. Which sways usurpingly these several titles,
  9. And put these same into young Arthur's hand,
  10. Thy nephew and right royal sovereign.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. What follows if we disallow of this?
  1. CHATILLON
  2. The proud control of fierce and bloody war,
  3. To enforce these rights so forcibly withheld.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Here have we war for war and blood for blood,
  3. Controlment for controlment: so answer France.
  1. CHATILLON
  2. Then take my king's defiance from my mouth,
  3. The farthest limit of my embassy.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Bear mine to him, and so depart in peace:
  3. Be thou as lightning in the eyes of France;
  4. For ere thou canst report I will be there,
  5. The thunder of my cannon shall be heard:
  6. So hence! Be thou the trumpet of our wrath
  7. And sullen presage of your own decay.
  8. An honourable conduct let him have:
  9. Pembroke, look to 't. Farewell, Chatillon.
Exeunt CHATILLON and PEMBROKE
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. What now, my son! have I not ever said
  3. How that ambitious Constance would not cease
  4. Till she had kindled France and all the world,
  5. Upon the right and party of her son?
  6. This might have been prevented and made whole
  7. With very easy arguments of love,
  8. Which now the manage of two kingdoms must
  9. With fearful bloody issue arbitrate.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Our strong possession and our right for us.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Your strong possession much more than your right,
  3. Or else it must go wrong with you and me:
  4. So much my conscience whispers in your ear,
  5. Which none but heaven and you and I shall hear.
Enter a Sheriff
  1. ESSEX
  2. My liege, here is the strangest controversy
  3. Come from country to be judged by you,
  4. That e'er I heard: shall I produce the men?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Let them approach.
  3. Our abbeys and our priories shall pay
  4. This expedition's charge.
  5. Enter ROBERT and the BASTARD
  6. What men are you?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Your faithful subject I, a gentleman
  3. Born in Northamptonshire and eldest son,
  4. As I suppose, to Robert Faulconbridge,
  5. A soldier, by the honour-giving hand
  6. Of Coeur-de-lion knighted in the field.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. What art thou?
  1. ROBERT
  2. The son and heir to that same Faulconbridge.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Is that the elder, and art thou the heir?
  3. You came not of one mother then, it seems.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Most certain of one mother, mighty king;
  3. That is well known; and, as I think, one father:
  4. But for the certain knowledge of that truth
  5. I put you o'er to heaven and to my mother:
  6. Of that I doubt, as all men's children may.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Out on thee, rude man! thou dost shame thy mother
  3. And wound her honour with this diffidence.
  1. BASTARD
  2. I, madam? no, I have no reason for it;
  3. That is my brother's plea and none of mine;
  4. The which if he can prove, a' pops me out
  5. At least from fair five hundred pound a year:
  6. Heaven guard my mother's honour and my land!
  1. KING JOHN
  2. A good blunt fellow. Why, being younger born,
  3. Doth he lay claim to thine inheritance?
  1. BASTARD
  2. I know not why, except to get the land.
  3. But once he slander'd me with bastardy:
  4. But whether I be as true begot or no,
  5. That still I lay upon my mother's head,
  6. But that I am as well begot, my liege —
  7. Fair fall the bones that took the pains for me! —
  8. Compare our faces and be judge yourself.
  9. If old sir Robert did beget us both
  10. And were our father and this son like him,
  11. O old sir Robert, father, on my knee
  12. I give heaven thanks I was not like to thee!
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Why, what a madcap hath heaven lent us here!
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. He hath a trick of Coeur-de-lion's face;
  3. The accent of his tongue affecteth him.
  4. Do you not read some tokens of my son
  5. In the large composition of this man?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Mine eye hath well examined his parts
  3. And finds them perfect Richard. Sirrah, speak,
  4. What doth move you to claim your brother's land?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Because he hath a half-face, like my father.
  3. With half that face would he have all my land:
  4. A half-faced groat five hundred pound a year!
  1. ROBERT
  2. My gracious liege, when that my father lived,
  3. Your brother did employ my father much —
  1. BASTARD
  2. Well, sir, by this you cannot get my land:
  3. Your tale must be how he employ'd my mother.
  1. ROBERT
  2. And once dispatch'd him in an embassy
  3. To Germany, there with the emperor
  4. To treat of high affairs touching that time.
  5. The advantage of his absence took the king
  6. And in the mean time sojourn'd at my father's;
  7. Where how he did prevail I shame to speak,
  8. But truth is truth: large lengths of seas and shores
  9. Between my father and my mother lay,
  10. As I have heard my father speak himself,
  11. When this same lusty gentleman was got.
  12. Upon his death-bed he by will bequeath'd
  13. His lands to me, and took it on his death
  14. That this my mother's son was none of his;
  15. And if he were, he came into the world
  16. Full fourteen weeks before the course of time.
  17. Then, good my liege, let me have what is mine,
  18. My father's land, as was my father's will.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Sirrah, your brother is legitimate;
  3. Your father's wife did after wedlock bear him,
  4. And if she did play false, the fault was hers;
  5. Which fault lies on the hazards of all husbands
  6. That marry wives. Tell me, how if my brother,
  7. Who, as you say, took pains to get this son,
  8. Had of your father claim'd this son for his?
  9. In sooth, good friend, your father might have kept
  10. This calf bred from his cow from all the world;
  11. In sooth he might; then, if he were my brother's,
  12. My brother might not claim him; nor your father,
  13. Being none of his, refuse him: this concludes;
  14. My mother's son did get your father's heir;
  15. Your father's heir must have your father's land.
  1. ROBERT
  2. Shall then my father's will be of no force
  3. To dispossess that child which is not his?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Of no more force to dispossess me, sir,
  3. Than was his will to get me, as I think.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Whether hadst thou rather be a Faulconbridge
  3. And like thy brother, to enjoy thy land,
  4. Or the reputed son of Coeur-de-lion,
  5. Lord of thy presence and no land beside?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Madam, an if my brother had my shape,
  3. And I had his, sir Robert's his, like him;
  4. And if my legs were two such riding-rods,
  5. My arms such eel-skins stuff'd, my face so thin
  6. That in mine ear I durst not stick a rose
  7. Lest men should say 'Look, where three-farthings goes!'
  8. And, to his shape, were heir to all this land,
  9. Would I might never stir from off this place,
  10. I would give it every foot to have this face;
  11. I would not be sir Nob in any case.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. I like thee well: wilt thou forsake thy fortune,
  3. Bequeath thy land to him and follow me?
  4. I am a soldier and now bound to France.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Brother, take you my land, I'll take my chance.
  3. Your face hath got five hundred pound a year,
  4. Yet sell your face for five pence and 'tis dear.
  5. Madam, I'll follow you unto the death.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Nay, I would have you go before me thither.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Our country manners give our betters way.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. What is thy name?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Philip, my liege, so is my name begun,
  3. Philip, good old sir Robert's wife's eldest son.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. From henceforth bear his name whose form thou bear'st:
  3. Kneel thou down Philip, but rise more great,
  4. Arise sir Richard and Plantagenet.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Brother by the mother's side, give me your hand:
  3. My father gave me honour, yours gave land.
  4. Now blessed by the hour, by night or day,
  5. When I was got, sir Robert was away!
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. The very spirit of Plantagenet!
  3. I am thy grandam, Richard; call me so.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Madam, by chance but not by truth; what though?
  3. Something about, a little from the right,
  4. In at the window, or else o'er the hatch:
  5. Who dares not stir by day must walk by night,
  6. And have is have, however men do catch:
  7. Near or far off, well won is still well shot,
  8. And I am I, howe'er I was begot.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Go, Faulconbridge: now hast thou thy desire;
  3. A landless knight makes thee a landed squire.
  4. Come, madam, and come, Richard, we must speed
  5. For France, for France, for it is more than need.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Brother, adieu: good fortune come to thee!
  3. For thou wast got i' the way of honesty.
  4. Exeunt all but BASTARD
  5. A foot of honour better than I was;
  6. But many a many foot of land the worse.
  7. Well, now can I make any Joan a lady.
  8. 'Good den, sir Richard!' — 'God-a-mercy, fellow!' —
  9. And if his name be George, I'll call him Peter;
  10. For new-made honour doth forget men's names;
  11. 'Tis too respective and too sociable
  12. For your conversion. Now your traveller,
  13. He and his toothpick at my worship's mess,
  14. And when my knightly stomach is sufficed,
  15. Why then I suck my teeth and catechise
  16. My picked man of countries: 'My dear sir,'
  17. Thus, leaning on mine elbow, I begin,
  18. 'I shall beseech you' — that is question now;
  19. And then comes answer like an Absey book:
  20. 'O sir,' says answer, 'at your best command;
  21. At your employment; at your service, sir;'
  22. 'No, sir,' says question, 'I, sweet sir, at yours:'
  23. And so, ere answer knows what question would,
  24. Saving in dialogue of compliment,
  25. And talking of the Alps and Apennines,
  26. The Pyrenean and the river Po,
  27. It draws toward supper in conclusion so.
  28. But this is worshipful society
  29. And fits the mounting spirit like myself,
  30. For he is but a bastard to the time
  31. That doth not smack of observation;
  32. And so am I, whether I smack or no;
  33. And not alone in habit and device,
  34. Exterior form, outward accoutrement,
  35. But from the inward motion to deliver
  36. Sweet, sweet, sweet poison for the age's tooth:
  37. Which, though I will not practise to deceive,
  38. Yet, to avoid deceit, I mean to learn;
  39. For it shall strew the footsteps of my rising.
  40. But who comes in such haste in riding-robes?
  41. What woman-post is this? hath she no husband
  42. That will take pains to blow a horn before her?
  43. Enter LADY FAULCONBRIDGE and GURNEY
  44. O me! it is my mother. How now, good lady!
  45. What brings you here to court so hastily?
  1. LADY FAULCONBRIDGE
  2. Where is that slave, thy brother? where is he,
  3. That holds in chase mine honour up and down?
  1. BASTARD
  2. My brother Robert? old sir Robert's son?
  3. Colbrand the giant, that same mighty man?
  4. Is it sir Robert's son that you seek so?
  1. LADY FAULCONBRIDGE
  2. Sir Robert's son! Ay, thou unreverend boy,
  3. Sir Robert's son: why scorn'st thou at sir Robert?
  4. He is sir Robert's son, and so art thou.
  1. BASTARD
  2. James Gurney, wilt thou give us leave awhile?
  1. GURNEY
  2. Good leave, good Philip.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Philip! sparrow: James,
  3. There's toys abroad: anon I'll tell thee more.
  4. Exit GURNEY
  5. Madam, I was not old sir Robert's son:
  6. Sir Robert might have eat his part in me
  7. Upon Good-Friday and ne'er broke his fast:
  8. Sir Robert could do well: marry, to confess,
  9. Could he get me? Sir Robert could not do it:
  10. We know his handiwork: therefore, good mother,
  11. To whom am I beholding for these limbs?
  12. Sir Robert never holp to make this leg.
  1. LADY FAULCONBRIDGE
  2. Hast thou conspired with thy brother too,
  3. That for thine own gain shouldst defend mine honour?
  4. What means this scorn, thou most untoward knave?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Knight, knight, good mother, Basilisco-like.
  3. What! I am dubb'd! I have it on my shoulder.
  4. But, mother, I am not sir Robert's son;
  5. I have disclaim'd sir Robert and my land;
  6. Legitimation, name and all is gone:
  7. Then, good my mother, let me know my father;
  8. Some proper man, I hope: who was it, mother?
  1. LADY FAULCONBRIDGE
  2. Hast thou denied thyself a Faulconbridge?
  1. BASTARD
  2. As faithfully as I deny the devil.
  1. LADY FAULCONBRIDGE
  2. King Richard Coeur-de-lion was thy father:
  3. By long and vehement suit I was seduced
  4. To make room for him in my husband's bed:
  5. Heaven lay not my transgression to my charge!
  6. Thou art the issue of my dear offence,
  7. Which was so strongly urged past my defence.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Now, by this light, were I to get again,
  3. Madam, I would not wish a better father.
  4. Some sins do bear their privilege on earth,
  5. And so doth yours; your fault was not your folly:
  6. Needs must you lay your heart at his dispose,
  7. Subjected tribute to commanding love,
  8. Against whose fury and unmatched force
  9. The aweless lion could not wage the fight,
  10. Nor keep his princely heart from Richard's hand.
  11. He that perforce robs lions of their hearts
  12. May easily win a woman's. Ay, my mother,
  13. With all my heart I thank thee for my father!
  14. Who lives and dares but say thou didst not well
  15. When I was got, I'll send his soul to hell.
  16. Come, lady, I will show thee to my kin;
  17. And they shall say, when Richard me begot,
  18. If thou hadst said him nay, it had been sin:
  19. Who says it was, he lies; I say 'twas not.
Exeunt

ACT II

SCENE I. France. Before Angiers.

Enter AUSTRIA and forces, drums, etc. on one side: on the other KING PHILIP and his power; LEWIS, ARTHUR, CONSTANCE and attendants
  1. LEWIS
  2. Before Angiers well met, brave Austria.
  3. Arthur, that great forerunner of thy blood,
  4. Richard, that robb'd the lion of his heart
  5. And fought the holy wars in Palestine,
  6. By this brave duke came early to his grave:
  7. And for amends to his posterity,
  8. At our importance hither is he come,
  9. To spread his colours, boy, in thy behalf,
  10. And to rebuke the usurpation
  11. Of thy unnatural uncle, English John:
  12. Embrace him, love him, give him welcome hither.
  1. ARTHUR
  2. God shall forgive you Coeur-de-lion's death
  3. The rather that you give his offspring life,
  4. Shadowing their right under your wings of war:
  5. I give you welcome with a powerless hand,
  6. But with a heart full of unstained love:
  7. Welcome before the gates of Angiers, duke.
  1. LEWIS
  2. A noble boy! Who would not do thee right?
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. Upon thy cheek lay I this zealous kiss,
  3. As seal to this indenture of my love,
  4. That to my home I will no more return,
  5. Till Angiers and the right thou hast in France,
  6. Together with that pale, that white-faced shore,
  7. Whose foot spurns back the ocean's roaring tides
  8. And coops from other lands her islanders,
  9. Even till that England, hedged in with the main,
  10. That water-walled bulwark, still secure
  11. And confident from foreign purposes,
  12. Even till that utmost corner of the west
  13. Salute thee for her king: till then, fair boy,
  14. Will I not think of home, but follow arms.
  1. CONSTANCE
  2. O, take his mother's thanks, a widow's thanks,
  3. Till your strong hand shall help to give him strength
  4. To make a more requital to your love!
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. The peace of heaven is theirs that lift their swords
  3. In such a just and charitable war.
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. Well then, to work: our cannon shall be bent
  3. Against the brows of this resisting town.
  4. Call for our chiefest men of discipline,
  5. To cull the plots of best advantages:
  6. We'll lay before this town our royal bones,
  7. Wade to the market-place in Frenchmen's blood,
  8. But we will make it subject to this boy.
  1. CONSTANCE
  2. Stay for an answer to your embassy,
  3. Lest unadvised you stain your swords with blood:
  4. My Lord Chatillon may from England bring,
  5. That right in peace which here we urge in war,
  6. And then we shall repent each drop of blood
  7. That hot rash haste so indirectly shed.
Enter CHATILLON
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. A wonder, lady! lo, upon thy wish,
  3. Our messenger Chatillon is arrived!
  4. What England says, say briefly, gentle lord;
  5. We coldly pause for thee; Chatillon, speak.
  1. CHATILLON
  2. Then turn your forces from this paltry siege
  3. And stir them up against a mightier task.
  4. England, impatient of your just demands,
  5. Hath put himself in arms: the adverse winds,
  6. Whose leisure I have stay'd, have given him time
  7. To land his legions all as soon as I;
  8. His marches are expedient to this town,
  9. His forces strong, his soldiers confident.
  10. With him along is come the mother-queen,
  11. An Ate, stirring him to blood and strife;
  12. With her her niece, the Lady Blanch of Spain;
  13. With them a bastard of the king's deceased,
  14. And all the unsettled humours of the land,
  15. Rash, inconsiderate, fiery voluntaries,
  16. With ladies' faces and fierce dragons' spleens,
  17. Have sold their fortunes at their native homes,
  18. Bearing their birthrights proudly on their backs,
  19. To make hazard of new fortunes here:
  20. In brief, a braver choice of dauntless spirits
  21. Than now the English bottoms have waft o'er
  22. Did nearer float upon the swelling tide,
  23. To do offence and scath in Christendom.
  24. Drum beats
  25. The interruption of their churlish drums
  26. Cuts off more circumstance: they are at hand,
  27. To parley or to fight; therefore prepare.
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. How much unlook'd for is this expedition!
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. By how much unexpected, by so much
  3. We must awake endavour for defence;
  4. For courage mounteth with occasion:
  5. Let them be welcome then: we are prepared.
Enter KING JOHN, QUEEN ELINOR, BLANCH, the BASTARD, Lords, and forces
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Peace be to France, if France in peace permit
  3. Our just and lineal entrance to our own;
  4. If not, bleed France, and peace ascend to heaven,
  5. Whiles we, God's wrathful agent, do correct
  6. Their proud contempt that beats His peace to heaven.
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. Peace be to England, if that war return
  3. From France to England, there to live in peace.
  4. England we love; and for that England's sake
  5. With burden of our armour here we sweat.
  6. This toil of ours should be a work of thine;
  7. But thou from loving England art so far,
  8. That thou hast under-wrought his lawful king
  9. Cut off the sequence of posterity,
  10. Out-faced infant state and done a rape
  11. Upon the maiden virtue of the crown.
  12. Look here upon thy brother Geffrey's face;
  13. These eyes, these brows, were moulded out of his:
  14. This little abstract doth contain that large
  15. Which died in Geffrey, and the hand of time
  16. Shall draw this brief into as huge a volume.
  17. That Geffrey was thy elder brother born,
  18. And this his son; England was Geffrey's right
  19. And this is Geffrey's: in the name of God
  20. How comes it then that thou art call'd a king,
  21. When living blood doth in these temples beat,
  22. Which owe the crown that thou o'ermasterest?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. From whom hast thou this great commission, France,
  3. To draw my answer from thy articles?
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. From that supernal judge, that stirs good thoughts
  3. In any breast of strong authority,
  4. To look into the blots and stains of right:
  5. That judge hath made me guardian to this boy:
  6. Under whose warrant I impeach thy wrong
  7. And by whose help I mean to chastise it.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Alack, thou dost usurp authority.
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. Excuse; it is to beat usurping down.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Who is it thou dost call usurper, France?
  1. CONSTANCE
  2. Let me make answer; thy usurping son.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. Out, insolent! thy bastard shall be king,
  3. That thou mayst be a queen, and cheque the world!
  1. CONSTANCE
  2. My bed was ever to thy son as true
  3. As thine was to thy husband; and this boy
  4. Liker in feature to his father Geffrey
  5. Than thou and John in manners; being as like
  6. As rain to water, or devil to his dam.
  7. My boy a bastard! By my soul, I think
  8. His father never was so true begot:
  9. It cannot be, an if thou wert his mother.
  1. QUEEN ELINOR
  2. There's a good mother, boy, that blots thy father.
  1. CONSTANCE
  2. There's a good grandam, boy, that would blot thee.
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. Peace!
  1. BASTARD
  2. Hear the crier.
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. What the devil art thou?
  1. BASTARD
  2. One that will play the devil, sir, with you,
  3. An a' may catch your hide and you alone:
  4. You are the hare of whom the proverb goes,
  5. Whose valour plucks dead lions by the beard;
  6. I'll smoke your skin-coat, an I catch you right;
  7. Sirrah, look to't; i' faith, I will, i' faith.
  1. BLANCH
  2. O, well did he become that lion's robe
  3. That did disrobe the lion of that robe!
  1. BASTARD
  2. It lies as sightly on the back of him
  3. As great Alcides' shows upon an ass:
  4. But, ass, I'll take that burthen from your back,
  5. Or lay on that shall make your shoulders crack.
  1. AUSTRIA
  2. What craker is this same that deafs our ears
  3. With this abundance of superfluous breath?
  1. KING PHILIP
  2. Lewis, determine what we shall do straight.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Women and fools, break off your conference.
  3. King John, this is the very sum of all;
  4. England and Ireland, Anjou, Touraine, Maine,
  5. In right of Arthur do I claim of thee:
  6. Wilt thou resign them and lay down thy arms?

  3. And looked upon, I hope, with cheerful eyes.

  4. And that high royalty was ne'er pluck'd off,
  5. The faiths of men ne'er stained with revolt;
  6. Fresh expectation troubled not the land
  7. With any long'd-for change or better state.

  2. Therefore, to be possess'd with double pomp,
  3. To guard a title that was rich before,
  4. To gild refined gold, to paint the lily,
  5. To throw a perfume on the violet,
  6. To smooth the ice, or add another hue
  7. Unto the rainbow, or with taper-light
  8. To seek the beauteous eye of heaven to garnish,
  9. Is wasteful and ridiculous excess.

  2. But that your royal pleasure must be done,
  3. This act is as an ancient tale new told,
  4. And in the last repeating troublesome,
  5. Being urged at a time unseasonable.

  2. In this the antique and well noted face
  3. Of plain old form is much disfigured;
  4. And, like a shifted wind unto a sail,
  5. It makes the course of thoughts to fetch about,
  6. Startles and frights consideration,
  7. Makes sound opinion sick and truth suspected,
  8. For putting on so new a fashion'd robe.

  3. They do confound their skill in covetousness;
  4. And oftentimes excusing of a fault
  5. Doth make the fault the worse by the excuse,
  6. As patches set upon a little breach
  7. Discredit more in hiding of the fault
  8. Than did the fault before it was so patch'd.

  9. With wrinkled brows, with nods, with rolling eyes.

  11. The whilst his iron did on the anvil cool,
  12. With open mouth swallowing a tailor's news;
  13. Who, with his shears and measure in his hand,
  14. Standing on slippers, which his nimble haste
  15. Had falsely thrust upon contrary feet,
  16. Told of a many thousand warlike French

  18. Another lean unwash'd artificer
  19. Cuts off his tale and talks of Arthur's death.

  2. Why seek'st thou to possess me with these fears?

  5. To wish him dead, but thou hadst none to kill him.
  1. HUBERT
  2. No had, my lord! why, did you not provoke me?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. It is the curse of kings to be attended
  3. By slaves that take their humours for a warrant
  4. To break within the bloody house of life,
  5. And on the winking of authority
  6. To understand a law, to know the meaning
  7. Of dangerous majesty, when perchance it frowns

  2. Here is your hand and seal for what I did.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. O, when the last account 'twixt heaven and earth

  11. Finding thee fit for bloody villany,
  12. Apt, liable to be employ'd in danger,
  13. I faintly broke with thee of Arthur's death;

  15. Made it no conscience to destroy a prince.
  1. HUBERT
  2. My lord —
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Hadst thou but shook thy head or made a pause
  3. When I spake darkly what I purposed,
  4. Or turn'd an eye of doubt upon my face,

  6. Deep shame had struck me dumb, made me break off,
  7. And those thy fears might have wrought fears in me:
  8. But thou didst understand me by my signs
  9. And didst in signs again parley with sin;
  10. Yea, without stop, didst let thy heart consent,
  11. And consequently thy rude hand to act
  12. The deed, which both our tongues held vile to name.

  15. Even at my gates, with ranks of foreign powers:
  16. Nay, in the body of this fleshly land,

  18. Hostility and civil tumult reigns
  19. Between my conscience and my cousin's death.

  2. Arm you against your other enemies,
  3. I'll make a peace between your soul and you.

  5. Is yet a maiden and an innocent hand,
  6. Not painted with the crimson spots of blood.

  8. The dreadful motion of a murderous thought;
  9. And you have slander'd nature in my form,
  10. Which, howsoever rude exteriorly,
  11. Is yet the cover of a fairer mind
  12. Than to be butcher of an innocent child.

  2. Doth Arthur live? O, haste thee to the peers,
  3. Throw this report on their incensed rage,
  4. And make them tame to their obedience!

  6. Upon thy feature; for my rage was blind,
  7. And foul imaginary eyes of blood
  8. Presented thee more hideous than thou art.

  10. The angry lords with all expedient haste.

ACT V

SCENE I. KING JOHN'S palace.

Enter KING JOHN, CARDINAL PANDULPH, and Attendants

  2. Thus have I yielded up into your hand
  3. The circle of my glory.
Giving the crown
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. Take again
  3. From this my hand, as holding of the pope
  4. Your sovereign greatness and authority.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Now keep your holy word: go meet the French,
  3. And from his holiness use all your power
  4. To stop their marches 'fore we are inflamed.
  5. Our discontented counties do revolt;
  6. Our people quarrel with obedience,
  7. Swearing allegiance and the love of soul
  8. To stranger blood, to foreign royalty.
  9. This inundation of mistemper'd humour
  10. Rests by you only to be qualified:
  11. Then pause not; for the present time's so sick,
  12. That present medicine must be minister'd,
  13. Or overthrow incurable ensues.
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. It was my breath that blew this tempest up,
  3. Upon your stubborn usage of the pope;
  4. But since you are a gentle convertite,
  5. My tongue shall hush again this storm of war
  6. And make fair weather in your blustering land.
  7. On this Ascension-day, remember well,
  8. Upon your oath of service to the pope,
  9. Go I to make the French lay down their arms.
Exit
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Is this Ascension-day? Did not the prophet
  3. Say that before Ascension-day at noon
  4. My crown I should give off? Even so I have:
  5. I did suppose it should be on constraint:
  6. But, heaven be thank'd, it is but voluntary.
Enter the BASTARD
  1. BASTARD
  2. All Kent hath yielded; nothing there holds out
  3. But Dover castle: London hath received,
  4. Like a kind host, the Dauphin and his powers:
  5. Your nobles will not hear you, but are gone
  6. To offer service to your enemy,
  7. And wild amazement hurries up and down
  8. The little number of your doubtful friends.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Would not my lords return to me again,
  3. After they heard young Arthur was alive?
  1. BASTARD
  2. They found him dead and cast into the streets,
  3. An empty casket, where the jewel of life
  4. By some damn'd hand was robb'd and ta'en away.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. That villain Hubert told me he did live.
  1. BASTARD
  2. So, on my soul, he did, for aught he knew.
  3. But wherefore do you droop? why look you sad?
  4. Be great in act, as you have been in thought;
  5. Let not the world see fear and sad distrust
  6. Govern the motion of a kingly eye:
  7. Be stirring as the time; be fire with fire;
  8. Threaten the threatener and outface the brow
  9. Of bragging horror: so shall inferior eyes,
  10. That borrow their behaviors from the great,
  11. Grow great by your example and put on
  12. The dauntless spirit of resolution.
  13. Away, and glister like the god of war,
  14. When he intendeth to become the field:
  15. Show boldness and aspiring confidence.
  16. What, shall they seek the lion in his den,
  17. And fright him there? and make him tremble there?
  18. O, let it not be said: forage, and run
  19. To meet displeasure farther from the doors,
  20. And grapple with him ere he comes so nigh.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. The legate of the pope hath been with me,
  3. And I have made a happy peace with him;
  4. And he hath promised to dismiss the powers
  5. Led by the Dauphin.
  1. BASTARD
  2. O inglorious league!
  3. Shall we, upon the footing of our land,
  4. Send fair-play orders and make compromise,
  5. Insinuation, parley and base truce
  6. To arms invasive? shall a beardless boy,
  7. A cocker'd silken wanton, brave our fields,
  8. And flesh his spirit in a warlike soil,
  9. Mocking the air with colours idly spread,
  10. And find no cheque? Let us, my liege, to arms:
  11. Perchance the cardinal cannot make your peace;
  12. Or if he do, let it at least be said
  13. They saw we had a purpose of defence.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Have thou the ordering of this present time.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Away, then, with good courage! yet, I know,
  3. Our party may well meet a prouder foe.

  2. My Lord Melun, let this be copied out,
  3. And keep it safe for our remembrance:
  4. Return the precedent to these lords again;
  5. That, having our fair order written down,
  6. Both they and we, perusing o'er these notes,
  7. May know wherefore we took the sacrament
  8. And keep our faiths firm and inviolable.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. Upon our sides it never shall be broken.
  3. And, noble Dauphin, albeit we swear
  4. A voluntary zeal and an unurged faith
  5. To your proceedings; yet believe me, prince,
  6. I am not glad that such a sore of time
  7. Should seek a plaster by contemn'd revolt,
  8. And heal the inveterate canker of one wound
  9. By making many. O, it grieves my soul,
  10. That I must draw this metal from my side
  11. To be a widow-maker! O, and there
  12. Where honourable rescue and defence
  13. Cries out upon the name of Salisbury!
  14. But such is the infection of the time,
  15. That, for the health and physic of our right,
  16. We cannot deal but with the very hand
  17. Of stern injustice and confused wrong.
  18. And is't not pity, O my grieved friends,
  19. That we, the sons and children of this isle,
  20. Were born to see so sad an hour as this;
  21. Wherein we step after a stranger march
  22. Upon her gentle bosom, and fill up
  23. Her enemies' ranks — I must withdraw and weep
  24. Upon the spot of this enforced cause —
  25. To grace the gentry of a land remote,
  26. And follow unacquainted colours here?
  27. What, here? O nation, that thou couldst remove!
  28. That Neptune's arms, who clippeth thee about,
  29. Would bear thee from the knowledge of thyself,
  30. And grapple thee unto a pagan shore;
  31. Where these two Christian armies might combine
  32. The blood of malice in a vein of league,
  33. And not to spend it so unneighbourly!
  1. LEWIS
  2. A noble temper dost thou show in this;
  3. And great affections wrestling in thy bosom
  4. Doth make an earthquake of nobility.
  5. O, what a noble combat hast thou fought
  6. Between compulsion and a brave respect!
  7. Let me wipe off this honourable dew,
  8. That silverly doth progress on thy cheeks:
  9. My heart hath melted at a lady's tears,
  10. Being an ordinary inundation;
  11. But this effusion of such manly drops,
  12. This shower, blown up by tempest of the soul,
  13. Startles mine eyes, and makes me more amazed
  14. Than had I seen the vaulty top of heaven
  15. Figured quite o'er with burning meteors.
  16. Lift up thy brow, renowned Salisbury,
  17. And with a great heart heave away the storm:
  18. Commend these waters to those baby eyes
  19. That never saw the giant world enraged;
  20. Nor met with fortune other than at feasts,
  21. Full of warm blood, of mirth, of gossiping.
  22. Come, come; for thou shalt thrust thy hand as deep
  23. Into the purse of rich prosperity
  24. As Lewis himself: so, nobles, shall you all,
  25. That knit your sinews to the strength of mine.
  26. And even there, methinks, an angel spake:
  27. Enter CARDINAL PANDULPH
  28. Look, where the holy legate comes apace,
  29. To give us warrant from the hand of heaven
  30. And on our actions set the name of right
  31. With holy breath.
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. Hail, noble prince of France!
  3. The next is this, King John hath reconciled
  4. Himself to Rome; his spirit is come in,
  5. That so stood out against the holy church,
  6. The great metropolis and see of Rome:
  7. Therefore thy threatening colours now wind up;
  8. And tame the savage spirit of wild war,
  9. That like a lion foster'd up at hand,
  10. It may lie gently at the foot of peace,
  11. And be no further harmful than in show.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Your grace shall pardon me, I will not back:
  3. I am too high-born to be propertied,
  4. To be a secondary at control,
  5. Or useful serving-man and instrument,
  6. To any sovereign state throughout the world.
  7. Your breath first kindled the dead coal of wars
  8. Between this chastised kingdom and myself,
  9. And brought in matter that should feed this fire;
  10. And now 'tis far too huge to be blown out
  11. With that same weak wind which enkindled it.
  12. You taught me how to know the face of right,
  13. Acquainted me with interest to this land,
  14. Yea, thrust this enterprise into my heart;
  15. And come ye now to tell me John hath made
  16. His peace with Rome? What is that peace to me?
  17. I, by the honour of my marriage-bed,
  18. After young Arthur, claim this land for mine;
  19. And, now it is half-conquer'd, must I back
  20. Because that John hath made his peace with Rome?
  21. Am I Rome's slave? What penny hath Rome borne,
  22. What men provided, what munition sent,
  23. To underprop this action? Is't not I
  24. That undergo this charge? who else but I,
  25. And such as to my claim are liable,
  26. Sweat in this business and maintain this war?
  27. Have I not heard these islanders shout out
  28. 'Vive le roi!' as I have bank'd their towns?
  29. Have I not here the best cards for the game,
  30. To win this easy match play'd for a crown?
  31. And shall I now give o'er the yielded set?
  32. No, no, on my soul, it never shall be said.
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. You look but on the outside of this work.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Outside or inside, I will not return
  3. Till my attempt so much be glorified
  4. As to my ample hope was promised
  5. Before I drew this gallant head of war,
  6. And cull'd these fiery spirits from the world,
  7. To outlook conquest and to win renown
  8. Even in the jaws of danger and of death.
  9. Trumpet sounds
  10. What lusty trumpet thus doth summon us?

  1. BASTARD
  2. According to the fair play of the world,
  3. Let me have audience; I am sent to speak:
  4. My holy lord of Milan, from the king
  5. I come, to learn how you have dealt for him;
  6. And, as you answer, I do know the scope
  7. And warrant limited unto my tongue.
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. The Dauphin is too wilful-opposite,
  3. And will not temporize with my entreaties;
  4. He flatly says he'll not lay down his arms.
  1. BASTARD
  2. By all the blood that ever fury breathed,
  3. The youth says well. Now hear our English king;
  4. For thus his royalty doth speak in me.
  5. He is prepared, and reason too he should:
  6. This apish and unmannerly approach,
  7. This harness'd masque and unadvised revel,
  8. This unhair'd sauciness and boyish troops,
  9. The king doth smile at; and is well prepared
  10. To whip this dwarfish war, these pigmy arms,
  11. From out the circle of his territories.
  12. That hand which had the strength, even at your door,
  13. To cudgel you and make you take the hatch,
  14. To dive like buckets in concealed wells,
  15. To crouch in litter of your stable planks,
  16. To lie like pawns lock'd up in chests and trunks,
  17. To hug with swine, to seek sweet safety out
  18. In vaults and prisons, and to thrill and shake
  19. Even at the crying of your nation's crow,
  20. Thinking his voice an armed Englishman;
  21. Shall that victorious hand be feebled here,
  22. That in your chambers gave you chastisement?
  23. No: know the gallant monarch is in arms
  24. And like an eagle o'er his aery towers,
  25. To souse annoyance that comes near his nest.
  26. And you degenerate, you ingrate revolts,
  27. You bloody Neroes, ripping up the womb
  28. Of your dear mother England, blush for shame;
  29. For your own ladies and pale-visaged maids
  30. Like Amazons come tripping after drums,
  31. Their thimbles into armed gauntlets change,
  32. Their needles to lances, and their gentle hearts
  33. To fierce and bloody inclination.
  1. LEWIS
  2. There end thy brave, and turn thy face in peace;
  3. We grant thou canst outscold us: fare thee well;
  4. We hold our time too precious to be spent
  5. With such a brabbler.
  1. CARDINAL PANDULPH
  2. Give me leave to speak.
  1. BASTARD
  2. No, I will speak.
  1. LEWIS
  2. We will attend to neither.
  3. Strike up the drums; and let the tongue of war
  4. Plead for our interest and our being here.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Indeed your drums, being beaten, will cry out;
  3. And so shall you, being beaten: do but start
  4. An echo with the clamour of thy drum,
  5. And even at hand a drum is ready braced
  6. That shall reverberate all as loud as thine;
  7. Sound but another, and another shall
  8. As loud as thine rattle the welkin's ear
  9. And mock the deep-mouth'd thunder: for at hand,
  10. Not trusting to this halting legate here,
  11. Whom he hath used rather for sport than need
  12. Is warlike John; and in his forehead sits
  13. A bare-ribb'd death, whose office is this day
  14. To feast upon whole thousands of the French.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Strike up our drums, to find this danger out.
  1. BASTARD
  2. And thou shalt find it, Dauphin, do not doubt.
Exeunt

SCENE III. The field of battle.

Alarums. Enter KING JOHN and HUBERT
  1. KING JOHN
  2. How goes the day with us? O, tell me, Hubert.
  1. HUBERT
  2. Badly, I fear. How fares your majesty?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. This fever, that hath troubled me so long,
  3. Lies heavy on me; O, my heart is sick!
Enter a Messenger
  1. Messenger
  2. My lord, your valiant kinsman, Faulconbridge,
  3. Desires your majesty to leave the field
  4. And send him word by me which way you go.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Tell him, toward Swinstead, to the abbey there.
  1. Messenger
  2. Be of good comfort; for the great supply
  3. That was expected by the Dauphin here,
  4. Are wreck'd three nights ago on Goodwin Sands.
  5. This news was brought to Richard but even now:
  6. The French fight coldly, and retire themselves.
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Ay me! this tyrant fever burns me up,
  3. And will not let me welcome this good news.
  4. Set on toward Swinstead: to my litter straight;
  5. Weakness possesseth me, and I am faint.
Exeunt

SCENE IV. Another part of the field.

Enter SALISBURY, PEMBROKE, and BIGOT
  1. SALISBURY
  2. I did not think the king so stored with friends.
  1. PEMBROKE
  2. Up once again; put spirit in the French:
  3. If they miscarry, we miscarry too.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. That misbegotten devil, Faulconbridge,
  3. In spite of spite, alone upholds the day.
  1. PEMBROKE
  2. They say King John sore sick hath left the field.
Enter MELUN, wounded
  1. MELUN
  2. Lead me to the revolts of England here.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. When we were happy we had other names.
  1. PEMBROKE
  2. It is the Count Melun.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. Wounded to death.
  1. MELUN
  2. Fly, noble English, you are bought and sold;
  3. Unthread the rude eye of rebellion
  4. And welcome home again discarded faith.
  5. Seek out King John and fall before his feet;
  6. For if the French be lords of this loud day,
  7. He means to recompense the pains you take
  8. By cutting off your heads: thus hath he sworn
  9. And I with him, and many moe with me,
  10. Upon the altar at Saint Edmundsbury;
  11. Even on that altar where we swore to you
  12. Dear amity and everlasting love.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. May this be possible? may this be true?
  1. MELUN
  2. Have I not hideous death within my view,
  3. Retaining but a quantity of life,
  4. Which bleeds away, even as a form of wax
  5. Resolveth from his figure 'gainst the fire?
  6. What in the world should make me now deceive,
  7. Since I must lose the use of all deceit?
  8. Why should I then be false, since it is true
  9. That I must die here and live hence by truth?
  10. I say again, if Lewis do win the day,
  11. He is forsworn, if e'er those eyes of yours
  12. Behold another day break in the east:
  13. But even this night, whose black contagious breath
  14. Already smokes about the burning crest
  15. Of the old, feeble and day-wearied sun,
  16. Even this ill night, your breathing shall expire,
  17. Paying the fine of rated treachery
  18. Even with a treacherous fine of all your lives,
  19. If Lewis by your assistance win the day.
  20. Commend me to one Hubert with your king:
  21. The love of him, and this respect besides,
  22. For that my grandsire was an Englishman,
  23. Awakes my conscience to confess all this.
  24. In lieu whereof, I pray you, bear me hence
  25. From forth the noise and rumour of the field,
  26. Where I may think the remnant of my thoughts
  27. In peace, and part this body and my soul
  28. With contemplation and devout desires.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. We do believe thee: and beshrew my soul
  3. But I do love the favour and the form
  4. Of this most fair occasion, by the which
  5. We will untread the steps of damned flight,
  6. And like a bated and retired flood,
  7. Leaving our rankness and irregular course,
  8. Stoop low within those bounds we have o'erlook'd
  9. And cabby run on in obedience
  10. Even to our ocean, to our great King John.
  11. My arm shall give thee help to bear thee hence;
  12. For I do see the cruel pangs of death
  13. Right in thine eye. Away, my friends! New flight;
  14. And happy newness, that intends old right.
Exeunt, leading off MELUN

SCENE V. The French camp.

Enter LEWIS and his train
  1. LEWIS
  2. The sun of heaven methought was loath to set,
  3. But stay'd and made the western welkin blush,
  4. When English measure backward their own ground
  5. In faint retire. O, bravely came we off,
  6. When with a volley of our needless shot,
  7. After such bloody toil, we bid good night;
  8. And wound our tattering colours clearly up,
  9. Last in the field, and almost lords of it!
Enter a Messenger
  1. Messenger
  2. Where is my prince, the Dauphin?
  1. LEWIS
  2. Here: what news?
  1. Messenger
  2. The Count Melun is slain; the English lords
  3. By his persuasion are again fall'n off,
  4. And your supply, which you have wish'd so long,
  5. Are cast away and sunk on Goodwin Sands.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Ah, foul shrewd news! beshrew thy very heart!
  3. I did not think to be so sad to-night
  4. As this hath made me. Who was he that said
  5. King John did fly an hour or two before
  6. The stumbling night did part our weary powers?
  1. Messenger
  2. Whoever spoke it, it is true, my lord.
  1. LEWIS
  2. Well; keep good quarter and good care to-night:
  3. The day shall not be up so soon as I,
  4. To try the fair adventure of to-morrow.
Exeunt

SCENE VI. An open place in the neighbourhood of Swinstead Abbey.

Enter the BASTARD and HUBERT, severally
  1. HUBERT
  2. Who's there? speak, ho! speak quickly, or I shoot.
  1. BASTARD
  2. A friend. What art thou?
  1. HUBERT
  2. Of the part of England.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Whither dost thou go?
  1. HUBERT
  2. What's that to thee? why may not I demand
  3. Of thine affairs, as well as thou of mine?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Hubert, I think?
  1. HUBERT
  2. Thou hast a perfect thought:
  3. I will upon all hazards well believe
  4. Thou art my friend, that know'st my tongue so well.
  5. Who art thou?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Who thou wilt: and if thou please,
  3. Thou mayst befriend me so much as to think
  4. I come one way of the Plantagenets.
  1. HUBERT
  2. Unkind remembrance! thou and eyeless night
  3. Have done me shame: brave soldier, pardon me,
  4. That any accent breaking from thy tongue
  5. Should 'scape the true acquaintance of mine ear.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Come, come; sans compliment, what news abroad?
  1. HUBERT
  2. Why, here walk I in the black brow of night,
  3. To find you out.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Brief, then; and what's the news?
  1. HUBERT
  2. O, my sweet sir, news fitting to the night,
  3. Black, fearful, comfortless and horrible.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Show me the very wound of this ill news:
  3. I am no woman, I'll not swoon at it.
  1. HUBERT
  2. The king, I fear, is poison'd by a monk:
  3. I left him almost speechless; and broke out
  4. To acquaint you with this evil, that you might
  5. The better arm you to the sudden time,
  6. Than if you had at leisure known of this.
  1. BASTARD
  2. How did he take it? who did taste to him?
  1. HUBERT
  2. A monk, I tell you; a resolved villain,
  3. Whose bowels suddenly burst out: the king
  4. Yet speaks and peradventure may recover.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Who didst thou leave to tend his majesty?
  1. HUBERT
  2. Why, know you not? the lords are all come back,
  3. And brought Prince Henry in their company;
  4. At whose request the king hath pardon'd them,
  5. And they are all about his majesty.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Withhold thine indignation, mighty heaven,
  3. And tempt us not to bear above our power!
  4. I'll tell tree, Hubert, half my power this night,
  5. Passing these flats, are taken by the tide;
  6. These Lincoln Washes have devoured them;
  7. Myself, well mounted, hardly have escaped.
  8. Away before: conduct me to the king;
  9. I doubt he will be dead or ere I come.
Exeunt

SCENE VII. The orchard in Swinstead Abbey.

Enter PRINCE HENRY, SALISBURY, and BIGOT
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. It is too late: the life of all his blood
  3. Is touch'd corruptibly, and his pure brain,
  4. Which some suppose the soul's frail dwelling-house,
  5. Doth by the idle comments that it makes
  6. Foretell the ending of mortality.
Enter PEMBROKE
  1. PEMBROKE
  2. His highness yet doth speak, and holds belief
  3. That, being brought into the open air,
  4. It would allay the burning quality
  5. Of that fell poison which assaileth him.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. Let him be brought into the orchard here.
  3. Doth he still rage?
Exit BIGOT
  1. PEMBROKE
  2. He is more patient
  3. Than when you left him; even now he sung.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. O vanity of sickness! fierce extremes
  3. In their continuance will not feel themselves.
  4. Death, having prey'd upon the outward parts,
  5. Leaves them invisible, and his siege is now
  6. Against the mind, the which he pricks and wounds
  7. With many legions of strange fantasies,
  8. Which, in their throng and press to that last hold,
  9. Confound themselves. 'Tis strange that death
  10. should sing.
  11. I am the cygnet to this pale faint swan,
  12. Who chants a doleful hymn to his own death,
  13. And from the organ-pipe of frailty sings
  14. His soul and body to their lasting rest.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. Be of good comfort, prince; for you are born
  3. To set a form upon that indigest
  4. Which he hath left so shapeless and so rude.
Enter Attendants, and BIGOT, carrying KING JOHN in a chair
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Ay, marry, now my soul hath elbow-room;
  3. It would not out at windows nor at doors.
  4. There is so hot a summer in my bosom,
  5. That all my bowels crumble up to dust:
  6. I am a scribbled form, drawn with a pen
  7. Upon a parchment, and against this fire
  8. Do I shrink up.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. How fares your majesty?
  1. KING JOHN
  2. Poison'd — ill fare — dead, forsook, cast off:
  3. And none of you will bid the winter come
  4. To thrust his icy fingers in my maw,
  5. Nor let my kingdom's rivers take their course
  6. Through my burn'd bosom, nor entreat the north
  7. To make his bleak winds kiss my parched lips
  8. And comfort me with cold. I do not ask you much,
  9. I beg cold comfort; and you are so strait
  10. And so ingrateful, you deny me that.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. O that there were some virtue in my tears,
  3. That might relieve you!
  1. KING JOHN
  2. The salt in them is hot.
  3. Within me is a hell; and there the poison
  4. Is as a fiend confined to tyrannize
  5. On unreprievable condemned blood.
Enter the BASTARD
  1. BASTARD
  2. O, I am scalded with my violent motion,
  3. And spleen of speed to see your majesty!
  1. KING JOHN
  2. O cousin, thou art come to set mine eye:
  3. The tackle of my heart is crack'd and burn'd,
  4. And all the shrouds wherewith my life should sail
  5. Are turned to one thread, one little hair:
  6. My heart hath one poor string to stay it by,
  7. Which holds but till thy news be uttered;
  8. And then all this thou seest is but a clod
  9. And module of confounded royalty.
  1. BASTARD
  2. The Dauphin is preparing hitherward,
  3. Where heaven He knows how we shall answer him;
  4. For in a night the best part of my power,
  5. As I upon advantage did remove,
  6. Were in the Washes all unwarily
  7. Devoured by the unexpected flood.
KING JOHN dies
  1. SALISBURY
  2. You breathe these dead news in as dead an ear.
  3. My liege! my lord! but now a king, now thus.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. Even so must I run on, and even so stop.
  3. What surety of the world, what hope, what stay,
  4. When this was now a king, and now is clay?
  1. BASTARD
  2. Art thou gone so? I do but stay behind
  3. To do the office for thee of revenge,
  4. And then my soul shall wait on thee to heaven,
  5. As it on earth hath been thy servant still.
  6. Now, now, you stars that move in your right spheres,
  7. Where be your powers? show now your mended faiths,
  8. And instantly return with me again,
  9. To push destruction and perpetual shame
  10. Out of the weak door of our fainting land.
  11. Straight let us seek, or straight we shall be sought;
  12. The Dauphin rages at our very heels.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. It seems you know not, then, so much as we:
  3. The Cardinal Pandulph is within at rest,
  4. Who half an hour since came from the Dauphin,
  5. And brings from him such offers of our peace
  6. As we with honour and respect may take,
  7. With purpose presently to leave this war.
  1. BASTARD
  2. He will the rather do it when he sees
  3. Ourselves well sinewed to our defence.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. Nay, it is in a manner done already;
  3. For many carriages he hath dispatch'd
  4. To the sea-side, and put his cause and quarrel
  5. To the disposing of the cardinal:
  6. With whom yourself, myself and other lords,
  7. If you think meet, this afternoon will post
  8. To consummate this business happily.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Let it be so: and you, my noble prince,
  3. With other princes that may best be spared,
  4. Shall wait upon your father's funeral.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. At Worcester must his body be interr'd;
  3. For so he will'd it.
  1. BASTARD
  2. Thither shall it then:
  3. And happily may your sweet self put on
  4. The lineal state and glory of the land!
  5. To whom with all submission, on my knee
  6. I do bequeath my faithful services
  7. And true subjection everlastingly.
  1. SALISBURY
  2. And the like tender of our love we make,
  3. To rest without a spot for evermore.
  1. PRINCE HENRY
  2. I have a kind soul that would give you thanks
  3. And knows not how to do it but with tears.
  1. BASTARD
  2. O, let us pay the time but needful woe,
  3. Since it hath been beforehand with our griefs.
  4. This England never did, nor never shall,
  5. Lie at the proud foot of a conqueror,
  6. But when it first did help to wound itself.
  7. Now these her princes are come home again,
  8. Come the three corners of the world in arms,
  9. And we shall shock them. Nought shall make us rue,
  10. If England to itself do rest but true.
Exeunt
