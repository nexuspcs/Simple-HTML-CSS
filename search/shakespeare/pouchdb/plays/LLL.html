SCENE Navarre.

ACT I

SCENE I. The king of Navarre's park.

Enter FERDINAND king of Navarre, BIRON, LONGAVILLE and DUMAIN
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Let fame, that all hunt after in their lives,
  3. Live register'd upon our brazen tombs
  4. And then grace us in the disgrace of death;
  5. When, spite of cormorant devouring Time,
  6. The endeavor of this present breath may buy
  7. That honour which shall bate his scythe's keen edge
  8. And make us heirs of all eternity.
  9. Therefore, brave conquerors — for so you are,
  10. That war against your own affections
  11. And the huge army of the world's desires —
  12. Our late edict shall strongly stand in force:
  13. Navarre shall be the wonder of the world;
  14. Our court shall be a little Academe,
  15. Still and contemplative in living art.
  16. You three, Biron, Dumain, and Longaville,
  17. Have sworn for three years' term to live with me
  18. My fellow-scholars, and to keep those statutes
  19. That are recorded in this schedule here:
  20. Your oaths are pass'd; and now subscribe your names,
  21. That his own hand may strike his honour down
  22. That violates the smallest branch herein:
  23. If you are arm'd to do as sworn to do,
  24. Subscribe to your deep oaths, and keep it too.
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. I am resolved; 'tis but a three years' fast:
  3. The mind shall banquet, though the body pine:
  4. Fat paunches have lean pates, and dainty bits
  5. Make rich the ribs, but bankrupt quite the wits.
  1. DUMAIN
  2. My loving lord, Dumain is mortified:
  3. The grosser manner of these world's delights
  4. He throws upon the gross world's baser slaves:
  5. To love, to wealth, to pomp, I pine and die;
  6. With all these living in philosophy.
  1. BIRON
  2. I can but say their protestation over;
  3. So much, dear liege, I have already sworn,
  4. That is, to live and study here three years.
  5. But there are other strict observances;
  6. As, not to see a woman in that term,
  7. Which I hope well is not enrolled there;
  8. And one day in a week to touch no food
  9. And but one meal on every day beside,
  10. The which I hope is not enrolled there;
  11. And then, to sleep but three hours in the night,
  12. And not be seen to wink of all the day —
  13. When I was wont to think no harm all night
  14. And make a dark night too of half the day —
  15. Which I hope well is not enrolled there:
  16. O, these are barren tasks, too hard to keep,
  17. Not to see ladies, study, fast, not sleep!
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Your oath is pass'd to pass away from these.
  1. BIRON
  2. Let me say no, my liege, an if you please:
  3. I only swore to study with your grace
  4. And stay here in your court for three years' space.
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. You swore to that, Biron, and to the rest.
  1. BIRON
  2. By yea and nay, sir, then I swore in jest.
  3. What is the end of study? let me know.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Why, that to know, which else we should not know.
  1. BIRON
  2. Things hid and barr'd, you mean, from common sense?
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Ay, that is study's godlike recompense.
  1. BIRON
  2. Come on, then; I will swear to study so,
  3. To know the thing I am forbid to know:
  4. As thus — to study where I well may dine,
  5. When I to feast expressly am forbid;
  6. Or study where to meet some mistress fine,
  7. When mistresses from common sense are hid;
  8. Or, having sworn too hard a keeping oath,
  9. Study to break it and not break my troth.
  10. If study's gain be thus and this be so,
  11. Study knows that which yet it doth not know:
  12. Swear me to this, and I will ne'er say no.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. These be the stops that hinder study quite
  3. And train our intellects to vain delight.
  1. BIRON
  2. Why, all delights are vain; but that most vain,
  3. Which with pain purchased doth inherit pain:
  4. As, painfully to pore upon a book
  5. To seek the light of truth; while truth the while
  6. Doth falsely blind the eyesight of his look:
  7. Light seeking light doth light of light beguile:
  8. So, ere you find where light in darkness lies,
  9. Your light grows dark by losing of your eyes.
  10. Study me how to please the eye indeed
  11. By fixing it upon a fairer eye,
  12. Who dazzling so, that eye shall be his heed
  13. And give him light that it was blinded by.
  14. Study is like the heaven's glorious sun
  15. That will not be deep-search'd with saucy looks:
  16. Small have continual plodders ever won
  17. Save base authority from others' books
  18. These earthly godfathers of heaven's lights
  19. That give a name to every fixed star
  20. Have no more profit of their shining nights
  21. Than those that walk and wot not what they are.
  22. Too much to know is to know nought but fame;
  23. And every godfather can give a name.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. How well he's read, to reason against reading!
  1. DUMAIN
  2. Proceeded well, to stop all good proceeding!
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. He weeds the corn and still lets grow the weeding.
  1. BIRON
  2. The spring is near when green geese are a-breeding.
  1. DUMAIN
  2. How follows that?
  1. BIRON
  2. Fit in his place and time.
  1. DUMAIN
  2. In reason nothing.
  1. BIRON
  2. Something then in rhyme.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Biron is like an envious sneaping frost,
  3. That bites the first-born infants of the spring.
  1. BIRON
  2. Well, say I am; why should proud summer boast
  3. Before the birds have any cause to sing?
  4. Why should I joy in any abortive birth?
  5. At Christmas I no more desire a rose
  6. Than wish a snow in May's new-fangled mirth;
  7. But like of each thing that in season grows.
  8. So you, to study now it is too late,
  9. Climb o'er the house to unlock the little gate.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Well, sit you out: go home, Biron: adieu.
  1. BIRON
  2. No, my good lord; I have sworn to stay with you:
  3. And though I have for barbarism spoke more
  4. Than for that angel knowledge you can say,
  5. Yet confident I'll keep what I have swore
  6. And bide the penance of each three years' day.
  7. Give me the paper; let me read the same;
  8. And to the strict'st decrees I'll write my name.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. How well this yielding rescues thee from shame!
  1. BIRON
  2. Reads 'Item, That no woman shall come within a
  3. mile of my court:' Hath this been proclaimed?
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. Four days ago.
  1. BIRON
  2. Let's see the penalty.
  3. Reads
  4. 'On pain of losing her tongue.' Who devised this penalty?
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. Marry, that did I.
  1. BIRON
  2. Sweet lord, and why?
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. To fright them hence with that dread penalty.
  1. BIRON
  2. A dangerous law against gentility!
  3. Reads
  4. 'Item, If any man be seen to talk with a woman
  5. within the term of three years, he shall endure such
  6. public shame as the rest of the court can possibly devise.'
  7. This article, my liege, yourself must break;
  8. For well you know here comes in embassy
  9. The French king's daughter with yourself to speak —
  10. A maid of grace and complete majesty —
  11. About surrender up of Aquitaine
  12. To her decrepit, sick and bedrid father:
  13. Therefore this article is made in vain,
  14. Or vainly comes the admired princess hither.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. What say you, lords? Why, this was quite forgot.
  1. BIRON
  2. So study evermore is overshot:
  3. While it doth study to have what it would
  4. It doth forget to do the thing it should,
  5. And when it hath the thing it hunteth most,
  6. 'Tis won as towns with fire, so won, so lost.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. We must of force dispense with this decree;
  3. She must lie here on mere necessity.
  1. BIRON
  2. Necessity will make us all forsworn
  3. Three thousand times within this three years' space;
  4. For every man with his affects is born,
  5. Not by might master'd but by special grace:
  6. If I break faith, this word shall speak for me;
  7. I am forsworn on 'mere necessity.'
  8. So to the laws at large I write my name:
  9. Subscribes
  10. And he that breaks them in the least degree
  11. Stands in attainder of eternal shame:
  12. Suggestions are to other as to me;
  13. But I believe, although I seem so loath,
  14. I am the last that will last keep his oath.
  15. But is there no quick recreation granted?
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Ay, that there is. Our court, you know, is haunted
  3. With a refined traveller of Spain;
  4. A man in all the world's new fashion planted,
  5. That hath a mint of phrases in his brain;
  6. One whom the music of his own vain tongue
  7. Doth ravish like enchanting harmony;
  8. A man of complements, whom right and wrong
  9. Have chose as umpire of their mutiny:
  10. This child of fancy, that Armado hight,
  11. For interim to our studies shall relate
  12. In high-born words the worth of many a knight
  13. From tawny Spain lost in the world's debate.
  14. How you delight, my lords, I know not, I;
  15. But, I protest, I love to hear him lie
  16. And I will use him for my minstrelsy.
  1. BIRON
  2. Armado is a most illustrious wight,
  3. A man of fire-new words, fashion's own knight.
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. Costard the swain and he shall be our sport;
  3. And so to study, three years is but short.
Enter DULL with a letter, and COSTARD
  1. DULL
  2. Which is the duke's own person?
  1. BIRON
  2. This, fellow: what wouldst?
  1. DULL
  2. I myself reprehend his own person, for I am his
  3. grace's tharborough: but I would see his own person
  4. in flesh and blood.
  1. BIRON
  2. This is he.
  1. DULL
  2. Signior Arme — Arme — commends you. There's villany
  3. abroad: this letter will tell you more.
  1. COSTARD
  2. Sir, the contempts thereof are as touching me.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. A letter from the magnificent Armado.
  1. BIRON
  2. How low soever the matter, I hope in God for high words.
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. A high hope for a low heaven: God grant us patience!
  1. BIRON
  2. To hear? or forbear laughing?
  1. LONGAVILLE
  2. To hear meekly, sir, and to laugh moderately; or to
  3. forbear both.
  1. BIRON
  2. Well, sir, be it as the style shall give us cause to
  3. climb in the merriness.
  1. COSTARD
  2. The matter is to me, sir, as concerning Jaquenetta.
  3. The manner of it is, I was taken with the manner.
  1. BIRON
  2. In what manner?
  1. COSTARD
  2. In manner and form following, sir; all those three:
  3. I was seen with her in the manor-house, sitting with
  4. her upon the form, and taken following her into the
  5. park; which, put together, is in manner and form
  6. following. Now, sir, for the manner — it is the
  7. manner of a man to speak to a woman: for the form —
  8. in some form.
  1. BIRON
  2. For the following, sir?
  1. COSTARD
  2. As it shall follow in my correction: and God defend
  3. the right!
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Will you hear this letter with attention?
  1. BIRON
  2. As we would hear an oracle.
  1. COSTARD
  2. Such is the simplicity of man to hearken after the flesh.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'Great deputy, the welkin's vicegerent and
  3. sole dominator of Navarre, my soul's earth's god,
  4. and body's fostering patron.'
  1. COSTARD
  2. Not a word of Costard yet.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'So it is,' —
  1. COSTARD
  2. It may be so: but if he say it is so, he is, in
  3. telling true, but so.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Peace!
  1. COSTARD
  2. Be to me and every man that dares not fight!
  1. FERDINAND
  2. No words!
  1. COSTARD
  2. Of other men's secrets, I beseech you.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'So it is, besieged with sable-coloured
  3. melancholy, I did commend the black-oppressing humour
  4. to the most wholesome physic of thy health-giving
  5. air; and, as I am a gentleman, betook myself to
  6. walk. The time when. About the sixth hour; when
  7. beasts most graze, birds best peck, and men sit down
  8. to that nourishment which is called supper: so much
  9. for the time when. Now for the ground which; which,
  10. I mean, I walked upon: it is y-cleped thy park. Then
  11. for the place where; where, I mean, I did encounter
  12. that obscene and preposterous event, that draweth
  13. from my snow-white pen the ebon-coloured ink, which
  14. here thou viewest, beholdest, surveyest, or seest;
  15. but to the place where; it standeth north-north-east
  16. and by east from the west corner of thy curious-
  17. knotted garden: there did I see that low-spirited
  18. swain, that base minnow of thy mirth,' —
  1. COSTARD
  2. Me?
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'that unlettered small-knowing soul,' —
  1. COSTARD
  2. Me?
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'that shallow vassal,' —
  1. COSTARD
  2. Still me?
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'which, as I remember, hight Costard,' —
  1. COSTARD
  2. O, me!
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'sorted and consorted, contrary to thy
  3. established proclaimed edict and continent canon,
  4. which with — O, with — but with this I passion to say
  5. wherewith —
  1. COSTARD
  2. With a wench.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'with a child of our grandmother Eve, a
  3. female; or, for thy more sweet understanding, a
  4. woman. Him I, as my ever-esteemed duty pricks me on,
  5. have sent to thee, to receive the meed of
  6. punishment, by thy sweet grace's officer, Anthony
  7. Dull; a man of good repute, carriage, bearing, and
  8. estimation.'
  1. DULL
  2. 'Me, an't shall please you; I am Anthony Dull.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Reads 'For Jaquenetta — so is the weaker vessel
  3. called which I apprehended with the aforesaid
  4. swain — I keep her as a vessel of the law's fury;
  5. and shall, at the least of thy sweet notice, bring
  6. her to trial. Thine, in all compliments of devoted
  7. and heart-burning heat of duty.
  8. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO.'
  1. BIRON
  2. This is not so well as I looked for, but the best
  3. that ever I heard.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Ay, the best for the worst. But, sirrah, what say
  3. you to this?
  1. COSTARD
  2. Sir, I confess the wench.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Did you hear the proclamation?
  1. COSTARD
  2. I do confess much of the hearing it but little of
  3. the marking of it.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. It was proclaimed a year's imprisonment, to be taken
  3. with a wench.
  1. COSTARD
  2. I was taken with none, sir: I was taken with a damsel.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Well, it was proclaimed 'damsel.'
  1. COSTARD
  2. This was no damsel, neither, sir; she was a virgin.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. It is so varied, too; for it was proclaimed 'virgin.'
  1. COSTARD
  2. If it were, I deny her virginity: I was taken with a maid.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. This maid will not serve your turn, sir.
  1. COSTARD
  2. This maid will serve my turn, sir.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. Sir, I will pronounce your sentence: you shall fast
  3. a week with bran and water.
  1. COSTARD
  2. I had rather pray a month with mutton and porridge.
  1. FERDINAND
  2. And Don Armado shall be your keeper.
  3. My Lord Biron, see him deliver'd o'er:
  4. And go we, lords, to put in practise that
  5. Which each to other hath so strongly sworn.
Exeunt FERDINAND, LONGAVILLE, and DUMAIN
  1. BIRON
  2. I'll lay my head to any good man's hat,
  3. These oaths and laws will prove an idle scorn.
  4. Sirrah, come on.
  1. COSTARD
  2. I suffer for the truth, sir; for true it is, I was
  3. taken with Jaquenetta, and Jaquenetta is a true
  4. girl; and therefore welcome the sour cup of
  5. prosperity! Affliction may one day smile again; and
  6. till then, sit thee down, sorrow!
Exeunt

SCENE II. The same.

Enter DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO and MOTH
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Boy, what sign is it when a man of great spirit
  3. grows melancholy?
  1. MOTH
  2. A great sign, sir, that he will look sad.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Why, sadness is one and the self-same thing, dear imp.
  1. MOTH
  2. No, no; O Lord, sir, no.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. How canst thou part sadness and melancholy, my
  3. tender juvenal?
  1. MOTH
  2. By a familiar demonstration of the working, my tough senior.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Why tough senior? why tough senior?
  1. MOTH
  2. Why tender juvenal? why tender juvenal?
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I spoke it, tender juvenal, as a congruent epitheton
  3. appertaining to thy young days, which we may
  4. nominate tender.
  1. MOTH
  2. And I, tough senior, as an appertinent title to your
  3. old time, which we may name tough.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Pretty and apt.
  1. MOTH
  2. How mean you, sir? I pretty, and my saying apt? or
  3. I apt, and my saying pretty?
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Thou pretty, because little.
  1. MOTH
  2. Little pretty, because little. Wherefore apt?
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. And therefore apt, because quick.
  1. MOTH
  2. Speak you this in my praise, master?
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. In thy condign praise.
  1. MOTH
  2. I will praise an eel with the same praise.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. What, that an eel is ingenious?
  1. MOTH
  2. That an eel is quick.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I do say thou art quick in answers: thou heatest my blood.
  1. MOTH
  2. I am answered, sir.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I love not to be crossed.
  1. MOTH
  2. Aside He speaks the mere contrary; crosses love not him.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I have promised to study three years with the duke.
  1. MOTH
  2. You may do it in an hour, sir.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Impossible.
  1. MOTH
  2. How many is one thrice told?
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I am ill at reckoning; it fitteth the spirit of a tapster.
  1. MOTH
  2. You are a gentleman and a gamester, sir.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I confess both: they are both the varnish of a
  3. complete man.
  1. MOTH
  2. Then, I am sure, you know how much the gross sum of
  3. deuce-ace amounts to.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. It doth amount to one more than two.
  1. MOTH
  2. Which the base vulgar do call three.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. True.
  1. MOTH
  2. Why, sir, is this such a piece of study? Now here
  3. is three studied, ere ye'll thrice wink: and how
  4. easy it is to put 'years' to the word 'three,' and
  5. study three years in two words, the dancing horse
  6. will tell you.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. A most fine figure!
  1. MOTH
  2. To prove you a cipher.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I will hereupon confess I am in love: and as it is
  3. base for a soldier to love, so am I in love with a
  4. base wench. If drawing my sword against the humour
  5. of affection would deliver me from the reprobate
  6. thought of it, I would take Desire prisoner, and
  7. ransom him to any French courtier for a new-devised
  8. courtesy. I think scorn to sigh: methinks I should
  9. outswear Cupid. Comfort, me, boy: what great men
  10. have been in love?
  1. MOTH
  2. Hercules, master.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Most sweet Hercules! More authority, dear boy, name
  3. more; and, sweet my child, let them be men of good
  4. repute and carriage.
  1. MOTH
  2. Samson, master: he was a man of good carriage, great
  3. carriage, for he carried the town-gates on his back
  4. like a porter: and he was in love.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. O well-knit Samson! strong-jointed Samson! I do
  3. excel thee in my rapier as much as thou didst me in
  4. carrying gates. I am in love too. Who was Samson's
  5. love, my dear Moth?
  1. MOTH
  2. A woman, master.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Of what complexion?
  1. MOTH
  2. Of all the four, or the three, or the two, or one of the four.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Tell me precisely of what complexion.
  1. MOTH
  2. Of the sea-water green, sir.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Is that one of the four complexions?
  1. MOTH
  2. As I have read, sir; and the best of them too.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Green indeed is the colour of lovers; but to have a
  3. love of that colour, methinks Samson had small reason
  4. for it. He surely affected her for her wit.
  1. MOTH
  2. It was so, sir; for she had a green wit.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. My love is most immaculate white and red.
  1. MOTH
  2. Most maculate thoughts, master, are masked under
  3. such colours.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Define, define, well-educated infant.
  1. MOTH
  2. My father's wit and my mother's tongue, assist me!
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Sweet invocation of a child; most pretty and
  3. pathetical!
  1. MOTH
  2. If she be made of white and red,
  3. Her faults will ne'er be known,
  4. For blushing cheeks by faults are bred
  5. And fears by pale white shown:
  6. Then if she fear, or be to blame,
  7. By this you shall not know,
  8. For still her cheeks possess the same
  9. Which native she doth owe.
  10. A dangerous rhyme, master, against the reason of
  11. white and red.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. Is there not a ballad, boy, of the King and the Beggar?
  1. MOTH
  2. The world was very guilty of such a ballad some
  3. three ages since: but I think now 'tis not to be
  4. found; or, if it were, it would neither serve for
  5. the writing nor the tune.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
  2. I will have that subject newly writ o'er, that I may
  3. example my digression by some mighty precedent.
  4. Boy, I do love that country girl that I took in the
  5. park with the rational hind Costard: she deserves well.
  1. MOTH
  2. Aside To be whipped; and yet a better love than
  3. my master.
  1. DON ADRIANO DE ARMADO
