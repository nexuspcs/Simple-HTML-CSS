ACT I

SCENE I. The same.

Enter LORD BARDOLPH
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Who keeps the gate here, ho?
  3. The Porter opens the gate
  4. Where is the earl?
  1. Porter
  2. What shall I say you are?
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Tell thou the earl
  3. That the Lord Bardolph doth attend him here.
  1. Porter
  2. His lordship is walk'd forth into the orchard;
  3. Please it your honour, knock but at the gate,
  4. And he himself wilt answer.
Enter NORTHUMBERLAND
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Here comes the earl.
Exit Porter
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. What news, Lord Bardolph? every minute now
  3. Should be the father of some stratagem:
  4. The times are wild: contention, like a horse
  5. Full of high feeding, madly hath broke loose
  6. And bears down all before him.
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Noble earl,
  3. I bring you certain news from Shrewsbury.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Good, an God will!
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. As good as heart can wish:
  3. The king is almost wounded to the death;
  4. And, in the fortune of my lord your son,
  5. Prince Harry slain outright; and both the Blunts
  6. Kill'd by the hand of Douglas; young Prince John
  7. And Westmoreland and Stafford fled the field;
  8. And Harry Monmouth's brawn, the hulk Sir John,
  9. Is prisoner to your son: O, such a day,
  10. So fought, so follow'd and so fairly won,
  11. Came not till now to dignify the times,
  12. Since Caesar's fortunes!
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. How is this derived?
  3. Saw you the field? came you from Shrewsbury?
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. I spake with one, my lord, that came from thence,
  3. A gentleman well bred and of good name,
  4. That freely render'd me these news for true.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Here comes my servant Travers, whom I sent
  3. On Tuesday last to listen after news.
Enter TRAVERS
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. My lord, I over-rode him on the way;
  3. And he is furnish'd with no certainties
  4. More than he haply may retail from me.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Now, Travers, what good tidings comes with you?
  1. TRAVERS
  2. My lord, Sir John Umfrevile turn'd me back
  3. With joyful tidings; and, being better horsed,
  4. Out-rode me. After him came spurring hard
  5. A gentleman, almost forspent with speed,
  6. That stopp'd by me to breathe his bloodied horse.
  7. He ask'd the way to Chester; and of him
  8. I did demand what news from Shrewsbury:
  9. He told me that rebellion had bad luck
  10. And that young Harry Percy's spur was cold.
  11. With that, he gave his able horse the head,
  12. And bending forward struck his armed heels
  13. Against the panting sides of his poor jade
  14. Up to the rowel-head, and starting so
  15. He seem'd in running to devour the way,
  16. Staying no longer question.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Ha! Again:
  3. Said he young Harry Percy's spur was cold?
  4. Of Hotspur Coldspur? that rebellion
  5. Had met ill luck?
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. My lord, I'll tell you what;
  3. If my young lord your son have not the day,
  4. Upon mine honour, for a silken point
  5. I'll give my barony: never talk of it.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Why should that gentleman that rode by Travers
  3. Give then such instances of loss?
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Who, he?
  3. He was some hilding fellow that had stolen
  4. The horse he rode on, and, upon my life,
  5. Spoke at a venture. Look, here comes more news.
Enter MORTON
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Yea, this man's brow, like to a title-leaf,
  3. Foretells the nature of a tragic volume:
  4. So looks the strand whereon the imperious flood
  5. Hath left a witness'd usurpation.
  6. Say, Morton, didst thou come from Shrewsbury?
  1. MORTON
  2. I ran from Shrewsbury, my noble lord;
  3. Where hateful death put on his ugliest mask
  4. To fright our party.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. How doth my son and brother?
  3. Thou tremblest; and the whiteness in thy cheek
  4. Is apter than thy tongue to tell thy errand.
  5. Even such a man, so faint, so spiritless,
  6. So dull, so dead in look, so woe-begone,
  7. Drew Priam's curtain in the dead of night,
  8. And would have told him half his Troy was burnt;
  9. But Priam found the fire ere he his tongue,
  10. And I my Percy's death ere thou report'st it.
  11. This thou wouldst say, 'Your son did thus and thus;
  12. Your brother thus: so fought the noble Douglas:'
  13. Stopping my greedy ear with their bold deeds:
  14. But in the end, to stop my ear indeed,
  15. Thou hast a sigh to blow away this praise,
  16. Ending with 'Brother, son, and all are dead.'
  1. MORTON
  2. Douglas is living, and your brother, yet;
  3. But, for my lord your son —
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Why, he is dead.
  3. See what a ready tongue suspicion hath!
  4. He that but fears the thing he would not know
  5. Hath by instinct knowledge from others' eyes
  6. That what he fear'd is chanced. Yet speak, Morton;
  7. Tell thou an earl his divination lies,
  8. And I will take it as a sweet disgrace
  9. And make thee rich for doing me such wrong.
  1. MORTON
  2. You are too great to be by me gainsaid:
  3. Your spirit is too true, your fears too certain.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. Yet, for all this, say not that Percy's dead.
  3. I see a strange confession in thine eye:
  4. Thou shakest thy head and hold'st it fear or sin
  5. To speak a truth. If he be slain, say so;
  6. The tongue offends not that reports his death:
  7. And he doth sin that doth belie the dead,
  8. Not he which says the dead is not alive.
  9. Yet the first bringer of unwelcome news
  10. Hath but a losing office, and his tongue
  11. Sounds ever after as a sullen bell,
  12. Remember'd tolling a departing friend.
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. I cannot think, my lord, your son is dead.
  1. MORTON
  2. I am sorry I should force you to believe
  3. That which I would to God I had not seen;
  4. But these mine eyes saw him in bloody state,
  5. Rendering faint quittance, wearied and out-breathed,
  6. To Harry Monmouth; whose swift wrath beat down
  7. The never-daunted Percy to the earth,
  8. From whence with life he never more sprung up.
  9. In few, his death, whose spirit lent a fire
  10. Even to the dullest peasant in his camp,
  11. Being bruited once, took fire and heat away
  12. From the best temper'd courage in his troops;
  13. For from his metal was his party steel'd;
  14. Which once in him abated, all the rest
  15. Turn'd on themselves, like dull and heavy lead:
  16. And as the thing that's heavy in itself,
  17. Upon enforcement flies with greatest speed,
  18. So did our men, heavy in Hotspur's loss,
  19. Lend to this weight such lightness with their fear
  20. That arrows fled not swifter toward their aim
  21. Than did our soldiers, aiming at their safety,
  22. Fly from the field. Then was the noble Worcester
  23. Too soon ta'en prisoner; and that furious Scot,
  24. The bloody Douglas, whose well-labouring sword
  25. Had three times slain the appearance of the king,
  26. 'Gan vail his stomach and did grace the shame
  27. Of those that turn'd their backs, and in his flight,
  28. Stumbling in fear, was took. The sum of all
  29. Is that the king hath won, and hath sent out
  30. A speedy power to encounter you, my lord,
  31. Under the conduct of young Lancaster
  32. And Westmoreland. This is the news at full.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. For this I shall have time enough to mourn.
  3. In poison there is physic; and these news,
  4. Having been well, that would have made me sick,
  5. Being sick, have in some measure made me well:
  6. And as the wretch, whose fever-weaken'd joints,
  7. Like strengthless hinges, buckle under life,
  8. Impatient of his fit, breaks like a fire
  9. Out of his keeper's arms, even so my limbs,
  10. Weaken'd with grief, being now enraged with grief,
  11. Are thrice themselves. Hence, therefore, thou nice crutch!
  12. A scaly gauntlet now with joints of steel
  13. Must glove this hand: and hence, thou sickly quoif!
  14. Thou art a guard too wanton for the head
  15. Which princes, flesh'd with conquest, aim to hit.
  16. Now bind my brows with iron; and approach
  17. The ragged'st hour that time and spite dare bring
  18. To frown upon the enraged Northumberland!
  19. Let heaven kiss earth! now let not Nature's hand
  20. Keep the wild flood confined! let order die!
  21. And let this world no longer be a stage
  22. To feed contention in a lingering act;
  23. But let one spirit of the first-born Cain
  24. Reign in all bosoms, that, each heart being set
  25. On bloody courses, the rude scene may end,
  26. And darkness be the burier of the dead!
  1. TRAVERS
  2. This strained passion doth you wrong, my lord.
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. Sweet earl, divorce not wisdom from your honour.
  1. MORTON
  2. The lives of all your loving complices
  3. Lean on your health; the which, if you give o'er
  4. To stormy passion, must perforce decay.
  5. You cast the event of war, my noble lord,
  6. And summ'd the account of chance, before you said
  7. 'Let us make head.' It was your presurmise,
  8. That, in the dole of blows, your son might drop:
  9. You knew he walk'd o'er perils, on an edge,
  10. More likely to fall in than to get o'er;
  11. You were advised his flesh was capable
  12. Of wounds and scars and that his forward spirit
  13. Would lift him where most trade of danger ranged:
  14. Yet did you say 'Go forth;' and none of this,
  15. Though strongly apprehended, could restrain
  16. The stiff-borne action: what hath then befallen,
  17. Or what hath this bold enterprise brought forth,
  18. More than that being which was like to be?
  1. LORD BARDOLPH
  2. We all that are engaged to this loss
  3. Knew that we ventured on such dangerous seas
  4. That if we wrought our life 'twas ten to one;
  5. And yet we ventured, for the gain proposed
  6. Choked the respect of likely peril fear'd;
  7. And since we are o'erset, venture again.
  8. Come, we will all put forth, body and goods.
  1. MORTON
  2. 'Tis more than time: and, my most noble lord,
  3. I hear for certain, and do speak the truth,
  4. The gentle Archbishop of York is up
  5. With well-appointed powers: he is a man
  6. Who with a double surety binds his followers.
  7. My lord your son had only but the corpse,
  8. But shadows and the shows of men, to fight;
  9. For that same word, rebellion, did divide
  10. The action of their bodies from their souls;
  11. And they did fight with queasiness, constrain'd,
  12. As men drink potions, that their weapons only
  13. Seem'd on our side; but, for their spirits and souls,
  14. This word, rebellion, it had froze them up,
  15. As fish are in a pond. But now the bishop
  16. Turns insurrection to religion:
  17. Supposed sincere and holy in his thoughts,
  18. He's followed both with body and with mind;
  19. And doth enlarge his rising with the blood
  20. Of fair King Richard, scraped from Pomfret stones;
  21. Derives from heaven his quarrel and his cause;
  22. Tells them he doth bestride a bleeding land,
  23. Gasping for life under great Bolingbroke;
  24. And more and less do flock to follow him.
  1. NORTHUMBERLAND
  2. I knew of this before; but, to speak truth,
  3. This present grief had wiped it from my mind.
  4. Go in with me; and counsel every man
  5. The aptest way for safety and revenge:
  6. Get posts and letters, and make friends with speed:
  7. Never so few, and never yet more need.
Exeunt

SCENE II. London. A street.

Enter FALSTAFF, with his Page bearing his sword and buckler
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Sirrah, you giant, what says the doctor to my water?
  1. Page
  2. He said, sir, the water itself was a good healthy
  3. water; but, for the party that owed it, he might
  4. have more diseases than he knew for.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Men of all sorts take a pride to gird at me: the
  3. brain of this foolish-compounded clay, man, is not
  4. able to invent anything that tends to laughter, more
  5. than I invent or is invented on me: I am not only
  6. witty in myself, but the cause that wit is in other
  7. men. I do here walk before thee like a sow that
  8. hath overwhelmed all her litter but one. If the
  9. prince put thee into my service for any other reason
  10. than to set me off, why then I have no judgment.
  11. Thou whoreson mandrake, thou art fitter to be worn
  12. in my cap than to wait at my heels. I was never
  13. manned with an agate till now: but I will inset you
  14. neither in gold nor silver, but in vile apparel, and
  15. send you back again to your master, for a jewel —
  16. the juvenal, the prince your master, whose chin is
  17. not yet fledged. I will sooner have a beard grow in
  18. the palm of my hand than he shall get one on his
  19. cheek; and yet he will not stick to say his face is
  20. a face-royal: God may finish it when he will, 'tis
  21. not a hair amiss yet: he may keep it still at a
  22. face-royal, for a barber shall never earn sixpence
  23. out of it; and yet he'll be crowing as if he had
  24. writ man ever since his father was a bachelor. He
  25. may keep his own grace, but he's almost out of mine,
  26. I can assure him. What said Master Dombledon about
  27. the satin for my short cloak and my slops?
  1. Page
  2. He said, sir, you should procure him better
  3. assurance than Bardolph: he would not take his
  4. band and yours; he liked not the security.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Let him be damned, like the glutton! pray God his
  3. tongue be hotter! A whoreson Achitophel! a rascally
  4. yea-forsooth knave! to bear a gentleman in hand,
  5. and then stand upon security! The whoreson
  6. smooth-pates do now wear nothing but high shoes, and
  7. bunches of keys at their girdles; and if a man is
  8. through with them in honest taking up, then they
  9. must stand upon security. I had as lief they would
  10. put ratsbane in my mouth as offer to stop it with
  11. security. I looked a' should have sent me two and
  12. twenty yards of satin, as I am a true knight, and he
  13. sends me security. Well, he may sleep in security;
  14. for he hath the horn of abundance, and the lightness
  15. of his wife shines through it: and yet cannot he
  16. see, though he have his own lanthorn to light him.
  17. Where's Bardolph?
  1. Page
  2. He's gone into Smithfield to buy your worship a horse.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. I bought him in Paul's, and he'll buy me a horse in
  3. Smithfield: an I could get me but a wife in the
  4. stews, I were manned, horsed, and wived.
Enter the Lord Chief-Justice and Servant
  1. Page
  2. Sir, here comes the nobleman that committed the
  3. Prince for striking him about Bardolph.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Wait, close; I will not see him.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. What's he that goes there?
  1. Servant
  2. Falstaff, an't please your lordship.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. He that was in question for the robbery?
  1. Servant
  2. He, my lord: but he hath since done good service at
  3. Shrewsbury; and, as I hear, is now going with some
  4. charge to the Lord John of Lancaster.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. What, to York? Call him back again.
  1. Servant
  2. Sir John Falstaff!
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Boy, tell him I am deaf.
  1. Page
  2. You must speak louder; my master is deaf.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. I am sure he is, to the hearing of any thing good.
  3. Go, pluck him by the elbow; I must speak with him.
  1. Servant
  2. Sir John!
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. What! a young knave, and begging! Is there not
  3. wars? is there not employment? doth not the king
  4. lack subjects? do not the rebels need soldiers?
  5. Though it be a shame to be on any side but one, it
  6. is worse shame to beg than to be on the worst side,
  7. were it worse than the name of rebellion can tell
  8. how to make it.
  1. Servant
  2. You mistake me, sir.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Why, sir, did I say you were an honest man? setting
  3. my knighthood and my soldiership aside, I had lied
  4. in my throat, if I had said so.
  1. Servant
  2. I pray you, sir, then set your knighthood and our
  3. soldiership aside; and give me leave to tell you,
  4. you lie in your throat, if you say I am any other
  5. than an honest man.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. I give thee leave to tell me so! I lay aside that
  3. which grows to me! if thou gettest any leave of me,
  4. hang me; if thou takest leave, thou wert better be
  5. hanged. You hunt counter: hence! avaunt!
  1. Servant
  2. Sir, my lord would speak with you.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Sir John Falstaff, a word with you.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. My good lord! God give your lordship good time of
  3. day. I am glad to see your lordship abroad: I heard
  4. say your lordship was sick: I hope your lordship
  5. goes abroad by advice. Your lordship, though not
  6. clean past your youth, hath yet some smack of age in
  7. you, some relish of the saltness of time; and I must
  8. humbly beseech your lordship to have a reverent care
  9. of your health.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Sir John, I sent for you before your expedition to
  3. Shrewsbury.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. An't please your lordship, I hear his majesty is
  3. returned with some discomfort from Wales.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. I talk not of his majesty: you would not come when
  3. I sent for you.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. And I hear, moreover, his highness is fallen into
  3. this same whoreson apoplexy.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Well, God mend him! I pray you, let me speak with
  3. you.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. This apoplexy is, as I take it, a kind of lethargy,
  3. an't please your lordship; a kind of sleeping in the
  4. blood, a whoreson tingling.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. What tell you me of it? be it as it is.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. It hath its original from much grief, from study and
  3. perturbation of the brain: I have read the cause of
  4. his effects in Galen: it is a kind of deafness.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. I think you are fallen into the disease; for you
  3. hear not what I say to you.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Very well, my lord, very well: rather, an't please
  3. you, it is the disease of not listening, the malady
  4. of not marking, that I am troubled withal.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. To punish you by the heels would amend the
  3. attention of your ears; and I care not if I do
  4. become your physician.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. I am as poor as Job, my lord, but not so patient:
  3. your lordship may minister the potion of
  4. imprisonment to me in respect of poverty; but how
  5. should I be your patient to follow your
  6. prescriptions, the wise may make some dram of a
  7. scruple, or indeed a scruple itself.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. I sent for you, when there were matters against you
  3. for your life, to come speak with me.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. As I was then advised by my learned counsel in the
  3. laws of this land-service, I did not come.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Well, the truth is, Sir John, you live in great infamy.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. He that buckles him in my belt cannot live in less.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Your means are very slender, and your waste is great.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. I would it were otherwise; I would my means were
  3. greater, and my waist slenderer.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. You have misled the youthful prince.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. The young prince hath misled me: I am the fellow
  3. with the great belly, and he my dog.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Well, I am loath to gall a new-healed wound: your
  3. day's service at Shrewsbury hath a little gilded
  4. over your night's exploit on Gad's-hill: you may
  5. thank the unquiet time for your quiet o'er-posting
  6. that action.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. My lord?
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. But since all is well, keep it so: wake not a
  3. sleeping wolf.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. To wake a wolf is as bad as to smell a fox.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. What! you are as a candle, the better part burnt
  3. out.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. A wassail candle, my lord, all tallow: if I did say
  3. of wax, my growth would approve the truth.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. There is not a white hair on your face but should
  3. have his effect of gravity.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. His effect of gravy, gravy, gravy.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. You follow the young prince up and down, like his
  3. ill angel.
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. Not so, my lord; your ill angel is light; but I hope
  3. he that looks upon me will take me without weighing:
  4. and yet, in some respects, I grant, I cannot go: I
  5. cannot tell. Virtue is of so little regard in these
  6. costermonger times that true valour is turned
  7. bear-herd: pregnancy is made a tapster, and hath
  8. his quick wit wasted in giving reckonings: all the
  9. other gifts appertinent to man, as the malice of
  10. this age shapes them, are not worth a gooseberry.
  11. You that are old consider not the capacities of us
  12. that are young; you do measure the heat of our
  13. livers with the bitterness of your galls: and we
  14. that are in the vaward of our youth, I must confess,
  15. are wags too.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Do you set down your name in the scroll of youth,
  3. that are written down old with all the characters of
  4. age? Have you not a moist eye? a dry hand? a
  5. yellow cheek? a white beard? a decreasing leg? an
  6. increasing belly? is not your voice broken? your
  7. wind short? your chin double? your wit single? and
  8. every part about you blasted with antiquity? and
  9. will you yet call yourself young? Fie, fie, fie, Sir John!
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. My lord, I was born about three of the clock in the
  3. afternoon, with a white head and something a round
  4. belly. For my voice, I have lost it with halloing
  5. and singing of anthems. To approve my youth
  6. further, I will not: the truth is, I am only old in
  7. judgment and understanding; and he that will caper
  8. with me for a thousand marks, let him lend me the
  9. money, and have at him! For the box of the ear that
  10. the prince gave you, he gave it like a rude prince,
  11. and you took it like a sensible lord. I have
  12. chequed him for it, and the young lion repents;
  13. marry, not in ashes and sackcloth, but in new silk
  14. and old sack.
  1. Lord Chief-Justice
  2. Well, God send the prince a better companion!
  1. FALSTAFF
  2. God send the companion a better prince! I cannot
  3. rid my hands of him.

SCENE I. Yorkshire. Gaultree Forest.

  2. 'Tis Gaultree Forest, an't shall please your grace.
  1. ARCHBISHOP OF YORK

  1. HASTINGS
  2. We have sent forth already.
  1. ARCHBISHOP OF YORK
  2. 'Tis well done.

  8. As might hold sortance with his quality,
  9. The which he could not levy; whereupon

  2. Thus do the hopes we have in him touch ground

  13. To diet rank minds sick of happiness

  16. I have in equal balance justly weigh'd
  17. What wrongs our arms may do, what wrongs we suffer,
  18. And find our griefs heavier than our offences.

  20. And are enforced from our most quiet there
  21. By the rough torrent of occasion;

  2. This will I show the general. Please you, lords,

  2. Yea, but our valuation shall be such

  7. And keep no tell-tale to his memory

  2. I know not how they sold themselves: but thou, like

  2. Fare you well, Falstaff: I, in my condition,

  1. FALSTAFF
  2. I would you had but the wit: 'twere better than
  3. your dukedom. Good faith, this same young sober-
  4. blooded boy doth not love me; nor a man cannot make
  5. him laugh; but that's no marvel, he drinks no wine.
  6. There's never none of these demure boys come to any
  7. proof; for thin drink doth so over-cool their blood,
  8. and making many fish-meals, that they fall into a
  9. kind of male green-sickness; and then when they
  10. marry, they get wenches: they are generally fools
  11. and cowards; which some of us should be too, but for
  12. inflammation. A good sherris sack hath a two-fold
  13. operation in it. It ascends me into the brain;
  14. dries me there all the foolish and dull and curdy
  15. vapours which environ it; makes it apprehensive,
  16. quick, forgetive, full of nimble fiery and
  17. delectable shapes, which, delivered o'er to the
  18. voice, the tongue, which is the birth, becomes
  19. excellent wit. The second property of your
  20. excellent sherris is, the warming of the blood;
  21. which, before cold and settled, left the liver
  22. white and pale, which is the badge of pusillanimity
  23. and cowardice; but the sherris warms it and makes
  24. it course from the inwards to the parts extreme:
  25. it illumineth the face, which as a beacon gives
  26. warning to all the rest of this little kingdom,
  27. man, to arm; and then the vital commoners and
  28. inland petty spirits muster me all to their captain,
  29. the heart, who, great and puffed up with this
  30. retinue, doth any deed of courage; and this valour
  31. comes of sherris. So that skill in the weapon is
  32. nothing without sack, for that sets it a-work; and
  33. learning a mere hoard of gold kept by a devil, till
  34. sack commences it and sets it in act and use.
  35. Hereof comes it that Prince Harry is valiant; for
  36. the cold blood he did naturally inherit of his
  37. father, he hath, like lean, sterile and bare land,
  38. manured, husbanded and tilled with excellent
  39. endeavour of drinking good and good store of fertile
  40. sherris, that he is become very hot and valiant. If
  41. I had a thousand sons, the first humane principle I
  42. would teach them should be, to forswear thin
  43. potations and to addict themselves to sack.

  5. my thumb, and shortly will I seal with him. Come away.

  5. And draw no swords but what are sanctified.

  11. Come underneath the yoke of government.

  2. Both which we doubt not but your majesty
  3. Shall soon enjoy.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. Humphrey, my son of Gloucester,
  3. Where is the prince your brother?

  2. I think he's gone to hunt, my lord, at Windsor.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. And how accompanied?
  1. GLOUCESTER
  2. I do not know, my lord.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. Is not his brother, Thomas of Clarence, with him?
  1. GLOUCESTER
  2. No, my good lord; he is in presence here.
  1. CLARENCE
  2. What would my lord and father?
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. Nothing but well to thee, Thomas of Clarence.
  3. How chance thou art not with the prince thy brother?
  4. He loves thee, and thou dost neglect him, Thomas;
  5. Thou hast a better place in his affection
  6. Than all thy brothers: cherish it, my boy,
  7. And noble offices thou mayst effect
  8. Of mediation, after I am dead,
  9. Between his greatness and thy other brethren:
  10. Therefore omit him not; blunt not his love,
  11. Nor lose the good advantage of his grace
  12. By seeming cold or careless of his will;
  13. For he is gracious, if he be observed:
  14. He hath a tear for pity and a hand
  15. Open as day for melting charity:
  16. Yet notwithstanding, being incensed, he's flint,
  17. As humorous as winter and as sudden
  18. As flaws congealed in the spring of day.
  19. His temper, therefore, must be well observed:
  20. Chide him for faults, and do it reverently,
  21. When thou perceive his blood inclined to mirth;
  22. But, being moody, give him line and scope,
  23. Till that his passions, like a whale on ground,
  24. Confound themselves with working. Learn this, Thomas,
  25. And thou shalt prove a shelter to thy friends,
  26. A hoop of gold to bind thy brothers in,
  27. That the united vessel of their blood,
  28. Mingled with venom of suggestion —
  29. As, force perforce, the age will pour it in —
  30. Shall never leak, though it do work as strong
  31. As aconitum or rash gunpowder.

  2. I shall observe him with all care and love.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. Why art thou not at Windsor with him, Thomas?

  2. He is not there to-day; he dines in London.

  2. And how accompanied? canst thou tell that?
  1. CLARENCE
  2. With Poins, and other his continual followers.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. Most subject is the fattest soil to weeds;
  3. And he, the noble image of my youth,
  4. Is overspread with them: therefore my grief
  5. Stretches itself beyond the hour of death:
  6. The blood weeps from my heart when I do shape
  7. In forms imaginary the unguided days
  8. And rotten times that you shall look upon
  9. When I am sleeping with my ancestors.
  10. For when his headstrong riot hath no curb,
  11. When rage and hot blood are his counsellors,
  12. When means and lavish manners meet together,
  13. O, with what wings shall his affections fly
  14. Towards fronting peril and opposed decay!

  2. My gracious lord, you look beyond him quite:
  3. The prince but studies his companions

  7. Your highness knows, comes to no further use
  8. But to be known and hated. So, like gross terms,
  9. The prince will in the perfectness of time
  10. Cast off his followers; and their memory

  13. Turning past evils to advantages.

  2. O Westmoreland, thou art a summer bird,

  9. This packet, please it you, contains at large.

  2. And wherefore should these good news make me sick?

  4. But write her fair words still in foulest letters?

  2. Comfort, your majesty!
  1. CLARENCE
  2. O my royal father!

  2. My sovereign lord, cheer up yourself, look up.

  2. The people fear me; for they do observe

  2. Speak lower, princes, for the king recovers.
  1. GLOUCESTER
  2. This apoplexy will certain be his end.
  1. KING HENRY IV
  2. I pray you, take me up, and bear me hence
